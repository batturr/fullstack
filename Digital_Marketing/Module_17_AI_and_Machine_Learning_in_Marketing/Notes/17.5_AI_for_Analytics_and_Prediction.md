## 17.5.1 Predictive Analytics

### What Is Predictive Analytics in Marketing

**Definition:** Predictive analytics uses historical data, statistical algorithms, and machine learning to forecast future outcomes — answering "What is LIKELY to happen?" rather than "What happened?"

**Analytics Maturity Spectrum:**

```
Level 1: DESCRIPTIVE          Level 2: DIAGNOSTIC         Level 3: PREDICTIVE           Level 4: PRESCRIPTIVE
"What happened?"              "Why did it happen?"        "What will happen?"           "What should we do?"
─────────────────── ──▶ ──────────────────── ──▶ ──────────────────── ──▶ ────────────────────
Dashboards, reports           Root cause analysis         Forecasting, scoring          Optimization, automation
GA4 traffic reports           Funnel analysis             Churn prediction              Next-best-action
Monthly revenue reports       Attribution modeling        CLV forecasting               Automated budget allocation

Most companies are HERE      Some companies are HERE     Few companies are HERE        Rare — competitive advantage
(85%)                        (60%)                       (25%)                         (10%)
```

**Predictive Analytics Process:**

```
Step 1: DEFINE THE BUSINESS QUESTION
├── "Which leads are most likely to convert?"
├── "Which customers will churn in the next 30 days?"
├── "What will demand look like next quarter?"
└── "What's the lifetime value of this customer?"

Step 2: COLLECT AND PREPARE DATA
├── Historical outcome data (who converted, who churned, what sold)
├── Feature data (demographics, behavior, engagement, transactions)
├── Clean data: handle missing values, outliers, duplicates
└── Minimum: 1,000+ examples; ideal: 10,000+

Step 3: BUILD THE MODEL
├── Select algorithm (logistic regression, random forest, XGBoost, neural net)
├── Split data: 80% training, 20% testing
├── Train model on historical data
└── Evaluate on test data (accuracy, precision, recall, AUC)

Step 4: VALIDATE AND DEPLOY
├── Test with holdout group in production
├── Compare AI predictions vs. actual outcomes
├── Deploy predictions into marketing workflows
└── Monitor performance; retrain periodically
```

### Propensity Models: Likelihood to Buy, Churn, Engage

**Types of Propensity Models:**

| Model | What It Predicts | Key Features (Input Signals) | Output | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Propensity to Buy** | Likelihood of purchase within X days | Page views, cart adds, past purchases, email engagement, search queries | Score 0-100 | High score → retargeting + sales outreach |
| **Propensity to Churn** | Likelihood of leaving/canceling | Usage decline, support tickets, payment failures, competitor research | Risk score | High risk → retention campaign + human outreach |
| **Propensity to Engage** | Likelihood of opening email/clicking ad | Past engagement, send time preferences, content affinity, device | Engagement score | Low score → re-engagement or suppression |
| **Propensity to Upgrade** | Likelihood of moving to higher tier/plan | Feature usage depth, team size growth, API calls, limit hits | Upgrade score | High score → upsell campaign + account manager |
| **Propensity to Refer** | Likelihood of recommending to others | NPS score, social sharing, repeat purchases, review history | Advocacy score | High score → referral program invitation |

**Building a Propensity-to-Buy Model — Practical Example:**

| Step | Action | Tool Options |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Define conversion | Purchase within 14 days of first visit | Business definition |
| 2. Label historical data | Tag users who converted vs. didn't in past 6 months | SQL/BigQuery |
| 3. Select features | Sessions count, pages viewed, products viewed, email opens, cart adds, time on site, referral source | Data warehouse |
| 4. Train model | Logistic regression or XGBoost | BigQuery ML, DataRobot, Python |
| 5. Evaluate | AUC > 0.75, Precision > 70%, Recall > 60% | Model evaluation metrics |
| 6. Activate | Score all current users nightly; push to ad platforms and email | CDP, Google Ads, Meta |

**Real-Time Example:**

Their "propensity to keep" model predicts which items in a Fix (box of clothing) a customer will keep vs. return — with 85% accuracy. ### Lead Scoring with Machine Learning

**Traditional vs. ML Lead Scoring:**

| Aspect | Traditional (Manual Points) | ML-Powered |
|---|---|---|
| **Method** | Marketing assigns point values (visited pricing = +10 pts) | Algorithm learns which behaviors correlate with conversion |
| **Accuracy** | 30-40% (based on assumptions) | 70-85% (based on data) |
| **Maintenance** | Manual updates quarterly | Self-improving with new data |
| **Bias** | Reflects marketing team's biases | Reflects actual data patterns (but needs bias monitoring) |
| **Adaptability** | Static until manually updated | Adapts as buyer behavior changes |

**ML Lead Scoring Features:**

| Feature Category | Specific Features | Weight (typical) |
|---|---|---|
| **Firmographic** | Company size, industry, revenue, location | 15-20% |
| **Demographic** | Job title, seniority, department | 10-15% |
| **Behavioral — Website** | Pages visited, pricing page views, feature page depth | 25-30% |
| **Behavioral — Email** | Open rate, click rate, unsubscribe | 10-15% |
| **Behavioral — Content** | Downloads, webinar attendance, video completion | 10-15% |
| **Intent signals** | Search queries, competitor comparison pages, review site visits | 15-20% |
| **Temporal** | Recency of engagement, velocity of activity | 5-10% |

**Lead Scoring Model Building:**

```
Step 1: DEFINE "Qualified Lead"
   └── SQL (Sales Qualified Lead): Lead that sales accepted and worked
   └── Typically: Had a demo/meeting AND had authority + budget

Step 2: GATHER HISTORICAL DATA (6-12 months)
   └── All leads with known outcomes (converted to SQL or not)
   └── Minimum: 500 SQLs and 2,000+ non-SQLs

Step 3: FEATURE ENGINEERING
   └── Calculate: total page views, pricing page visits, email engagement
       score, content downloads, days since first touch, lead source

Step 4: TRAIN MODEL
   └── Algorithm: XGBoost or logistic regression
   └── Tool: HubSpot predictive scoring, Salesforce Einstein, or BigQuery ML

Step 5: SCORE AND ACTIVATE
   └── Score all leads 0-100
   └── A leads (80-100): Immediate sales outreach
   └── B leads (60-79): SDR follow-up within 24 hours
   └── C leads (40-59): Nurture sequence
   └── D leads (0-39): Marketing automation only
```

### Multi-Touch Attribution with Machine Learning

**The Attribution Problem:**
A customer might interact with your brand 20+ times before purchasing. Which touchpoints deserve credit for the conversion?

**Attribution Models Compared:**

| Model | How It Works | Pros | Cons | Accuracy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Last Click** | 100% credit to last touchpoint | Simple, easy to implement | Ignores awareness/consideration journey | Low |
| **First Click** | 100% credit to first touchpoint | Values awareness | Ignores nurturing and conversion touchpoints | Low |
| **Linear** | Equal credit to all touchpoints | Fair distribution | Treats all touches equally (unrealistic) | Medium |
| **Time Decay** | More credit to recent touchpoints | Values conversion-proximate touches | Undervalues awareness | Medium |
| **Position-Based** | 40% first, 40% last, 20% middle | Values both awareness and conversion | Arbitrary weight allocation | Medium |
| **Data-Driven (ML)** | ML determines credit based on actual impact | Most accurate; adapts to your data | Requires significant conversion data (300+/month) | High |
| **Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM)** | Econometric model using aggregate data | Privacy-safe; includes offline channels | Slower to update; less granular | High (different use case) |

**Data-Driven Attribution — How ML Works:**

```
The ML model (Shapley value-based or Markov chain) analyzes thousands of 
conversion paths and asks:

"If we REMOVED touchpoint X from all journeys, how many conversions would 
we lose?"

Example conversion paths:
Path 1: Social → Blog → Email → Purchase ✓
Path 2: Search → Blog → Purchase ✓
Path 3: Social → Blog → No Purchase ✗
Path 4: Search → Email → Purchase ✓

The ML model calculates that:
- Blog appears in both converting and non-converting paths → Medium credit
- Email appears only in converting paths → Higher credit
- Social appears in both converting and non-converting → Lower credit
- Search appears only in converting paths → Higher credit
```

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Airbnb** moved from last-click attribution to ML-based multi-touch attribution using their internal data science platform. The ML model revealed that branded search (which received most credit under last-click) was getting 50% too much credit, while upper-funnel display and social were getting 60% too little. By reallocating budget based on ML attribution, they improved overall ROAS by 15% — shifting $50M+ of annual budget from branded search to awareness channels that ML proved were driving incremental bookings.

### Marketing Mix Modeling with AI

**What Is Marketing Mix Modeling (MMM)?**
An econometric approach that uses aggregate data (not user-level) to measure the impact of each marketing channel on business outcomes. Privacy-safe alternative to user-level attribution.

**MMM vs. Multi-Touch Attribution:**

| Aspect | MMM | MTA (Multi-Touch Attribution) |
|---|---|---|
| Data type | Aggregate (weekly/monthly totals) | User-level (individual journeys) |
| Privacy | Privacy-safe (no user tracking) | Requires cross-device/cross-site tracking |
| Channels covered | ALL channels including offline (TV, radio, OOH) | Digital channels only |
| Granularity | Channel level | Touchpoint/ad level |
| Speed | Slower (needs 2+ years of data; monthly updates) | Real-time or near-real-time |
| Best for | Budget allocation across channels | Campaign optimization within channels |

**Modern AI-Enhanced MMM Tools:**

| Tool | Developer | Key Innovation | Pricing |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Meridian** | Google | Open-source Bayesian MMM with Google data integration | Free (open source) |
| **Robyn** | Meta | Open-source automated MMM with ridge regression | Free (open source) |
| **LiftLab** | LiftLab | Automated MMM with scenario planning | Enterprise pricing |
| **Measured** | Measured | Always-on incrementality + MMM | Enterprise pricing |
| **Recast** | Recast | Bayesian MMM designed for growth companies | $5K+/month |
| **Cassandra** | Uber (internal) | Real-time MMM for marketplace businesses | Internal only |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **HelloFresh** uses Meta's Robyn (open-source MMM) to model the impact of their marketing mix across 15+ countries. By running MMM quarterly, they discovered: (1) TV advertising had 2x the long-term impact they originally measured (because the effects lasted 8 weeks, not 2), (2) Podcast ads had the highest incremental ROAS of any channel but were receiving only 3% of budget, (3) Paid social had diminishing returns beyond $X/month per market. **Essential SQL for Marketing:**

| SQL Concept | Marketing Use Case | Example Query Pattern |
|---|---|---|
| **SELECT + WHERE** | Filter campaign data | "Show me all campaigns with CTR > 2% in Q1" |
| **GROUP BY** | Aggregate by dimension | "Total revenue by channel by month" |
| **JOIN** | Combine data from multiple tables | "Match CRM contacts with website behavior" |
| **COUNT, SUM, AVG** | Calculate KPIs | "Average order value by customer segment" |
| **DATE functions** | Time-based analysis | "Week-over-week change in sign-ups" |
| **CASE WHEN** | Create custom categories | "Classify leads as Hot/Warm/Cold based on score" |
| **Window functions** | Running calculations | "7-day rolling average of daily conversions" |

**Practical SQL Examples for Marketing:**

```sql
-- Top 10 landing pages by conversion rate (this month)
SELECT 
    landing_page,
    COUNT(*) as sessions,
    SUM(CASE WHEN converted = 1 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) as conversions,
    ROUND(SUM(CASE WHEN converted = 1 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) * 100.0 / COUNT(*), 2) as conversion_rate
FROM website_sessions
WHERE session_date >= DATE_TRUNC('month', CURRENT_DATE)
GROUP BY landing_page
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 100
ORDER BY conversion_rate DESC
LIMIT 10;
```

```sql
-- Customer cohort analysis: Monthly retention
SELECT 
    first_purchase_month,
    months_since_first,
    COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) as customers,
    ROUND(COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id) * 100.0 / 
        FIRST_VALUE(COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id)) OVER (
            PARTITION BY first_purchase_month ORDER BY months_since_first
        ), 1) as retention_rate
FROM customer_cohorts
GROUP BY first_purchase_month, months_since_first
ORDER BY first_purchase_month, months_since_first;
```

### Understanding Model Evaluation: Accuracy, Precision, Recall

**Why This Matters for Marketers:**
When data science teams present ML model results, marketers need to understand whether the model is good enough for business decisions.

**Key Metrics Explained in Marketing Context:**

| Metric | Definition | Marketing Translation | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | % of correct predictions overall | "How often is the model right?" | "The model correctly identifies 85% of leads" |
| **Precision** | Of predicted positives, how many are actually positive | "When the model says YES, how reliable is it?" | "When model says 'will buy,' 75% actually buy" — high precision = fewer wasted sales calls |
| **Recall** | Of actual positives, how many did we catch | "How many real opportunities did we miss?" | "Of customers who actually churned, we predicted 80%" — high recall = fewer missed saves |
| **F1 Score** | Harmonic mean of precision and recall | Balance between reliability and completeness | F1 of 0.8+ is generally good for marketing |
| **AUC-ROC** | Model's ability to distinguish between classes | "How well does the model separate buyers from non-buyers?" | AUC of 0.8+ is good; 0.9+ is excellent |

**When to Prioritize Which Metric:**

| Scenario | Prioritize | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Lead scoring for expensive sales outreach** | Precision | Each sales call costs $50+; can't waste on bad leads |
| **Churn prediction for retention** | Recall | Missing a churning customer is more costly than sending an extra email |
| **Fraud detection** | Recall | Missing fraud is catastrophic; false alarms are tolerable |
| **Email send optimization** | F1 Score | Balance between reaching interested users and not annoying uninterested ones |
| **Ad targeting** | AUC-ROC | Want the model to be good at ranking users by conversion probability |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **HubSpot's predictive lead scoring** communicates model quality to non-technical users by showing: "Leads in the top 20% of our scoring model are 5x more likely to close than average leads." This business-language framing (rather than "AUC = 0.82") makes the model's value immediately clear to sales leadership.

```sql
-- Create a churn prediction model with SQL (no Python needed)
CREATE OR REPLACE MODEL `project.dataset.churn_model`
OPTIONS(
    model_type='LOGISTIC_REG',
    input_label_cols=['churned']
) AS
SELECT
    days_since_last_purchase,
    total_orders,
    average_order_value,
    support_tickets_last_90d,
    email_engagement_score,
    churned
FROM `project.dataset.customer_features`
WHERE signup_date < '2025-01-01';

-- Score current customers
SELECT 
    customer_id,
    predicted_churned,
    predicted_churned_probs[OFFSET(1)].prob as churn_probability
FROM ML.PREDICT(
    MODEL `project.dataset.churn_model`,
    (SELECT * FROM `project.dataset.current_customers`)
)
WHERE predicted_churned_probs[OFFSET(1)].prob > 0.5
ORDER BY churn_probability DESC;
```

   - Identify top 3 prediction use cases (churn, lead scoring, demand forecasting)
   - Assess data availability and quality for each use case

2. **Week 3-4: Skill Building**
   - Start SQL training for marketing team (2-week sprint)
   - Identify 1-2 "data champions" for deeper Python/ML training
   - Set up access to BigQuery/data warehouse for marketing team

3. **Week 5-8: First Predictive Model**
   - Select the highest-impact, most feasible prediction use case
   - Prepare training data (historical outcomes + features)
   - Build model using no-code tool (BigQuery ML or DataRobot)
   - Validate model accuracy; test with holdout group

4. **Month 3-4: Operationalize**
   - Deploy predictive scores into marketing workflows
   - Set up automated anomaly detection dashboards
   - Implement natural language querying for the team

5. **Month 5-6: Advanced Analytics**
   - Build marketing mix model (Robyn or Meridian)
   - Implement data-driven attribution
   - Create a prediction-driven campaign framework
   - Establish regular model retraining schedule

## Key Gaps to Watch

1. **Data Quality Gap** — Predictive models are only as good as input data; most marketing data has 20-30% quality issues
2. **Talent Gap** — Only 15% of marketing teams have someone who can build and interpret ML models
3. **Actionability Gap** — Many teams build predictive models but fail to operationalize predictions into campaigns
4. **Attribution Gap** — Cookie deprecation is making user-level attribution increasingly difficult; MMM is resurging as an alternative
5. **Real-Time Gap** — Most analytics are still backward-looking; real-time anomaly detection and prediction remain underadopted
6. **Cross-Channel Gap** — Data sits in platform silos (Google, Meta, email, CRM); unified cross-channel analysis requires significant data engineering
7. **Explainability Gap** — Complex ML models are hard to explain to stakeholders; this limits adoption and trust

## Best Practices Summary

1. **Start with business questions, not models** — "What decision would I make differently if I had this prediction?" should always come first
2. **Build on clean data foundations** — Invest in data quality and unification before building predictive models
3. **Use no-code tools first** — BigQuery ML, DataRobot, and similar tools let marketers build models without data science dependencies
4. **Validate with business metrics** — Model accuracy (AUC/F1) matters less than business impact (revenue lift, churn reduction)
5. **Start simple** — Logistic regression often performs within 5% of complex models and is much easier to explain and maintain
6. **Operationalize or it's wasted** — A model that produces scores must be integrated into campaigns, dashboards, or workflows
7. **Retrain regularly** — Customer behavior changes; models degrade over time; schedule quarterly retraining minimum
8. **Combine MMM + MTA** — Use marketing mix modeling for channel-level budget allocation and multi-touch attribution for campaign optimization
9. **Invest in SQL literacy** — Teaching marketers SQL has the highest ROI of any technical skill investment
10. **Create a testing culture** — Every prediction should be validated with holdout groups and incrementality testing

## 12.5.1 Location-Based Marketing Strategies

### Definition & Context

Location-based marketing (LBM) uses a mobile device's geographic position to deliver targeted, contextually relevant marketing messages, ads, or experiences to users based on where they are, where they've been, or where they're going. It bridges the physical and digital worlds — turning real-world movement into marketing signals.

### Location-Based Marketing Methods

#### 1. Geotargeting

Geotargeting delivers ads or content to users based on their current geographic location — at the city, zip code, neighborhood, or even street level.

**How it works:**
- User's location detected via IP address, GPS, or Wi-Fi
- Ad platforms match location to targeting parameters
- Ads served only to users in the defined geographic area

| Platform | Geotargeting Capability | Minimum Radius |
|---|---|---|
| **Google Ads** | Country → zip code → radius | 1 km radius |
| **Meta (Facebook/Instagram)** | Country → city → radius | 1 mile radius |
| **Snapchat** | Country → city → radius | Custom polygons |
| **DV360/Programmatic** | Latitude/longitude targeting | 100 meter radius |
| **Apple Search Ads** | Country → state/region | State-level only |

**Real-Time Example — McDonald's Geotargeted Ads:**
McDonald's runs Google Ads that target users within a 3 km radius of each restaurant during breakfast hours (7-10 AM). The ad creative changes based on proximity: at 3 km it shows the breakfast menu, at 1 km it shows "Just around the corner," and within 200m it shows a "Walk-in and order" CTA with the specific store address. This proximity-graduated messaging increased breakfast sales by 18%.

#### 2. Geofencing

Geofencing creates a virtual boundary around a specific geographic area. When a user's mobile device enters or exits this boundary, it triggers a predefined action (push notification, SMS, ad serve, or data collection).

**How it works:**

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     GEOFENCING MECHANICS                          │
│                                                                   │
│        ┌──────────────────────────┐                               │
│        │    Virtual Boundary       │                               │
│        │    (50m – 50km radius)    │                               │
│        │                          │                               │
│        │     ┌──────────────┐     │                               │
│        │     │  Target       │     │                               │
│        │     │  Location     │     │                               │
│        │     │  (Store/Event)│     │                               │
│        │     └──────────────┘     │                               │
│        │                          │                               │
│        └──────────────────────────┘                               │
│                                                                   │
│  User ENTERS geofence → Trigger: Push notification / Ad / Log     │
│  User DWELLS in geofence → Trigger: Follow-up message / Offer     │
│  User EXITS geofence → Trigger: Thank you / Survey / Retarget     │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Geofencing use cases:**

| Use Case | Geofence Location | Trigger Action | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Own store promotion** | Around your own stores | Push: "Welcome! Show this for 15% off" | Starbucks reward notification at store entry |
| **Competitor conquesting** | Around competitor stores | Ad: "Why pay more? We're cheaper" | Burger King's "Whopper Detour" at McDonald's locations |
| **Event marketing** | Around event venues | Push: "Visit our booth at Hall 3 for freebies" | Red Bull at music festivals |
| **Tourism/travel** | Around landmarks | App: "You're near the Taj Mahal — here's the audio guide" | Google Maps/TripAdvisor |
| **Employee tracking** | Around office/job sites | Log: Check-in/out for attendance | Field workforce management |

**Real-Time Example — Burger King "Whopper Detour":**
Burger King created a geofence around every McDonald's location in the US. When a Burger King app user entered a McDonald's geofence, they received a push notification offering a Whopper for just 1 cent — but only if they ordered through the BK app while at McDonald's. The app then navigated them to the nearest Burger King for pickup. Results: 1.5M app downloads in 9 days, #1 app in the App Store, and 3.5x increase in mobile sales. This campaign won the Cannes Grand Prix.

#### 3. Beacon Technology (Bluetooth Low Energy)

Beacons are small Bluetooth devices placed at physical locations that communicate with nearby smartphones (within 1-70 meters) to trigger actions.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Technology** | Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) |
| **Range** | 1–70 meters (configurable) |
| **Accuracy** | ±1-3 meters (much more precise than GPS) |
| **Battery life** | 1–3 years per beacon |
| **Cost per beacon** | $5–$30 |
| **Protocols** | iBeacon (Apple), Eddystone (Google) |
| **Requirement** | User must have app installed + Bluetooth enabled |

**How beacons work:**

```
Step 1: Beacon broadcasts unique ID via Bluetooth
        ↓
Step 2: User's phone app detects beacon signal
        ↓
Step 3: App identifies beacon location (e.g., "Aisle 3, Electronics")
        ↓
Step 4: App triggers action (push notification, content, coupon)
        ↓
Step 5: User engages (views offer, navigates to product, makes purchase)
```

**Real-Time Example — Target (US Retail):**
Target deployed 3,000+ beacons across their stores. When a user with the Target app walks past the electronics section, they receive a personalized notification: "Sarah, that Samsung TV you browsed online is on display right here — and it's $100 off today." Their beacon program increased in-store conversions by 16% for digitally influenced purchases.

#### 6. Foot Traffic Measurement & Attribution

**The challenge:** Proving that a digital ad led to a physical store visit.

**Methods:**

| Method | How It Works | Accuracy | Provider |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Google Store Visits** | Uses GPS/WiFi from opted-in Google users to detect store visits post-ad-click | High (modeled data) | Google Ads |
| **Meta Store Visits** | Uses Facebook app location data to attribute foot traffic | Medium-High | Meta Ads Manager |
| **SDK-based measurement** | Partner apps with location SDKs track store visits | High | Foursquare, Cuebiq |
| **Beacon-based** | In-store beacons confirm physical presence | Very High | inMarket, Gimbal |
| **Wi-Fi/cellular** | Detects device presence via store Wi-Fi or cellular signals | High | RetailNext, Shoppertrak |
| **Survey-based** | "Did you visit our store recently?" post-exposure survey | Low (recall bias) | Brand Lift studies |

### In-Store Beacon Deployment

**Deployment planning:**

### Click-and-Collect / BOPIS Marketing

BOPIS (Buy Online, Pick Up In Store) has become a critical channel — especially post-pandemic.

| Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| **Consumers who used BOPIS (2024)** | 67% |
| **Additional in-store purchases during BOPIS pickup** | 85% of BOPIS customers buy something else in-store |
| **Speed expectation** | 70% expect same-day pickup |
| **Revenue impact** | BOPIS customers spend 25% more per order than ship-to-home |

**BOPIS marketing flow:**

```
Online browse → "Pick up in 2 hours" CTA → Order confirmation + map →
Arrival detection (geofence) → "We're preparing your order" push →
Pickup ready notification → In-store upsell offer → Post-pickup feedback
```

**Real-Time Example — Decathlon India:**
Decathlon's app allows BOPIS with a 2-hour pickup promise. When the customer approaches the store (geofence trigger), the app sends a push: "We see you're nearby! Your order is ready at Counter 3." During pickup, the app shows personalized recommendations: "Customers who bought this cricket bat also bought these grips." This drives a 22% additional purchase rate during pickup.

## Implementation Steps for Location-Based Marketing

### Step 1: Location Data Foundation (Week 1–2)
1. Audit current location data collection (app permissions, web tracking)
2. Ensure app requests location permission at contextually relevant moment
3. Choose location technology stack (GPS + beacons + Wi-Fi)
4. Set up Google Business Profile for all store locations
5. Implement location consent flow compliant with privacy regulations

### Step 2: Geotargeting Campaigns (Week 3–4)
1. Set up location-targeted campaigns on Google Ads and Meta
2. Create radius-based audiences around store locations
3. Build competitor geofences (for conquesting campaigns)
4. Design location-specific ad creative (mention nearby landmarks, store name)
5. Enable Store Visit conversion tracking in Google Ads

### Step 3: Proximity Infrastructure (Week 5–8)
1. Deploy beacons in top-performing store locations
2. Configure beacon triggers and notification content
3. Build BOPIS/curbside pickup flow in app
4. Integrate proximity data with loyalty program
5. Set up foot traffic measurement and attribution

### Step 4: Optimization (Ongoing)
1. Analyze foot traffic attribution data weekly
2. A/B test geofence radius sizes (smaller = more relevant, larger = more reach)
3. Test beacon notification content and timing
4. Expand beacon deployment to additional locations based on ROI
5. Monitor privacy regulation changes and adjust consent flows

## Strategies

### 1. Hyperlocal Conquesting Strategy
- Geofence around competitor stores
- Serve mobile ads to competitor's customers with compelling switch messaging
- Track store visit conversions for users who saw your conquesting ads
- Measure incremental lift vs. users who didn't see the ads
- Budget tip: Start with your top competitor's 10 highest-traffic locations

### 2. Weather + Location Dynamic Creative
- Combine real-time weather data with location for contextual ads
- Rainy day near your cafe → "Perfect weather for a hot latte — we're 2 min away"
- Hot day near your store → "Cool off with a Frappuccino — GPS says you're close"
- Tools: WeatherAPI + programmatic DSP (DV360) + dynamic creative

### 3. Event-Based Geofencing
- Geofence around concerts, sports stadiums, trade shows, festivals
- Target attendees during and after events
- Retarget event visitors for 30 days post-event
- Use case: A sportswear brand geofences around IPL stadiums, serving ads during matches and retargeting cricket fans for 4 weeks after

### 4. Location-Based Retargeting
- Build audience segments from location data: "Visited competitor store," "Lives within 5km of our store," "Visited a gym in the last 30 days"
- Retarget these segments across programmatic display, social, and search
- Measure store visit uplift from retargeting campaigns

## Best Practices

1. **Request location permission with context** — "We need your location to find nearby stores and send you relevant deals" (not a cold request)
2. **Keep geofences appropriately sized** — 200-500m for retail, 1-5km for brand awareness, 50-100m for in-store beacons
3. **Don't stalk users** — 1-2 location-based messages per visit maximum; never follow users with notifications after they leave
4. **Be genuinely useful** — "The checkout line is shorter at our downtown store" is more valuable than "Buy stuff!"
5. **Combine location with behavior** — Location alone is weak; location + browsing history + purchase data = powerful
6. **Test different geofence sizes** — Smaller isn't always better; test radius impact on conversion
7. **Update Google Business Profile weekly** — Fresh content signals activity; posts expire after 7 days
8. **Measure incrementality** — Not all store visits are caused by your ads; use control groups to measure true lift
9. **Respect privacy regulations** — GDPR, CCPA, and India's DPDPA require explicit consent for location tracking
10. **Think about the "after"** — Post-visit retargeting and feedback collection are as important as the in-store notification

## Privacy Considerations for Location Marketing

| Regulation | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **GDPR (EU)** | Explicit consent for location tracking; right to erasure; data minimization |
| **CCPA (California)** | Right to opt-out of location data sale; disclosure of data collection |
| **DPDPA (India)** | Consent for personal data processing; purpose limitation |
| **Apple iOS** | Users choose "Allow Once," "While Using App," or "Always." "Always" is increasingly rare |
| **Google Android 12+** | "Precise" vs. "Approximate" location choice; background location requires separate permission |

**Best approach:** Collect the minimum location precision needed. If city-level works, don't request precise GPS. If foreground location suffices, don't ask for background access.

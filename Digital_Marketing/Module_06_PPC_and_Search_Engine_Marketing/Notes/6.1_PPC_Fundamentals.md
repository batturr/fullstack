## 6.1.1 Introduction to Paid Search

### What is PPC and How Does It Work

Pay-Per-Click (PPC) is a digital advertising model where advertisers pay a fee each time their ad is clicked. Instead of earning visits organically, you buy visits to your site. The advertiser sets bids on keywords, creates ads, and when a user searches for those keywords, the ad appears — the advertiser pays only when the user clicks.

**The PPC Lifecycle:**

```
Advertiser          Search Engine           User
    │                    │                    │
    │  Creates ads,      │                    │
    │  sets bids,        │                    │
    │  defines keywords  │                    │
    │───────────────────►│                    │
    │                    │    User searches    │
    │                    │◄───────────────────│
    │                    │                    │
    │                    │  Runs ad auction    │
    │                    │  (milliseconds)     │
    │                    │                    │
    │                    │  Shows winning ads  │
    │                    │───────────────────►│
    │                    │                    │
    │                    │    User clicks ad   │
    │                    │◄───────────────────│
    │                    │                    │
    │  Pays per click    │                    │
    │◄───────────────────│                    │
    │                    │                    │
    │  User lands on     │                    │
    │  advertiser page   │                    │
    │◄─────────────────────────────────────────│
```

**Real-Time Example — Zomato (India):**
When you search "restaurants near me" on Google, Zomato's paid ad appears above organic results. Zomato bids on this keyword, and when you click the ad and open the Zomato app/website, Zomato pays Google the CPC (say Rs. 8-15 per click). If 10,000 people click daily at Rs. 12 average CPC, Zomato spends Rs. 1.2 lakh/day — but if 5% of those clickers place an order worth Rs. 500 average, that's 500 orders = Rs. 2.5 lakh revenue. Net profit from that single keyword.

### PPC vs. SEO: Complementary Strategies

| Dimension | PPC (Paid) | SEO (Organic) |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Instant — ads live within hours | Slow — 3-6 months for rankings |
| **Cost** | Pay per click (ongoing spend) | Free clicks but costs time, content, links |
| **Position** | Above organic results (top 1-4) | Below ads, positions 1-10 |
| **Control** | Full control over messaging, timing | Google controls snippet/title display |
| **Trust** | Users know it's an ad (lower trust) | Higher trust — earned ranking |
| **Longevity** | Stops when budget stops | Compounds over time |
| **Testing** | Rapid A/B testing of messages | Slow to test — algorithm dependent |
| **Data** | Immediate keyword performance data | Delayed data via Search Console |

**Why They're Complementary (Not Competing):**

1. **PPC tests what SEO should target** — Run ads for 50 keywords, find the 10 that convert best, then invest in SEO for those 10
2. **SEO reduces PPC dependency** — As organic rankings grow, reduce PPC spend on those terms
3. **PPC fills SEO gaps** — Rank organically for brand terms, use PPC for competitor terms where organic ranking is hard
4. **Both together dominate SERPs** — Having both a paid ad and organic result for the same query increases total clicks by 89% (Google study)

**Real-Time Example — Freshworks (SaaS):**
- **SEO:** Ranks #1 organically for "best CRM software for small business" through blog content
- **PPC:** Runs Google Ads for "Salesforce alternatives" (a competitor term they can't rank #1 for organically)
- **Combined:** For "CRM software," they appear both as a paid ad (position 1) and organic result (position 3-5), capturing 2x the clicks

### The PPC Auction Model

Every time a user searches on Google, an auction happens in milliseconds. The three core components:

**1. Bid** — The maximum amount you're willing to pay per click
**2. Quality Score** — Google's rating (1-10) of your ad's overall quality
**3. Ad Rank** — The final score that determines your ad position

```
Ad Rank = Max CPC Bid × Quality Score × Expected Impact of Extensions

Example:
┌──────────────┬──────────┬───────────────┬──────────┬──────────┐
│  Advertiser  │ Max Bid  │ Quality Score │ Ad Rank  │ Position │
├──────────────┼──────────┼───────────────┼──────────┼──────────┤
│  Brand A     │ Rs. 20   │ 9/10          │ 180      │ #1       │
│  Brand B     │ Rs. 30   │ 5/10          │ 150      │ #2       │
│  Brand C     │ Rs. 25   │ 4/10          │ 100      │ #3       │
│  Brand D     │ Rs. 15   │ 6/10          │ 90       │ Not shown│
└──────────────┴──────────┴───────────────┴──────────┴──────────┘

Brand A wins position #1 despite bidding LESS than Brand B.
This is the power of Quality Score.
```

**Key Insight:** You don't always pay your max bid. In a second-price auction (Google's model), you pay just enough to beat the advertiser below you. Brand A with Ad Rank 180 might pay only Rs. 17 per click — just enough to stay above Brand B.

### Cost Models in PPC

| Model | Full Name | You Pay For | Best For | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **CPC** | Cost Per Click | Each click on your ad | Direct response, traffic | Google Search Ads |
| **CPM** | Cost Per Mille (1000 impressions) | Every 1,000 views of your ad | Brand awareness, display | YouTube masthead |
| **CPA** | Cost Per Acquisition | Each conversion (sale/lead) | Performance campaigns | Lead gen with target CPA |
| **CPV** | Cost Per View | Each video view (30 sec or full) | Video campaigns | YouTube TrueView ads |
| **CPL** | Cost Per Lead | Each lead captured | B2B, SaaS, services | LinkedIn Lead Gen Forms |

**Real-Time Example — Cost Comparison for a Travel Agency:**
- **CPC:** Bidding Rs. 25 per click on "Bali tour packages" — 100 clicks = Rs. 2,500 spent
- **CPM:** Paying Rs. 150 per 1,000 impressions on travel blog display ads — 50,000 impressions = Rs. 7,500
- **CPA:** Setting a Rs. 500 target CPA — Google auto-optimizes to get leads at Rs. 500 each
- **CPV:** Paying Rs. 2 per YouTube video view of their Bali travel vlog ad — 10,000 views = Rs. 20,000

### When to Invest in PPC

| Scenario | Why PPC Makes Sense | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **New launch** | No organic presence yet, need immediate visibility | A new D2C brand launching on Shopify needs traffic on day one |
| **High competition** | Organic rankings take 6+ months in competitive niches | Insurance, loans, legal — dominated by big players organically |
| **Seasonality** | Capture demand spikes during peak periods | Flipkart bidding heavily on "Diwali sale" during October |
| **Testing** | Validate product-market fit or messaging before SEO investment | Testing if "AI writing tool" or "content generator" converts better |
| **Retargeting** | Re-engage visitors who didn't convert | Showing ads to users who added to cart but didn't buy |
| **Competitor defense** | Prevent competitors from stealing your brand traffic | Swiggy bidding on "Zomato" as a keyword |

## 6.1.2 Understanding the Ad Auction

### How Real-Time Bidding Works

Every single Google search triggers an auction — billions of auctions per day. The process takes under 100 milliseconds:

**Step-by-Step Auction Process:**

```
Step 1: User types "best running shoes" → hits Enter
            │
Step 2: Google identifies ALL advertisers bidding on this keyword
            │ (could be 50+ advertisers)
            │
Step 3: Google FILTERS out ineligible ads
            │ - Budget exhausted
            │ - Location mismatch
            │ - Ad scheduling exclusion
            │ - Disapproved ads
            │
Step 4: For remaining ads, Google calculates Ad Rank
            │ = Bid × Quality Score × Extensions Impact
            │
Step 5: Ads RANKED by Ad Rank score (highest to lowest)
            │
Step 6: Top 3-4 ads displayed above organic results
            │ Remaining 1-3 shown below organic results
            │
Step 7: Actual CPC calculated (second-price + 1 cent rule)
            │
ALL THIS HAPPENS IN < 100 MILLISECONDS
```

**Real-Time Example — "Car Insurance" Auction:**

This is one of the most expensive keywords globally (CPC: $50-$80 in the US, Rs. 150-400 in India).

When someone searches "car insurance," HDFC ERGO, Bajaj Allianz, ICICI Lombard, Acko, and PolicyBazaar are all bidding. Google evaluates each ad's quality, bid, and extensions. PolicyBazaar might win position #1 with a Rs. 200 bid and 9/10 Quality Score, while HDFC ERGO at Rs. 350 bid but 5/10 Quality Score gets position #2.

### Quality Score Components (Deep Dive)

Quality Score is Google's 1-10 rating of your keyword's overall health. It has three components:

```
QUALITY SCORE (1-10)
├── Expected CTR (Click-Through Rate)
│   ├── Historical CTR of your ads for this keyword
│   ├── Compared to average CTR for that ad position
│   └── Rating: Above Average / Average / Below Average
│
├── Ad Relevance
│   ├── How closely your ad copy matches the search intent
│   ├── Does the keyword appear in headlines/descriptions?
│   └── Rating: Above Average / Average / Below Average
│
└── Landing Page Experience
    ├── Page load speed (under 3 seconds ideal)
    ├── Mobile-friendliness
    ├── Content relevance to the ad and keyword
    ├── Easy navigation and original content
    └── Rating: Above Average / Average / Below Average
```

**Quality Score Impact on CPC:**

| Quality Score | Average CPC Multiplier | If Base CPC = Rs. 100 |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | 0.50x (50% discount) | Rs. 50 |
| 8 | 0.75x (25% discount) | Rs. 75 |
| 7 | 1.00x (baseline) | Rs. 100 |
| 5 | 1.50x (50% premium) | Rs. 150 |
| 3 | 2.50x (150% premium) | Rs. 250 |
| 1 | 4.00x (300% premium) | Rs. 400 |

**Real-Time Example — Zerodha vs. a New Broker:**
- **Zerodha** bids on "open demat account" with Quality Score 9/10 (great landing page, high CTR, relevant ad). CPC: Rs. 15
- **New Broker X** bids on the same keyword with Quality Score 4/10 (slow page, generic ad). CPC: Rs. 45 for the same keyword
- Zerodha gets 3x more clicks for the same budget

### Ad Rank Formula: Deep Dive

```
Ad Rank = Max CPC Bid × Quality Score × Expected Impact of Ad Extensions

Where:
- Max CPC Bid: Your maximum willingness to pay per click
- Quality Score: 1-10 (factoring CTR, relevance, landing page)
- Extensions Impact: Estimated additional CTR from sitelinks, callouts, etc.
```

**Additional Ad Rank factors (Google's thresholds):**
1. **Ad Rank thresholds** — Minimum Ad Rank required to appear (varies by query competitiveness)
2. **Search context** — User's location, device, time of day, nature of search
3. **Auction competitiveness** — If two ads have similar Ad Rank, either could win
4. **Expected impact of assets** — How much sitelinks, callouts, structured snippets, and other extensions improve click probability

### First-Price vs. Second-Price Auctions

| Type | How It Works | Used By |
|---|---|---|
| **First-Price** | You pay exactly what you bid | Programmatic display (DV360, Trade Desk) |
| **Second-Price** | You pay $0.01 more than the next highest bidder | Google Search Ads (with modifications) |
| **Google's Generalized Second-Price** | You pay the minimum needed to maintain your position, based on the Ad Rank of the competitor below you | Google Ads (actual model) |

**Example — Second Price Auction:**

```
Advertiser A bids Rs. 50  → Ad Rank: 450 → Position #1 → Pays Rs. 34
Advertiser B bids Rs. 40  → Ad Rank: 320 → Position #2 → Pays Rs. 26
Advertiser C bids Rs. 30  → Ad Rank: 250 → Position #3 → Pays Rs. 21
Advertiser D bids Rs. 25  → Ad Rank: 200 → Not shown

(Each pays just enough to beat the one below them)
```

### Auction Insights and Competitive Metrics

Found inside Google Ads under `Keywords > Auction Insights`:

| Metric | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| **Impression Share** | % of auctions where your ad appeared out of total eligible |
| **Overlap Rate** | How often a competitor's ad appeared alongside yours |
| **Position Above Rate** | How often a competitor's ad ranked higher than yours |
| **Top of Page Rate** | How often your ad appeared at the top of the page |
| **Absolute Top of Page Rate** | How often your ad was the very first ad |
| **Outranking Share** | % of time your ad ranked higher than a specific competitor |

**Real-Time Example — Using Auction Insights:**
A Bangalore-based digital agency managing ads for a SaaS product sees that Zoho has 85% impression share vs. their client's 45%. Zoho's "Position Above Rate" is 72%. The agency recommends increasing budget (to gain impression share) and improving Quality Score (to improve position) rather than just raising bids.

### Ad Group Theming and Organization

**Rule of Thumb:** Each ad group should contain keywords that share the SAME intent so one ad can speak to all of them.

**Bad Ad Group (Too Broad):**
```
Ad Group: "Shoes"
Keywords: running shoes, formal shoes, baby shoes, shoe repair, shoe rack
→ One ad can't be relevant to ALL of these
```

**Good Ad Group (Tightly Themed):**
```
Ad Group: "Men's Running Shoes"
Keywords: men's running shoes, best running shoes for men, running shoes men online
→ One ad about men's running shoes is relevant to ALL keywords
```

### Naming Conventions and Labeling Systems

Consistent naming saves hours during optimization:

```
Convention: [Campaign Type] | [Product/Service] | [Targeting] | [Match Type]

Examples:
- Search | CRM Software | Non-Brand | Exact
- Search | CRM Software | Competitor | Phrase
- Display | CRM Software | Remarketing | Cart Abandoners
- Shopping | Electronics | High Priority | Custom Label
- Video | Brand Awareness | In-stream | Demographics 25-34
```

**Labels to Apply:**
- `Top Performer` — Keywords with CPA below target
- `Needs Review` — Keywords with high spend but no conversions
- `Seasonal` — Campaigns active only during peak periods
- `Test` — Experimental campaigns or ad copy tests
- `Paused - Review Q2` — Campaigns paused with a reason and date

## Implementation Steps

### Step 1: Account Setup
1. Create a Google Ads account at ads.google.com
2. Link Google Analytics 4 (GA4) and Google Search Console
3. Set up billing (credit card, UPI, or invoice for large spenders)
4. Install the Google Ads tag (or use Google Tag Manager)
5. Set up conversion tracking before launching any campaign

### Step 2: Research Phase
1. Use Google Keyword Planner for keyword discovery and bid estimates
2. Analyze competitors using SpyFu or SEMrush (see what keywords they bid on)
3. Review Auction Insights if you have existing campaigns
4. Define target CPA or ROAS based on unit economics

### Step 3: Campaign Architecture
1. Map your business goals to campaign types
2. Create a campaign naming convention document
3. Build ad groups with 5-20 tightly themed keywords each
4. Add negative keywords at campaign and account level
5. Set initial budgets based on keyword volume and estimated CPCs

### Step 4: Launch & Monitor
1. Start with manual CPC to gather data (first 2-4 weeks)
2. Monitor daily: spend, clicks, impressions, CPC, conversion rate
3. After 30-50 conversions, consider switching to automated bidding

## Gap Finding Checklist

| Gap Area | What to Look For | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Missing conversions** | No conversion tracking set up | Set up Google Ads tag or GTM immediately |
| **No negative keywords** | Search terms wasting budget on irrelevant queries | Add negative keyword lists from search term reports |
| **Poor account structure** | Too many keywords in one ad group | Restructure into tightly themed ad groups |
| **No competitor analysis** | Not knowing who you're competing against | Run SpyFu/SEMrush competitor analysis |
| **Wrong cost model** | Using CPC when CPM makes more sense (or vice versa) | Align cost model with campaign objective |
| **Budget misallocation** | Spreading budget equally across all campaigns | Shift budget to highest-performing campaigns |
| **No landing page alignment** | Ads going to homepage instead of relevant pages | Create dedicated landing pages per ad group |

## Strategies

1. **Start Small, Scale Fast** — Launch with 3-5 high-intent keywords, prove ROI, then expand
2. **Brand + Non-Brand Split** — Always separate brand campaigns (cheap, high CTR) from non-brand (expensive, lower CTR) for accurate performance measurement
3. **Competitor Conquest** — Bid on competitor brand names to capture comparison shoppers (legal and common)
4. **The 80/20 Rule** — 80% of conversions typically come from 20% of keywords; identify and protect those keywords
5. **Full-Funnel PPC** — Use Search for bottom-funnel, Display for mid-funnel awareness, YouTube for top-funnel brand
6. **Geographic Layering** — Create separate campaigns for top-performing cities with higher budgets

---

## Best Practices

1. **Never launch without conversion tracking** — Without it, you're flying blind
2. **Check search terms weekly** — Add irrelevant queries as negative keywords
3. **Don't set and forget** — PPC requires weekly optimization at minimum
4. **Test continuously** — Always have at least 2 ads per ad group running
5. **Quality Score > Bid Amount** — Invest in ad relevance and landing page quality before raising bids
6. **Use all available ad extensions** — They're free and improve CTR by 10-15%
7. **Set realistic expectations** — New accounts need 2-4 weeks of learning before optimization
8. **Segment data** — Analyze by device, location, time of day, and audience before making changes
9. **Document everything** — Keep a change log so you know what worked and what didn't
10. **Budget for testing** — Allocate 10-20% of budget for experimental keywords and ad copy

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Sending all traffic to the homepage** — Use dedicated landing pages
2. **Using only broad match keywords** — This drains budget on irrelevant searches
3. **Ignoring mobile performance** — Over 60% of clicks come from mobile
4. **Not linking Google Ads to Analytics** — You miss critical post-click behavior data
5. **Chasing vanity metrics** — Impressions and clicks don't matter if they don't convert
6. **Copying competitor ads** — Differentiate; don't replicate
7. **Pausing campaigns during low periods** — This resets learning; reduce budget instead

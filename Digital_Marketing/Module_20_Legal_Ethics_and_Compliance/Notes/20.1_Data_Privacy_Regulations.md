## 20.1.1 GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

### What Is GDPR

GDPR is the world's most comprehensive data privacy regulation, enacted by the European Union on May 25, 2018. It governs how organizations collect, store, process, and transfer personal data of EU/EEA residents — regardless of where the organization is located.

**Extraterritorial Scope:** If a company in India, the US, or Brazil processes data of someone in Germany, GDPR applies. This is what makes it a global regulation in practice, not just a European one.

### Who GDPR Applies To

| Entity | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Data Controller** | Decides why and how personal data is processed | An e-commerce brand collecting customer emails |
| **Data Processor** | Processes data on behalf of a controller | Mailchimp sending emails for that brand |
| **Data Subject** | The individual whose data is being processed | The customer who signed up |

**Applies if you:**
- Offer goods/services to EU residents (even free services)
- Monitor behavior of EU residents (analytics, tracking, profiling)
- Are established in the EU regardless of where processing occurs

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Google** was fined €50 million by France's CNIL in 2019 for lack of transparency and valid consent in personalized advertising. Users were not clearly informed about how their data was being used across Google services.
- **Meta/Facebook** was fined €1.2 billion in 2023 by Ireland's DPC for transferring EU user data to US servers without adequate protections — the largest GDPR fine in history.

### The 7 Key Principles of GDPR

| # | Principle | What It Means for Marketers |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Lawfulness, Fairness, Transparency** | Tell users clearly what data you collect and why — no hidden tracking |
| 2 | **Purpose Limitation** | Data collected for email marketing cannot be used for credit scoring |
| 3 | **Data Minimization** | Only collect what you need — don't ask for phone number if you only need email |
| 4 | **Accuracy** | Keep data up to date — provide preference centers for users to correct info |
| 5 | **Storage Limitation** | Don't keep data forever — set retention policies and auto-delete |
| 6 | **Integrity & Confidentiality** | Encrypt data, restrict access, prevent breaches |
| 7 | **Accountability** | Document everything — prove you comply, don't just claim it |

### Legal Bases for Processing Data

You must have at least ONE legal basis before touching personal data:

| Legal Basis | When It Applies | Marketing Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Consent** | User explicitly opts in | Email newsletter sign-up with double opt-in |
| **Legitimate Interest** | Business has a justified reason, balanced against user rights | Sending product recommendations to existing customers |
| **Contract** | Data needed to fulfill a contract | Processing shipping address for an order |
| **Legal Obligation** | Required by law | Retaining tax-related transaction records |
| **Vital Interests** | Protecting someone's life | Sharing patient data during a medical emergency |
| **Public Task** | Public authority performing duties | Government health campaign communications |

**Real-Time Example — Legitimate Interest vs. Consent:**
- **Legitimate Interest:** Amazon emailing existing customers about related products they browsed — the customer already has a relationship, and Amazon can argue this serves both parties.
- **Consent Required:** A company buying a third-party email list and cold-emailing EU residents — no prior relationship exists, explicit opt-in consent is required.

### Rights of Data Subjects (What Customers Can Demand)

| Right | What It Means | Practical Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Right of Access** | "Show me all data you have on me" | Customer requests a copy of all data — you must provide within 30 days |
| **Right to Rectification** | "My name is spelled wrong, fix it" | Update incorrect profile information upon request |
| **Right to Erasure** | "Delete everything about me" | Customer requests deletion — you remove from CRM, email lists, analytics |
| **Right to Portability** | "Give me my data in a usable format" | Export customer data as CSV/JSON to transfer to a competitor |
| **Right to Object** | "Stop using my data for marketing" | Customer opts out of profiling — you must stop immediately |
| **Right to Restrict Processing** | "Keep my data but stop using it" | Pause marketing while a dispute is being resolved |
| **Right Against Automated Decision-Making** | "Don't let algorithms decide for me" | Customer challenges an AI-driven credit scoring decision |

### Data Protection Officer (DPO) Requirements

A DPO is mandatory when:
- You are a public authority
- You process data on a large scale as a core activity
- You process special categories of data (health, religion, biometrics) at scale

**DPO Responsibilities:** Monitor compliance, advise on data protection impact assessments (DPIAs), act as contact point for supervisory authorities, train staff.

**Real-Time Example:**

### Penalties

| Tier | Maximum Fine | Violation Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Lower Tier** | Up to €10 million or 2% of global annual revenue | Technical/organizational failures (record-keeping, DPO appointment) |
| **Upper Tier** | Up to €20 million or 4% of global annual revenue | Violations of data subject rights, illegal cross-border transfers, consent failures |

### Impact on Digital Marketing

| Area | Before GDPR | After GDPR |
|---|---|---|
| **Email Marketing** | Single opt-in, pre-ticked boxes | Double opt-in required, granular consent |
| **Cookie Tracking** | Cookies set on page load | Prior consent required before non-essential cookies |
| **Retargeting** | Track users freely across the web | Requires consent, data processing agreements |
| **Analytics** | Full tracking by default | Consent-gated analytics, IP anonymization |
| **Lead Generation** | Buy lists, scrape emails | Consent-based lead capture only |
| **CRM Data** | Store indefinitely | Retention policies mandatory, right-to-erasure compliance |

### Implementation Steps for GDPR Compliance

1. **Data Mapping & Audit** — Catalog every data flow: what personal data you collect, where it is stored, who accesses it, and where it is transferred
2. **Legal Basis Assessment** — For every data processing activity, document which legal basis applies
3. **Privacy Policy Overhaul** — Rewrite in plain language, cover all required disclosures (identity, contact, DPO, purposes, recipients, transfers, retention, rights)
4. **Consent Mechanism Setup** — Implement double opt-in for email, granular cookie consent, clear affirmative action (no pre-ticked boxes)
5. **Data Subject Request Workflow** — Build a system to handle access/deletion/portability requests within 30 days
6. **Data Processing Agreements** — Execute DPAs with every third-party processor (Mailchimp, Google Analytics, HubSpot, etc.)
7. **Breach Response Plan** — Establish a 72-hour notification workflow with roles, escalation paths, and templates
8. **Staff Training** — Train marketing, sales, customer service, and IT teams on GDPR obligations
9. **Record of Processing Activities (ROPA)** — Maintain a detailed register as proof of compliance
10. **Regular Audits** — Conduct annual privacy audits and DPIAs for high-risk processing

### Common Gaps Found in Marketing Teams

| Gap | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No record of processing activities | €10M+ fine exposure | Create and maintain ROPA using OneTrust or spreadsheet |
| Pre-ticked consent boxes | Invalid consent = data collected illegally | Replace with unchecked opt-in checkboxes |
| Email lists purchased from third parties | No valid consent chain, direct violation | Stop immediately; build organic lists |
| No data retention policy | Storing data indefinitely violates storage limitation | Define retention periods per data type, automate deletion |
| Third-party tools without DPAs | Controller liable for processor violations | Audit all vendors and execute DPAs |
| No breach response plan | Failure to notify within 72 hours | Build incident response playbook and conduct drills |

### Best Practices

- **Privacy by Design:** Build privacy into every campaign, landing page, and form from the start — don't retrofit
- **Granular Consent:** Let users choose specifically (e.g., "marketing emails" separate from "product updates" separate from "partner offers")
- **Preference Center:** Give users a self-service dashboard to manage their consent and data
- **Double Opt-In:** Use confirmation emails for all subscriptions — it proves consent and improves list quality
- **Regular Purging:** Auto-delete inactive subscriber data after defined periods (e.g., 24 months of inactivity)
- **Vendor Audit:** Review every marketing tool's data practices annually — if they are non-compliant, you are liable

## 20.1.2 CCPA/CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act / California Privacy Rights Act)

### What Is CCPA/CPRA

The California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA), effective January 1, 2020, was the first major US state privacy law. It was amended and expanded by the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA), effective January 1, 2023, which created the California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) as a dedicated enforcement body.

### Who It Applies To

Your business is subject to CCPA/CPRA if it operates in California AND meets any ONE of these thresholds:

| Threshold | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Revenue** | Annual gross revenue exceeding $25 million |
| **Data Volume** | Buys, sells, or shares personal information of 100,000+ consumers/households |
| **Revenue from Data** | Derives 50% or more of annual revenue from selling/sharing personal information |

### Consumer Rights Under CCPA/CPRA

| Right | Description | Marketing Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Right to Know** | What personal info is collected, used, shared, sold | Must provide transparent disclosures in privacy policy |
| **Right to Delete** | Request deletion of personal information | CRM, analytics, ad platform data must be deletable on request |
| **Right to Opt-Out** | Opt out of sale/sharing of personal info | "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link required |
| **Right to Non-Discrimination** | Cannot penalize users who exercise rights | Cannot charge more or provide worse service to opt-out users |
| **Right to Correct** | Fix inaccurate personal information | Provide a mechanism for corrections |
| **Right to Limit** | Restrict use of sensitive personal information | Sensitive data (race, health, geolocation) requires opt-in or limit option |

### "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information"

CPRA expanded the original CCPA's "Do Not Sell" to include "sharing" — which covers cross-context behavioral advertising (sending data to ad platforms for retargeting, even without monetary exchange).

**Real-Time Example:**
When you visit a website and your browsing data is sent to Meta's pixel for retargeting ads on Facebook — that counts as "sharing." Under CPRA, the website must provide an opt-out link, and if a user clicks it, the pixel must be disabled for that user.

### CCPA/CPRA vs. GDPR — Key Differences

| Dimension | GDPR | CCPA/CPRA |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope** | Anyone processing EU residents' data | Businesses meeting revenue/data thresholds in California |
| **Default Consent** | Opt-in required before processing | Opt-out model (collect by default, user can opt out) |
| **Children** | Under 16 requires parental consent | Under 16 requires opt-in; under 13 requires parental consent |
| **Right to Delete** | Broad, with exceptions | Similar, with more business exceptions |
| **Private Right of Action** | Not directly (file with DPA) | Yes, for data breaches (statutory damages $100-$750 per incident) |
| **Enforcement** | Data Protection Authorities | California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) + Attorney General |
| **Penalties** | Up to €20M or 4% revenue | $2,500/violation (unintentional), $7,500/violation (intentional) |

**Real-Time Example:**
Sephora was fined $1.2 million in 2022 — the first CCPA enforcement action — for failing to disclose it was selling customer data to third parties and not providing a "Do Not Sell" opt-out link.

### Implementation Steps

1. **Data Inventory** — Map all personal information categories collected, sources, purposes, and third parties receiving data
2. **Privacy Policy Update** — Include all CCPA/CPRA-required disclosures (categories collected, purposes, third parties, retention periods, rights)
3. **"Do Not Sell or Share" Link** — Add a prominent link on your website homepage and footer
4. **Opt-Out Mechanism** — Implement Global Privacy Control (GPC) signal recognition
5. **Intake System for Consumer Requests** — Build a verified request process (two-step identity verification for deletion/access)
6. **Sensitive Data Handling** — Identify sensitive personal information and implement "Limit Use" mechanisms
7. **Service Provider Agreements** — Update contracts with all vendors to include CCPA/CPRA-required data processing terms
8. **Employee Training** — Train customer-facing staff on handling consumer rights requests
9. **Annual Risk Assessments** — CPRA requires annual cybersecurity audits for high-risk processing

### Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **DataGrail** | Automated consumer rights request management |
| **Ethyca (Fides)** | Open-source privacy engineering platform for DSR and consent |
| **Securiti** | Data intelligence platform — mapping, classification, DSR |
| **WireWheel** | Privacy compliance automation |
| **TrustArc** | End-to-end privacy management and assessments |

### Gaps Commonly Found

| Gap | Consequence |
|---|---|
| No "Do Not Sell or Share" link | Direct violation — $7,500 per intentional violation |
| Ignoring Global Privacy Control signals | CPPA has stated GPC must be honored as a valid opt-out |
| Not verifying identity for consumer requests | Risk of releasing data to unauthorized persons |
| Treating "sharing" as separate from "selling" | CPRA treats both the same — retargeting pixels = sharing |
| No retention schedules disclosed | CPRA requires you to state how long you keep each data category |

### Best Practices

- **Honor GPC Signals:** Treat browser-level Global Privacy Control signals as valid opt-out requests — the CPPA expects this
- **Two-Click Opt-Out:** Don't make users jump through hoops — one click on "Do Not Sell" + one confirmation
- **Separate Consent for Sensitive Data:** Geolocation, health, race, sexual orientation, financial data require explicit opt-in or "Limit Use" option
- **Data Mapping Quarterly:** Re-map your data flows every quarter — marketing tools change rapidly and new integrations create new flows
- **Proactive Disclosure:** Go beyond the minimum — publish a clear, visual data practices page showing exactly what you collect and why

### Key Regulation Summaries

**ePrivacy Directive (EU)** — The "Cookie Law" that works alongside GDPR specifically for electronic communications. The upcoming ePrivacy Regulation will replace it and directly regulate cookies, email marketing, and metadata processing.

**UK Data Protection Act 2018 + UK GDPR** — Post-Brexit, the UK adopted its own version of GDPR. Largely mirrors EU GDPR but is enforced by the ICO. The UK is developing reforms to be "business-friendly" while maintaining adequacy with the EU.

**LGPD (Brazil)** — Modeled heavily on GDPR. Requires consent or legitimate interest. Covers all organizations processing data of Brazilian residents. Fines up to 2% of revenue (capped at R$50 million per violation).

**PIPL (China)** — The strictest regulation globally for cross-border transfers. Requires government security assessments for data transfers outside China. Massive fines (up to ¥50 million or 5% of revenue). Critical for any brand marketing to Chinese consumers.

**Real-Time Example:**
LinkedIn shut down its main platform in China in 2021, citing a "significantly more challenging operating environment" — largely driven by PIPL's requirements around data localization and government access to data.

### Strategies for Global Compliance

1. **Adopt the Highest Standard** — Build to GDPR-level compliance as your baseline, then layer jurisdiction-specific requirements
2. **Unified Consent Framework** — Use a single consent management platform that adapts consent flows by region
3. **Data Localization Planning** — Identify which regulations require data to stay in-country (China's PIPL, Russia's data localization law)
4. **Cross-Border Transfer Mechanisms** — Use Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs), adequacy decisions, or Binding Corporate Rules for international transfers
5. **Regulatory Monitoring Service** — Subscribe to regulatory update services (IAPP, OneTrust DataGuidance) to stay ahead of changes

### Best Practices

- **"Privacy by Highest Standard":** Apply GDPR-level protections globally rather than trying to comply with each law minimally
- **Regional Legal Counsel:** Engage local privacy lawyers in key markets (EU, US, China, Brazil, India)
- **Translation & Localization:** Privacy policies and consent flows must be in the local language and culturally appropriate
- **Annual Compliance Review:** Laws are changing rapidly — what was compliant in 2024 may not be in 2026

## 20.1.4 Cookie Compliance & Consent Management

### Types of Cookies

| Cookie Type | Purpose | Consent Required? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strictly Necessary** | Essential for site function | No (exempt) | Session ID, shopping cart, CSRF tokens |
| **Performance/Analytics** | Measure site usage and performance | Yes | Google Analytics, Hotjar, Mixpanel |
| **Functional** | Remember preferences and personalization | Yes (in most jurisdictions) | Language preference, saved login, chat widget |
| **Targeting/Advertising** | Track users for ad delivery and retargeting | Yes | Facebook Pixel, Google Ads conversion, LinkedIn Insight |

### Cookie Consent Requirements by Region

| Region | Model | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| **EU (GDPR + ePrivacy)** | Opt-in | Explicit consent before setting non-essential cookies. No cookie walls. |
| **UK** | Opt-in | Similar to EU. ICO enforces. Implied consent no longer acceptable. |
| **US (California)** | Opt-out | No prior consent needed, but "Do Not Sell/Share" opt-out must be available. |
| **US (Colorado)** | Opt-out + Universal | Must honor universal opt-out mechanisms (GPC). |
| **Brazil (LGPD)** | Opt-in | Consent required for non-essential cookies. |
| **Canada (PIPEDA)** | Implied to Express | Moving toward express consent; implied consent still accepted for some uses. |

### Cookie Banner Design — UX Best Practices

**What Regulators Expect:**
- Equal prominence for "Accept" and "Reject" buttons (no dark patterns)
- Granular controls — users must be able to choose categories
- No pre-ticked boxes
- Cookie wall (blocking content until consent) is prohibited in the EU
- Withdrawal of consent must be as easy as giving consent

**Compliant Banner Example Layout:**
```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  We use cookies to improve your experience.                    │
│                                                                │
│  [Necessary ✓ always on]                                       │
│  [Analytics ☐]                                                 │
│  [Marketing ☐]                                                 │
│  [Functional ☐]                                                │
│                                                                │
│  Learn more in our [Cookie Policy]                             │
│                                                                │
│  [ Accept All ]    [ Save Preferences ]    [ Reject All ]      │
│                                                                │
│  All three buttons EQUAL SIZE and EQUAL PROMINENCE             │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Dark Pattern Banner (NON-COMPLIANT):**
```
❌ WRONG — Do NOT do this:
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  We use cookies to enhance your experience.                │
│                                                            │
│  [████ ACCEPT ALL ████]                                    │
│                                                            │
│  manage preferences (tiny gray text, hard to find)         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Real-Time Example:**
France's CNIL fined Google €150 million and Facebook €60 million in 2022 because their cookie banners made "Accept" easy but required multiple clicks to "Reject." Both were forced to add equally prominent reject buttons.

### Google Consent Mode v2

Google Consent Mode v2 (mandatory from March 2024 for EEA advertisers) adjusts how Google tags behave based on user consent.

**How It Works:**

| Consent State | `analytics_storage` | `ad_storage` | `ad_user_data` | `ad_personalization` |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Full Consent** | Granted | Granted | Granted | Granted |
| **Partial Consent** | Granted | Denied | Denied | Denied |
| **No Consent** | Denied | Denied | Denied | Denied |

When consent is denied, Google uses "cookieless pings" — anonymized signals that use conversion modeling to fill data gaps with AI-estimated data.

**Implementation Steps:**
1. Choose a Google-certified CMP (OneTrust, Cookiebot, Osano, etc.)
2. Implement the CMP on your site with proper cookie categorization
3. Add the Google Consent Mode snippet before your Google tags
4. Map CMP consent categories to Google consent signals
5. Configure default consent state (typically `denied` for EU users)
6. Test with Google Tag Assistant to verify consent states fire correctly
7. Monitor consent rates and modeling quality in GA4 reports

### Server-Side Tracking and Privacy

As browsers block third-party cookies and privacy laws tighten, server-side tracking is becoming the industry standard.

**Client-Side vs. Server-Side Tracking:**

| Aspect | Client-Side | Server-Side |
|---|---|---|
| **How it works** | JavaScript tag fires in browser | Data sent to your server first, then forwarded to platforms |
| **Privacy control** | Limited — data goes directly to third parties | Full control — you decide what to send |
| **Ad blocker impact** | Blocked by ad blockers | Not affected by ad blockers |
| **Data accuracy** | Declining (ITP, cookie limits) | Higher accuracy and longer attribution |
| **Compliance** | Harder to manage | Easier to filter PII before sharing |
| **Setup complexity** | Simple | Requires server infrastructure |

**Tools for Server-Side Tracking:**

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| **Google Tag Manager Server-Side** | Google's server container for GTM — runs on Cloud Run or custom server |
| **Stape.io** | Managed server-side GTM hosting |
| **Meta Conversions API (CAPI)** | Server-side event tracking for Facebook/Instagram ads |
| **TikTok Events API** | Server-to-server event forwarding for TikTok ads |
| **Segment** | Customer data platform with server-side event routing |

### Cookie-Less Tracking Alternatives

| Alternative | How It Works | Privacy Status |
|---|---|---|
| **First-Party Data** | Data collected directly from your own properties | Privacy-friendly — you own the relationship |
| **Google Privacy Sandbox** | Topics API, Attribution Reporting, Protected Audiences | Browser-based, no individual tracking |
| **Contextual Targeting** | Ads based on page content, not user behavior | Fully privacy-compliant |
| **Data Clean Rooms** | Aggregate insights from matched datasets without sharing raw data | Privacy-preserving analytics |
| **Probabilistic/Cohort Models** | AI-estimated audience segments based on signals | Varying compliance — depends on implementation |
| **Universal IDs (UID2, ID5)** | Hashed email-based identity solutions with consent | Requires explicit consent |

### Implementation Steps for Cookie Compliance

1. **Cookie Audit** — Scan your site with Cookiebot or CookieServe to identify every cookie being set
2. **Categorize Cookies** — Classify each as necessary, analytics, functional, or marketing
3. **Select a CMP** — Choose a Google-certified CMP that supports your regions and platforms
4. **Design Compliant Banners** — Equal-prominence accept/reject, granular controls, no dark patterns
5. **Implement Consent Mode v2** — Wire CMP consent signals to Google tags
6. **Configure Tag Firing** — Ensure non-essential tags only fire after consent is granted
7. **Add Server-Side Tracking** — Set up CAPI for Meta, server-side GTM, and Events APIs for other platforms
8. **Test and Validate** — Use Google Tag Assistant, browser dev tools, and CMP testing modes
9. **Monitor Consent Rates** — Track accept/reject rates and adjust UX for better opt-in without being manipulative
10. **Regular Re-Scans** — New tags, plugins, and tools introduce new cookies — re-scan monthly

### Gaps in Cookie Compliance

| Gap | Impact | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Tags firing before consent | Illegal data collection | Configure CMP to block all non-essential tags until consent |
| No cookie scanning/audit | Unknown cookies on your site | Use Cookiebot or CookieServe to scan monthly |
| Consent banner with no "Reject" button | Regulatory fines (France fined Google/Facebook for this) | Add equally prominent Reject button |
| Google Consent Mode not implemented | Loss of EEA ad measurement data (Google requires it) | Implement with certified CMP |
| No server-side tracking fallback | Losing 30-40% of conversion data due to browser restrictions | Deploy Meta CAPI, GA4 server-side |
| Cookie policy not updated | Lists outdated or missing cookies | Re-generate policy after each cookie audit |

### Best Practices

- **Consent Rates Benchmark:** Aim for 70-85% opt-in rate through clear, honest communication (not manipulation)
- **A/B Test Banner Copy:** Test different messaging — "We respect your privacy" vs. "Customize your experience" — measure consent rates
- **Remember Consent:** Store consent state for returning visitors — don't re-prompt on every visit
- **Consent Receipts:** Log and timestamp every consent decision as proof
- **Progressive Consent:** Don't ask for everything on the first visit — request additional permissions contextually (e.g., ask for location only when the user clicks "Find stores near me")
- **Annual CMP Review:** Evaluate your CMP annually for feature updates, regulatory changes, and cost efficiency

## Key Strategies for Data Privacy Compliance

### Strategy 1: Privacy-First Marketing Stack

Build your marketing technology stack with privacy as the foundation, not an afterthought:
- Choose tools with built-in privacy features (e.g., Plausible Analytics instead of GA4 for simpler sites)
- Prefer first-party data collection over third-party data purchasing
- Invest in CDPs (Customer Data Platforms) that centralize consent management across channels

### Strategy 2: Data Minimization as Competitive Advantage

Collect less, convert more:
- Shorter forms = higher conversion rates AND better compliance
- Apple proved this with App Tracking Transparency — brands that adapted actually saw improved customer trust and lifetime value

### Strategy 3: Consent-Driven Personalization

Build personalization on consented data:
- Progressive profiling — ask for data gradually over multiple interactions
- Zero-party data — data customers intentionally share (preferences, surveys, quizzes)
- Contextual targeting — personalize based on what the user is doing now, not historical tracking

### Strategy 4: Regional Compliance Automation

Don't manually manage compliance across 50+ jurisdictions:
- Use geo-IP detection to show region-appropriate consent flows
- Automate data subject request routing to the right team
- Use compliance platforms that update policies automatically when laws change

1. **AI and Privacy:** Regulators are examining how AI models use personal data for training — expect new rules around AI-generated personalization
2. **Children's Privacy Expansion:** Age verification requirements are expanding globally (UK's Age Appropriate Design Code, proposed US KOSA)

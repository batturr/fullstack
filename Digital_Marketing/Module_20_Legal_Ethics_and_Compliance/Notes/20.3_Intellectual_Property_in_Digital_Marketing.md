# 20.3 Intellectual Property in Digital Marketing — Comprehensive Notes

## 20.3.1 Copyright Law for Marketers

### What Is Copyright

Copyright is an automatic legal right that protects original works of authorship the moment they are fixed in a tangible medium. You don't need to register, file paperwork, or even add a copyright symbol — the protection exists from the instant of creation. However, registration provides stronger enforcement rights (ability to sue for statutory damages in the US).

### What Copyright Protects in Digital Marketing

| Content Type | Protected | Not Protected |
|---|---|---|
| **Blog posts, articles, web copy** | Yes — the specific expression of ideas | The ideas or facts themselves |
| **Photographs and illustrations** | Yes — original creative works | Generic stock photo concepts |
| **Videos and animations** | Yes — audiovisual works | Short, common phrases or slogans |
| **Music and sound recordings** | Yes — both composition and recording | Individual musical notes or chords |
| **Software code** | Yes — the specific code expression | The underlying algorithm or function |
| **Email templates and designs** | Yes — original design expression | Standard layout patterns |
| **Social media posts** | Yes — original text, images, videos | Hashtags, individual facts |
| **Infographics** | Yes — the design and creative expression | The underlying data |
| **Fonts/typefaces** | Varies — font software yes, typeface design depends on jurisdiction | Basic letter forms |

### Fair Use Doctrine

Fair use allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission under certain circumstances. It is NOT a blanket right — it is an affirmative defense that depends on a four-factor balancing test:

| Factor | Analysis | Marketing Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose and Character** | Commercial use weighs against fair use; transformative use weighs for it | Using a competitor's ad in a parody may be fair use; copying their ad to sell your product is not |
| **Nature of the Work** | Creative works get stronger protection than factual works | Using a screenshot from a news article has a better fair use argument than using an artist's painting |
| **Amount Used** | Using a small portion is more likely fair use | Quoting one sentence from a 5,000-word article: likely fair use. Copying the whole article: not fair use |
| **Market Effect** | If your use substitutes for the original, it's not fair use | Reposting an entire article on your blog reduces traffic to the original — fails this factor |

**Fair Use IS NOT:**
- A right to use anything for educational purposes
- A right to use anything if you give credit
- A right to use anything if you don't make money from it
- A right to use anything just because it's on the internet

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2023, the US Supreme Court ruled in Andy Warhol Foundation v. Goldsmith that Warhol's famous silkscreen of Prince (based on photographer Lynn Goldsmith's photo) was NOT fair use when licensed commercially. The ruling narrowed the scope of "transformative use" — critical for marketers who remix or modify existing works.

### Creative Commons Licenses

Creative Commons provides standardized licenses that creators can apply to their works to grant specific permissions:

| License | Abbreviation | Permissions | Commercial Use? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Attribution** | CC BY | Use, share, adapt with credit | Yes |
| **Attribution-ShareAlike** | CC BY-SA | Use, share, adapt with credit; derivatives under same license | Yes |
| **Attribution-NoDerivs** | CC BY-ND | Use and share with credit; no modifications | Yes |
| **Attribution-NonCommercial** | CC BY-NC | Use, share, adapt with credit; non-commercial only | No |
| **Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike** | CC BY-NC-SA | Non-commercial, share alike, with credit | No |
| **Attribution-NonCommercial-NoDerivs** | CC BY-NC-ND | Most restrictive — non-commercial, no changes, with credit | No |
| **CC0 (Public Domain)** | CC0 | No rights reserved — use for anything | Yes |

**For Marketing:** Only CC BY, CC BY-SA, and CC0 licenses allow commercial use. If you use a CC BY-NC image in an ad, you are violating the license — even if you credit the creator.

### Stock Photography and Licensing

| License Type | What It Allows | Cost Model | Example Provider |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Royalty-Free (RF)** | One-time payment for unlimited use within license scope | Per-download or subscription | Shutterstock, Adobe Stock, iStock |
| **Rights-Managed (RM)** | License for specific use (size, placement, duration, geography) | Per-use pricing based on parameters | Getty Images (RM collection) |
| **Editorial Use Only** | For news, commentary, or educational context — NOT advertising | Varies | Getty, AP Images |
| **Extended License** | Expanded rights (merchandise, unlimited copies, resale) | Premium pricing | Most stock platforms offer extended options |

**Critical Distinction:** Editorial images cannot be used in ads, marketing materials, or any content that promotes a product or service. A photo of a celebrity at a public event is typically editorial — using it in your ad without a license is infringement.

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2019, a photographer sued Mashable for embedding his Instagram photo in an article. The court initially ruled embedding was acceptable, but later reversed — establishing that even embedding (displaying via Instagram's own embed feature) can constitute copyright infringement if done without permission.

### User-Generated Content (UGC) Rights

Using customer photos, videos, or reviews in marketing requires explicit permission:

**UGC Rights Framework:**

| Method | Legal Safety | Best Practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Explicit Written Permission** | Highest — contract with creator | Always use for paid campaigns, ads, and website |
| **Platform-Specific Rights Request** | Medium — varies by platform ToS | Use for social media reposts |
| **Hashtag-Based License** | Low — risky unless terms are clear | Include T&Cs page explaining that using #[brandhashtag] grants usage rights |
| **Implied Permission** | Lowest — very risky | Never rely on assumed consent |

**Real-Time Example:**
Peloton was sued in 2019 for using instructors' playlists containing copyrighted music in their on-demand classes. The lawsuit sought $150 million in damages. Peloton had not obtained synchronization licenses for the music used in recorded classes (live classes had different licenses).

### DMCA Takedown Process

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA) provides a notice-and-takedown system for copyright infringement online:

```
COPYRIGHT OWNER FINDS INFRINGING CONTENT
              │
              ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 1. Send DMCA Takedown Notice to     │
│    platform/host                    │
│    - Identify copyrighted work      │
│    - Identify infringing URL        │
│    - State good faith belief        │
│    - Signed under penalty of perjury│
└──────────────┬──────────────────────┘
               │
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 2. Platform must "expeditiously"    │
│    remove or disable access         │
│    (typically within 24-72 hours)   │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────┘
               │
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 3. Alleged infringer may file       │
│    Counter-Notice if they believe   │
│    the takedown was wrong           │
└──────────────┬──────────────────────┘
               │
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│ 4. If counter-notice filed,         │
│    copyright owner has 10-14 days   │
│    to file a lawsuit or content     │
│    is restored                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### AI-Generated Content and Copyright

This is the most rapidly evolving area of IP law in digital marketing:

| Question | Current Legal Status (2025-2026) |
|---|---|
| **Is AI-generated content copyrightable?** | US Copyright Office says no — works must have human authorship. A fully AI-generated image has no copyright protection. |
| **Who owns AI-assisted content?** | If a human provides substantial creative input and uses AI as a tool, copyright may exist in the human-authored portions. |
| **Can AI training on copyrighted works constitute infringement?** | Active litigation — The New York Times v. OpenAI, Getty Images v. Stability AI, and others are testing this question. |
| **Can I use AI-generated images in ads?** | Legally unclear — no copyright protection means anyone can copy your AI-generated ad creative. |

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2023, the US Copyright Office denied copyright registration for the comic book "Zarya of the Dawn" for its AI-generated images (made with Midjourney), while granting copyright to the human-written text and the overall arrangement. This established that AI-generated visual content currently cannot be copyrighted in the US.

### Implementation Steps

1. **Content Audit** — Catalog all images, videos, music, and content used in marketing — verify licensing for each
2. **License Tracking System** — Maintain a centralized database of all content licenses with expiration dates and usage restrictions
3. **UGC Rights Workflow** — Build a systematic process for requesting, documenting, and managing UGC permissions
4. **AI Content Policy** — Establish internal guidelines for AI-generated content use, including disclosure and risk assessment
5. **DMCA Response Plan** — Create templates and workflows for both sending and receiving DMCA notices
6. **Copyright Registration** — Register your most valuable original content (brand guides, signature creative, major campaigns)
7. **Employee Training** — Train content teams on copyright basics: no "right-click save" from Google Images
8. **Freelancer Agreements** — Ensure all freelancer/agency contracts include clear IP assignment clauses (work-for-hire or assignment)

### Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **TinEye** | Reverse image search to find unauthorized use of your images |
| **Google Reverse Image Search** | Find where your images appear online |
| **Pixsy** | Automated image copyright enforcement and licensing |
| **CopyScape** | Detect plagiarism and unauthorized use of your written content |
| **YouTube Content ID** | Automated copyright detection for video content |
| **Pex** | Music and video copyright identification across platforms |

### Gaps Commonly Found

| Gap | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Using Google Images in marketing | Copyright infringement, statutory damages up to $150,000/work | Use licensed stock photos or original content only |
| No license tracking system | Using expired or out-of-scope licenses | Build a license database with alerts for expirations |
| No UGC permission process | Using customer content without consent | Implement rights request workflow before any UGC use |
| No AI content disclosure policy | Reputational risk, unclear ownership | Create policy on when/how AI content can be used |
| Freelancers retain copyright | Company doesn't own content it paid for | Use work-for-hire clauses or IP assignment in contracts |
| Music in video without sync license | Expensive infringement claims | Use royalty-free music platforms or obtain proper licenses |

### Best Practices

- **When in Doubt, License It:** The cost of a stock photo ($10-$50) is infinitely cheaper than a copyright lawsuit ($15,000-$150,000+)
- **Document Every License:** Screenshot purchase confirmations, save license agreements, record URLs of Creative Commons sources
- **Credit Is Not a License:** Giving credit to the creator does NOT substitute for permission
- **Original First:** Your original content is your strongest IP asset — invest in original photography, video, and design
- **Review Music Carefully:** Music licensing is complex — ensure you have sync (composition) AND master (recording) rights for any music used in video content

## 20.3.2 Trademark Law for Digital Marketing

### Trademark Basics

A trademark is a word, phrase, symbol, design, or combination that identifies and distinguishes the source of goods or services. Unlike copyright (which protects expression), trademarks protect brand identity.

| Trademark Type | Example |
|---|---|
| **Word Mark** | "Nike," "Google," "Coca-Cola" |
| **Logo/Design Mark** | Nike Swoosh, Apple logo, McDonald's golden arches |
| **Sound Mark** | Netflix "ta-dum," MGM lion roar |
| **Color Mark** | Tiffany Blue (Pantone 1837), UPS Brown |
| **Trade Dress** | Distinctive product packaging or design (Coca-Cola bottle shape) |
| **Tagline** | "Just Do It," "Think Different," "I'm Lovin' It" |

### Trademark in PPC — Bidding on Competitor Brand Names

One of the most debated areas in digital marketing is whether you can bid on competitor trademarks as keywords in Google Ads.

| Activity | Legal Status (US) | Legal Status (EU) |
|---|---|---|
| **Bidding on competitor keywords** | Generally allowed | Generally allowed |
| **Using competitor name in ad headline** | Risky — may constitute infringement | Often constitutes infringement |
| **Using competitor name in display URL** | Not allowed by Google policy | Not allowed |
| **Comparative claims in ad copy** | Allowed if truthful and substantiated | Allowed if truthful |

**Real-Time Example:**
In the landmark case of 1-800 Contacts v. FTC (2021), the FTC found that 1-800 Contacts violated antitrust law by entering agreements with competitors to stop bidding on each other's trademarks. The ruling confirmed that trademark keyword bidding is a competitive practice, and agreements to NOT bid can be anti-competitive.

### Domain Name Disputes and Cybersquatting

**UDRP (Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy):**
If someone registers a domain confusingly similar to your trademark in bad faith, you can file a UDRP complaint with WIPO or another provider. To win, you must prove:
1. The domain is identical or confusingly similar to your trademark
2. The registrant has no legitimate interest in the domain
3. The domain was registered and used in bad faith

**ACPA (Anticybersquatting Consumer Protection Act):**
US law that allows trademark owners to sue cybersquatters for damages (up to $100,000 per domain).

**Real-Time Example:**
Facebook (now Meta) has recovered thousands of domains through UDRP proceedings, including variations like facebookslogin.com, facebokk.com, and faceb00k.com used for phishing.

### Brand Protection Monitoring

| Monitoring Area | What to Watch | Tool |
|---|---|---|
| **Domain Registrations** | New domains containing your brand name | DomainTools, MarkMonitor |
| **Social Media Handles** | Impersonation accounts, unauthorized use | Brandwatch, Mention |
| **Marketplace Listings** | Counterfeit products on Amazon, eBay | Brand Registry (Amazon), eBay VeRO |
| **App Stores** | Copycat apps using your brand | AppFollow, Appbot |
| **Search Ads** | Competitors using your trademark in ad copy | SpyFu, SEMrush, Adthena |
| **Content Piracy** | Unauthorized use of brand content | Red Points, BrandShield |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Trademark Registration** — Register your brand name, logo, and key taglines with the USPTO (US), EUIPO (EU), or relevant national offices
2. **International Protection** — Use the Madrid Protocol for international trademark registration in multiple countries
3. **Monitoring Setup** — Deploy brand monitoring tools to detect unauthorized use across domains, social, marketplaces, and ads
4. **Trademark Usage Guidelines** — Create a brand guide specifying how your trademarks should be used (capitalization, spacing, symbol usage)
5. **Competitor Ad Monitoring** — Regularly check if competitors are using your trademark in their ad copy
6. **Domain Portfolio Management** — Register common misspellings and TLD variations of your brand domain
7. **Enforcement Protocol** — Establish a tiered response: cease and desist letter → UDRP/platform complaint → litigation

### Best Practices

- **Use the Symbols:** ™ for unregistered marks, ® for registered marks — this puts others on notice
- **Monitor Proactively:** Don't wait for infringement to find you — active monitoring catches issues early when they're cheaper to resolve
- **Don't Over-Enforce:** Sending cease-and-desist letters to fan accounts or parody can backfire publicly — assess proportionality
- **Competitor Keywords:** You can bid on competitor keywords, but keep your ad copy focused on your own brand — avoid confusion

## 20.3.3 Music, Video & Image Licensing

### Music Licensing for Marketing

Music licensing is one of the most complex areas of IP because every piece of music has TWO separate copyrights:

| Copyright | Owner | License Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Composition** (the song itself — melody, lyrics) | Songwriter/Publisher | Synchronization (Sync) License |
| **Sound Recording** (the specific recorded version) | Record Label/Artist | Master Use License |

To use a popular song in a marketing video, you need BOTH a sync license and a master use license — and they come from different parties.

**Royalty-Free Music Platforms:**

| Platform | Pricing Model | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Epidemic Sound** | Subscription ($15-$49/mo) | YouTube, social media, podcasts |
| **Artlist** | Subscription ($10-$25/mo) | Video production, ads, social |
| **AudioJungle (Envato)** | Per-track ($1-$30+) | One-off projects |
| **Musicbed** | Per-project or subscription | High-end brand films, commercials |
| **Soundstripe** | Subscription ($11-$21/mo) | Content creators, small businesses |
| **YouTube Audio Library** | Free | YouTube content only |

### Video Licensing and Stock Footage

| Provider | Pricing | Resolution | License Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shutterstock Video** | Subscription or per-clip ($79-$199) | HD/4K | Royalty-free |
| **Adobe Stock Video** | Subscription or per-clip | HD/4K | Royalty-free |
| **Getty Images Video** | Premium pricing | HD/4K/6K | Rights-managed and RF |
| **Pond5** | Per-clip ($5-$500+) | Up to 8K | Royalty-free |
| **Pexels Video** | Free | HD/4K | CC0 (public domain) |
| **Artgrid (by Artlist)** | Subscription ($25/mo) | Up to 4K | Royalty-free |

### Model and Property Releases

| Release Type | When Required | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Model Release** | Any recognizable person in commercial content | Permission to use a person's likeness for commercial purposes |
| **Property Release** | Recognizable private property, interiors, pets | Permission to use property images commercially |
| **Not Required** | Public spaces, unrecognizable people, editorial use | News, commentary, education (not advertising) |

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2017, a Chicago man sued Shutterstock and multiple companies for $6 million because his photo (originally from a medical stock photo shoot) was used in erectile dysfunction ads across the internet without his specific consent for that use — his model release did not cover sensitive medical advertising.

### AI-Generated Asset Copyright Considerations

| Scenario | Copyright Status | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| **Fully AI-generated image for an ad** | No copyright protection — anyone can copy it | High (no legal protection for your creative) |
| **AI-assisted design with significant human input** | Potentially copyrightable for human-authored elements | Medium (unclear enforcement) |
| **AI-generated music** | No copyright protection (per current US guidance) | High |
| **AI tool trained on copyrighted works** | Active litigation — training may constitute infringement | High (legal risk from using the tool itself) |

### Licensing for Different Marketing Uses

| Use | License Level Needed | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Social media post** | Standard social media license | Posting a stock photo on Instagram |
| **Website/blog** | Standard web license | Featured image on a blog post |
| **Paid advertising** | Enhanced/extended license required | Stock image in a Facebook ad |
| **Print (brochures, billboards)** | Print license (often extended) | Image on a trade show banner |
| **Merchandise** | Extended/merchandise license | Image printed on a t-shirt for sale |
| **Broadcast (TV/OTT)** | Broadcast license or rights-managed | Image used in a TV commercial |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Licensing Audit** — Review all music, video, and image assets currently in use and verify licensing status
2. **Centralized Asset Library** — Build a DAM (Digital Asset Management) system with license metadata attached to every asset
3. **License Renewal Alerts** — Set calendar alerts for expiring licenses (especially rights-managed)
4. **Usage Matching** — Ensure the license scope matches the actual use (social post license ≠ paid ad license)
5. **Vendor Standardization** — Consolidate stock providers to reduce license management complexity
6. **Release Collection** — For original shoots, collect model and property releases before production wraps
7. **AI Content Guidelines** — Define when AI-generated content is acceptable and require human creative input for copyrightable output

### Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Bynder** | Digital Asset Management with license tracking |
| **Brandfolder** | DAM with built-in rights management |
| **Canto** | Asset library with metadata and licensing fields |
| **Epidemic Sound** | Licensed music for marketing content |
| **Getty Images** | Premium stock imagery and video with clear licensing |
| **Pixsy** | Automated image rights enforcement |

### Gaps Commonly Found

| Gap | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No centralized license tracking | Using expired or wrong-scope licenses | Implement DAM with license metadata |
| Using popular music without sync license | Massive infringement damages ($150,000/work statutory) | Use royalty-free platforms or obtain proper licenses |
| AI-generated images in ads without policy | Unprotectable creative, potential infringement claims | Create AI content policy with risk assessment |
| No model releases for original photos | Privacy/right-of-publicity lawsuits | Collect releases before every shoot |
| Standard license used for paid ads | License violation — stock providers monitor this | Verify license covers advertising use |

### Best Practices

- **License Before You Create:** Secure all music, footage, and image licenses before production begins
- **Read the Fine Print:** "Royalty-free" does NOT mean "free" — it means one payment for defined uses, not unlimited use
- **Keep License Receipts:** Store purchase confirmations and license terms with the asset in your DAM
- **Avoid "Found Online" Content:** If you can't prove you licensed it, don't use it
- **Separate Social from Ads:** Many licenses allow social media posts but NOT paid advertising — know the difference
- **AI Disclosure:** When using AI-generated assets, consider disclosing this to your audience for transparency and to manage expectations

## Key Strategies for IP Protection in Marketing

### Strategy 1: IP Asset Register

Maintain a living document of all IP assets your organization owns and uses:
- Registered trademarks (status, renewal dates, jurisdictions)
- Copyrighted works (registration status, authorship, creation dates)
- Licensed third-party content (source, license type, expiration, permitted uses)
- Domain portfolio (registered domains, renewal dates)

### Strategy 2: Proactive Enforcement

Don't wait for problems to find you:
- Monthly brand monitoring reports
- Automated alerts for new domain registrations matching your brand
- Quarterly marketplace audits for counterfeits
- Annual IP portfolio review with legal counsel

### Strategy 3: Creator Economy IP Framework

As brands work with more creators, establish clear IP terms:
- Who owns content created by influencers/freelancers for your brand?
- What usage rights do you retain after a campaign ends?
- How are derivative works (edits, remixes) handled?
- What happens to content if the creator relationship ends?

### Strategy 4: AI-Ready IP Policy

Prepare for the rapidly evolving AI landscape:
- Document which AI tools your team uses and their training data sources
- Establish guidelines for AI-assisted vs. fully AI-generated content
- Monitor litigation outcomes that will set precedent (NYT v. OpenAI, Getty v. Stability AI)
- Consider watermarking AI-generated content for internal tracking

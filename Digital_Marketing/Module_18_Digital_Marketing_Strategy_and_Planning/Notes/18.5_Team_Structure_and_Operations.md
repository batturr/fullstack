## 18.5.1 Digital Marketing Team Structure

### Team Models

#### 1. Centralized Model

All digital marketing functions sit in one team, reporting to a single head of digital marketing.

```
                    CMO / VP Marketing
                          │
                ┌─────────┴─────────┐
                │  Head of Digital  │
                │    Marketing      │
                └─────────┬─────────┘
                          │
    ┌──────────┬──────────┼──────────┬──────────┐
    │          │          │          │          │
   SEO     Content    Social    Paid Media  Analytics
   Lead     Lead       Lead      Lead        Lead
```

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Consistent strategy and messaging | Can become siloed from business units |
| Efficient resource allocation | Slow to respond to individual BU needs |
| Deep specialization | May not understand product nuances |
| Shared tools and processes | Single point of failure if leader leaves |

**Best For:** Small to mid-size companies, single-product companies, startups.

#### 2. Decentralized (Embedded) Model

Digital marketers are embedded in each business unit or product line.

```
        CEO
         │
    ┌────┼────┬────────────┐
    │         │            │
  BU-1      BU-2         BU-3
    │         │            │
 Marketing  Marketing   Marketing
 (own SEO,  (own SEO,   (own SEO,
  content,   content,    content,
  paid)      paid)       paid)
```

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Deep product/market understanding | Inconsistent brand and messaging |
| Fast response to BU needs | Duplicated tools and processes |
| Strong BU alignment | No cross-pollination of learnings |
| Accountability to BU P&L | Higher total cost (duplicate roles) |

**Best For:** Large enterprises with diverse product lines (e.g., P&G, Unilever).

#### 3. Hybrid Model (Hub-and-Spoke)

Central team sets strategy, standards, and platforms. Embedded specialists execute within BUs.

```
                    CMO
                     │
              ┌──────┴──────┐
              │  Central    │
              │  Marketing  │
              │  (Strategy, │
              │  Analytics, │
              │  Brand,     │
              │  MarTech)   │
              └──────┬──────┘
                     │
         ┌───────────┼───────────┐
         │           │           │
    BU Marketer  BU Marketer  BU Marketer
    (executes    (executes    (executes
     with BU      with BU      with BU
     context)     context)     context)
```

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Strategic consistency + BU agility | Complex reporting lines |
| Shared best practices | Potential for conflict between central and BU |
| Efficient use of expensive tools | Requires strong coordination |
| Career growth paths | Can be slow to set up |

**Best For:** Growth-stage companies, mid-market, companies with 3+ product lines.

**Real-Time Example — Google's Marketing Structure:**

Google uses a hybrid model:
- **Central:** Brand marketing, creative, analytics platform (Marketing Platform team)
- **Embedded:** Each product (Search, Cloud, Workspace, YouTube, Pixel) has dedicated marketers
- **Shared services:** Agency management, media buying, marketing ops are centralized
- This lets Google Cloud market to enterprises differently than YouTube markets to creators — while maintaining Google brand coherence

#### 4. Center of Excellence (CoE)

A specialized team that builds capabilities, tools, templates, and training — then other teams execute.

```
                    CMO
                     │
              ┌──────┴──────┐
              │  Marketing  │
              │    CoE      │
              │ (Builds     │
              │  playbooks, │
              │  trains,    │
              │  sets       │
              │  standards) │
              └──────┬──────┘
                     │
         ┌───────────┼───────────┐
         │           │           │
   Team A (uses   Team B (uses  Team C (uses
   CoE playbooks) CoE tools)    CoE training)
```

**Best For:** Large organizations where marketing is done by many teams (product marketing, field marketing, partner marketing) and consistency is critical.

### Generalists vs. Specialists

| Factor | Generalist | Specialist |
|---|---|---|
| **Breadth** | Knows 6+ channels at a working level | Deep expertise in 1–2 channels |
| **When to Hire** | Early stage (team of 1–5) | Growth stage (team of 5+) |
| **Analogy** | General Practitioner (doctor) | Cardiologist (specialist) |
| **Cost** | Lower (one person covers multiple areas) | Higher (dedicated resource for one area) |
| **Speed** | Can move fast across areas | Moves fast within their domain |
| **Risk** | Jack of all trades, master of none | Narrow focus may miss cross-channel opportunities |
| **The T-Shaped Ideal** | Broad knowledge + 1 deep skill | Deep expertise + basic cross-channel awareness |

**The T-Shaped Marketer (Ideal Hire):**

```
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │    BROAD KNOWLEDGE (horizontal bar)          │
  │    SEO, Social, Email, Ads, Content,         │
  │    Analytics, CRO (working knowledge)        │
  └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                         │
                         │ DEEP EXPERTISE
                         │ (vertical bar)
                         │
                         │ e.g., Performance
                         │ Marketing
                         │ (expert level)
                         │
                         │
                         ▼
```

| Stage | Revenue | Team Size | Typical Roles |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-seed / Seed** | <₹1Cr | 1 (founder + freelancers) | 1 generalist marketer + freelance designer + freelance writer |
| **Series A** | ₹1–10Cr | 3–5 | Head of Marketing, Content, Performance, Designer |
| **Series B** | ₹10–50Cr | 6–12 | + SEO, Social, Email, Analytics, Marketing Ops |

**Real-Time Example — Razorpay's Marketing Team Evolution:**

| Stage | Year | Team Size | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Early** | 2014–2016 | 2 people | Founder-led, content + community |
| **Growth** | 2017–2019 | 8 people | + SEO, events, performance, brand |
| **Scale** | 2020–2022 | 25 people | + Product marketing, partner marketing, international |
| **Enterprise** | 2023+ | 50+ people | Full org with specialists, CoE, analytics, multiple products |

### Fractional and Contract Team Members

| Resource Type | When to Use | Cost | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Full-time hire** | Core competency, ongoing need | ₹5–40L/year + benefits | Permanent |
| **Freelancer** | Specific project, specialized skill | ₹2K–50K/deliverable | Project-based |
| **Fractional CMO** | Strategy leadership without full-time cost | ₹1–3L/month | 3–12 months |
| **Consultant** | Strategy, audit, or transformation | ₹50K–5L/engagement | 1–6 months |
| **Agency** | Execution at scale, specialized channel | ₹1–10L/month | Retainer (6–12 months) |
| **Contract employee** | Extended project, capacity boost | ₹30K–2L/month | 3–12 months |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Assess current state:** List all marketing activities and who does them (including informally)
2. **Choose a team model** based on company size, product complexity, and growth stage
3. **Define roles needed** using the role table — prioritize roles that address your biggest gaps
4. **Hire your first generalist** if you're early stage — they'll build the foundation
5. **Add specialists** as channels prove ROI and require deeper expertise
6. **Build the T-shape** — every team member should have broad awareness + one deep skill
7. **Review structure annually** — team structure should evolve with company stage

### Gap Finding

- Most startups hire specialists too early (hiring a "Head of SEO" when they need a generalist who can also do SEO)
- Marketing ops / MarTech roles are chronically underinvested — teams have tools but no one managing them
- Analytics roles are filled last, not first — but you can't optimize what you can't measure
- Org structure is copied from other companies without considering their unique context
- Career paths for marketers are unclear — leading to high turnover

### Best Practices

- First hire should be a T-shaped generalist, not a specialist
- Hire analytics capabilities early — data-informed decisions multiply the impact of every other hire
- Use fractional / freelance for specialized or intermittent needs — don't hire full-time for a part-time need
- Document every role with clear KPIs — ambiguous roles lead to ambiguous performance
- Review team structure every 6 months — what worked at 5 people won't work at 20

## 18.5.2 Agency & Vendor Management

### When to Hire an Agency vs. Build In-House

| Factor | Go Agency | Go In-House |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Need results in <3 months (no time to hire/train) | Can invest 3–6 months in hiring and ramping |
| **Expertise** | Need specialized skill you don't have internally | Core competency you'll need permanently |
| **Scale** | Seasonal or variable workload | Consistent, ongoing workload |
| **Budget** | Have budget but not headcount approval | Have headcount approval |
| **Strategy** | Need fresh outside perspective | Need deep internal context and continuity |
| **Control** | Comfortable with oversight, not execution | Need direct control over day-to-day execution |

**The Build-In-House Progression:**

```
STAGE 1: All Agency         (Year 0-1)
STAGE 2: Strategy In-House + Agency Execution  (Year 1-2)
STAGE 3: Core In-House + Agency Specialties    (Year 2-3)
STAGE 4: Mostly In-House + Agency Overflow     (Year 3+)
```

### Agency Types

| Type | What They Do | When to Use | Example Agencies |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Full-Service** | Everything: strategy, creative, media, analytics | Need a single partner for everything | WPP, Publicis, Dentsu, Ogilvy |
| **Specialist (Digital)** | Digital-only: SEO, PPC, social, content | Need digital expertise specifically | iProspect, Merkle, WebFX |
| **Performance** | ROI-focused: paid acquisition, CRO | Need measurable, accountable results | Tinuiti, Rise Interactive |
| **Creative** | Brand, campaigns, storytelling, design | Need breakthrough creative work | Droga5, Wieden+Kennedy |
| **PR / Communications** | Media relations, reputation, influencer | Need media coverage and credibility | Edelman, Weber Shandwick |
| **Content** | Content creation, SEO content, video | Need consistent content production | Contently, Skyword |
| **MarTech / Consulting** | Tool implementation, strategy consulting | Need tech stack optimization | Accenture Song, Deloitte Digital |

### Agency Selection Process

**Step 1: RFP (Request for Proposal)**

| RFP Section | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **Company Overview** | Your business, products, market, current state |
| **Scope of Work** | What you need the agency to do (be specific) |
| **Objectives** | What success looks like (measurable) |
| **Budget Range** | Give a range — agencies need to know if it's ₹5L or ₹50L/month |
| **Timeline** | When you need to start and key milestones |
| **Team Expectations** | Dedicated team? Who will work on your account? |
| **Questions** | Ask about their experience, approach, case studies |
| **Evaluation Criteria** | Tell them how you'll evaluate (transparency builds better proposals) |

**Step 2: Pitch Evaluation**

| Criteria | Weight | Score (1-10) |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic thinking and approach | 25% | |
| Relevant industry experience | 20% | |
| Team quality and chemistry | 20% | |
| Case studies and proven results | 15% | |
| Pricing and value | 10% | |
| Cultural fit | 10% | |

**Step 3: Reference Checks**

Ask references:
- "What was the agency's biggest strength?"
- "What was their biggest weakness?"
- "Would you hire them again? Why/why not?"
- "How did they handle a crisis or disagreement?"
- "Did they meet or exceed the KPIs agreed upon?"

### Agency Compensation Models

| Model | Description | Best For | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Retainer** | Fixed monthly fee for agreed scope | Ongoing, predictable work | Overpaying if workload is light |
| **Project-Based** | Fixed fee per project | Defined projects with clear scope | Scope creep increases cost |
| **Hourly** | Pay for hours worked | Variable or unpredictable workload | Can get expensive; incentivizes hours, not outcomes |
| **Performance** | Fee tied to results (CPL, CPA, ROAS) | Performance marketing, lead gen | Agency may over-optimize for the metric at the expense of quality |
| **Hybrid** | Base retainer + performance bonus | Balanced — stability + incentive alignment | Complex to structure and administer |

**Real-Time Example — Performance-Based Agency Model:**

A D2C brand hires a performance agency:
- **Base retainer:** ₹2L/month (covers team, tools, reporting)
- **Performance bonus:** 10% of revenue above ₹50L/month target
- **Cap:** Maximum bonus ₹3L/month
- **Review:** Quarterly with option to adjust targets

Month 1: Revenue = ₹40L → Agency earns ₹2L (retainer only)
Month 2: Revenue = ₹65L → Agency earns ₹2L + ₹1.5L (10% of ₹15L above target) = ₹3.5L
Month 3: Revenue = ₹90L → Agency earns ₹2L + ₹3L (capped) = ₹5L

This aligns incentives — the agency earns more when you earn more.

### Managing Agency Relationships

| Practice | Frequency | Who Attends |
|---|---|---|
| **Weekly status call** | Weekly | Account manager + your lead |
| **Monthly performance review** | Monthly | Agency team + your marketing team |
| **Quarterly business review (QBR)** | Quarterly | Agency leadership + your leadership |
| **Annual strategy session** | Annually | Senior leadership from both sides |

**Red Flags to Watch For:**
- Agency proposes only tactics they're good at (instead of what you need)
- Junior staff doing the work, senior staff only in meetings
- Lack of proactive recommendations — they only do what you ask
- Poor responsiveness (>24 hours for emails, missed deadlines)
- Hiding behind vanity metrics instead of business outcomes
- High team turnover on your account

### Implementation Steps

1. **Define what you need** before talking to agencies — be specific about scope, objectives, and budget
2. **Create a shortlist of 3–5 agencies** through referrals, case studies, and industry reputation
3. **Issue a focused RFP** — not a 50-page document, but a clear 5–10 page brief
4. **Evaluate chemistry and capability equally** — the best strategy means nothing if you can't work together
5. **Start with a pilot project** before committing to a long-term retainer
6. **Set clear KPIs and review cadence** from day one
7. **Invest in onboarding** — the first 30 days determine the next 12 months

### Gap Finding

- Companies hire agencies before defining internal strategy — agencies execute, they shouldn't set your strategy
- RFPs focus too much on price and not enough on strategic thinking
- Agency onboarding is rushed — teams dump a Google Drive link and expect the agency to figure it out
- Performance reviews happen too infrequently — by the time you review, 3 months of budget may be wasted
- No exit strategy — contracts don't define how to transition work if the relationship ends

### Best Practices

- Hire the agency for their thinking, not just their doing — you can outsource execution, not strategy
- Always check 3+ references and ask specifically about the team that will work on YOUR account
- Start with a 3-month pilot before signing a 12-month contract
- Define "what good looks like" in week 1 — if you can't define success, the agency can't deliver it
- Treat agencies as partners, not vendors — the best results come from collaborative relationships

## 18.5.3 Marketing Operations (MOps)

### What Is Marketing Operations?

MOps is the backbone of modern marketing — the people, processes, and technology that enable marketing to execute efficiently, measure accurately, and scale sustainably. If marketing is the engine, MOps is the pit crew.

**MOps covers:**

```
┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              MARKETING OPERATIONS                  │
├───────────────┬───────────────┬───────────────────┤
│  TECHNOLOGY   │   PROCESS     │   DATA            │
│               │               │                   │
│ MarTech stack │ Campaign ops  │ Data management   │
│ Integration   │ Workflows     │ Data governance   │
│ Automation    │ Quality       │ Attribution       │
│ Tool admin    │ assurance     │ Reporting         │
│               │ Compliance    │ Analytics         │
│               │ Brand         │ Hygiene           │
│               │ governance    │                   │
└───────────────┴───────────────┴───────────────────┘
```

### Process Documentation and Standardization

**Every marketing team needs documented processes for:**

| Process | Why It Matters | Document Format |
|---|---|---|
| Campaign launch workflow | Ensures nothing is missed, QA is consistent | Checklist + Flowchart |
| Content publishing workflow | Consistent quality, proper approvals | Step-by-step SOP |
| Lead handoff (marketing → sales) | Prevents lead leakage, ensures follow-up | SLA document |
| Vendor/agency onboarding | Faster ramp-up, consistent quality | Onboarding guide |
| Data request process | Prevents ad hoc requests from consuming analyst time | Request form + SLA |
| Brand asset usage | Consistent brand representation | Brand guidelines + asset library |
| Reporting cadence | Everyone knows what, when, and how | Reporting calendar |

### Data Management and Governance

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Data quality** | Standardize naming conventions (UTM parameters, campaign names, audience segments) |
| **Data hygiene** | Regular cleaning — remove duplicates, fix formatting, update outdated records |
| **Data access** | Role-based access to analytics, CRM, and marketing platforms |
| **Data privacy** | GDPR/DPDPA compliance, consent management, opt-out mechanisms |
| **Data integration** | Single source of truth across CRM, analytics, email, ads |
| **Data documentation** | Data dictionary defining every field, metric, and calculation |

**UTM Parameter Naming Convention (Critical):**

| Parameter | Convention | Example |
|---|---|---|
| utm_source | Platform/site name (lowercase) | google, facebook, linkedin, newsletter |
| utm_medium | Marketing medium (lowercase) | cpc, email, social, organic, referral |
| utm_campaign | Campaign identifier (snake_case) | q1_product_launch, diwali_sale_2025 |
| utm_content | Content variation (snake_case) | hero_video, cta_red, testimonial_banner |
| utm_term | Keyword (if applicable) | blue_running_shoes |

**Real-Time Example — How HubSpot Manages Marketing Data:**

HubSpot's MOps team:
- Maintains a 200+ page data dictionary defining every property in their CRM
- Uses automated workflows to clean data weekly (merge duplicates, standardize fields)
- Enforces UTM naming convention through a custom UTM builder tool (no free-form UTMs allowed)
- Has a "data steward" for each marketing function (content, demand gen, product marketing)
- Conducts quarterly data audits comparing CRM data to financial actuals

### Campaign Operations and Quality Assurance

**The QA Checklist (Before Any Campaign Goes Live):**

| Category | Check | Pass/Fail |
|---|---|---|
| **Links** | All links work and have correct UTMs | ☐ |
| **Tracking** | Conversion tracking fires correctly | ☐ |
| **Creative** | Correct creative assets in correct placements | ☐ |
| **Copy** | No typos, correct brand voice, disclaimers present | ☐ |
| **Targeting** | Correct audience segments, exclusions applied | ☐ |
| **Budget** | Daily/lifetime budget set correctly | ☐ |
| **Landing Page** | Page loads <3 seconds, mobile responsive, CTA works | ☐ |
| **Email** | Subject line, preheader, sender name, unsubscribe link | ☐ |
| **Legal** | Disclaimers, terms, privacy compliance | ☐ |
| **Scheduling** | Correct date, time, timezone | ☐ |

### Marketing Operations Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures | Target |
|---|---|---|
| **Campaign velocity** | Time from brief to launch | Decreasing over time |
| **Error rate** | % of campaigns with errors (broken links, wrong targeting) | <2% |
| **Tool utilization** | % of MarTech features actually used | >60% |
| **Data quality score** | % of records that are complete and accurate | >90% |
| **Cost per campaign** | Total operational cost to run a campaign | Decreasing with scale |
| **Compliance rate** | % of campaigns that pass legal/brand review | 100% target |
| **SLA adherence** | % of lead handoffs, reports, requests delivered on time | >95% |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Audit current processes** — document what exists (even if informal) and identify gaps
2. **Standardize naming conventions** — UTMs, campaign names, audience segments
3. **Build QA checklists** for every campaign type — make QA non-negotiable
4. **Create a data dictionary** — define every metric, field, and calculation
5. **Implement data governance** — assign data stewards, schedule regular audits
6. **Document SOPs** for top 10 recurring processes — start with the highest-volume ones
7. **Measure MOps metrics** — velocity, error rate, utilization — to prove operational improvement

### Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **HubSpot / Marketo / Pardot** | Marketing automation and operations |
| **Workato / Zapier / Make** | Integration and workflow automation |
| **Confluence / Notion** | Process documentation |
| **Spreadsheet (UTM builder)** | UTM standardization |
| **Asana / Monday.com** | Campaign operations workflow |
| **Segment / mParticle** | Customer data platform (data integration) |
| **OneTrust / Cookiebot** | Privacy compliance and consent management |

### Gap Finding

- MOps is treated as "admin work" instead of a strategic function
- UTM naming is inconsistent — making cross-channel attribution unreliable
- QA is done informally ("just check the links") instead of systematically
- Data governance is nonexistent — duplicate records, inconsistent fields, no data dictionary
- MarTech tool utilization is below 30% in most organizations — paying for features nobody uses
- Process documentation exists in someone's head, not in a shared system

### Best Practices

- Treat MOps as a strategic function, not administrative — the MOps team enables everything else
- Standardize before you automate — automating a broken process makes it break faster
- QA checklists are non-negotiable — one broken link can waste an entire campaign budget
- Invest in a data dictionary early — it prevents a data quality crisis later
- Conduct quarterly MarTech audits — cancel tools that aren't being used

## 18.5.4 MarTech Stack Strategy

### What Is a MarTech Stack?

A MarTech (Marketing Technology) stack is the collection of software tools a marketing team uses to execute, manage, measure, and optimize marketing activities. As of 2024, the MarTech landscape has grown to 14,000+ tools.

### Core vs. Supplementary Tools

**Core Stack (Every team needs these):**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              CORE MARTECH STACK                   │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  1. CRM (Customer Relationship Management)       │
│     HubSpot, Salesforce, Zoho CRM                │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  2. Analytics (Web + Product)                    │
│     GA4, Amplitude, Mixpanel                     │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  3. Email + Marketing Automation                 │
│     HubSpot, Mailchimp, Braze, Klaviyo           │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  4. CMS (Content Management System)              │
│     WordPress, Webflow, Contentful                │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  5. Ad Platforms                                 │
│     Google Ads, Meta Ads, LinkedIn Ads            │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  6. Social Media Management                      │
│     Sprout Social, Buffer, Hootsuite              │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  7. SEO                                          │
│     SEMrush, Ahrefs, Moz                         │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Supplementary (Add based on need):**

| Category | Tools | When to Add |
|---|---|---|
| A/B Testing / CRO | Optimizely, VWO | When traffic >50K/month |
| Customer Data Platform | Segment, mParticle | When data silos become a problem |
| Chat / Conversational | Intercom, Drift | When website has >5K/month visits |
| Influencer Management | AspireIQ, CreatorIQ | When running regular influencer campaigns |
| Project Management | Asana, Monday.com | When team >5 people |
| Design | Canva, Figma | From day one |
| Video | Loom, Wistia | When video is a key content format |
| Attribution | Rockerbox, Triple Whale | When multi-channel ad spend >₹10L/month |
| Survey / Feedback | Typeform, Hotjar | When running regular customer research |

### Stack Audit and Rationalization

**The MarTech Audit Framework:**

| Question | What You're Assessing |
|---|---|
| What tools do we have? | Inventory — list every tool with owner, cost, and users |
| Who uses each tool? | Utilization — are people actually using it? |
| What does each tool cost? | Financial — total cost of ownership |
| Does it overlap with another tool? | Redundancy — are we paying for the same capability twice? |
| Is it integrated with other tools? | Integration — does data flow between tools? |
| Does it still serve our strategy? | Relevance — do we still need this capability? |

**Real-Time Example — A MarTech Audit Finding:**

A mid-size e-commerce company audited their stack and found:
- 23 active tools across marketing
- 4 tools had overlapping functionality (2 email platforms, 2 social schedulers)
- 3 tools had <10% utilization (purchased but barely used)
- Monthly MarTech spend: ₹4.5L/month
- After rationalization: 14 tools, ₹2.8L/month — 38% cost reduction with no capability loss

### Integration Strategy and Data Flow

```
                    ┌───────────┐
                    │    CRM    │
                    │ (HubSpot) │
                    └─────┬─────┘
                          │
          ┌───────────────┼───────────────┐
          │               │               │
   ┌──────▼──────┐ ┌──────▼──────┐ ┌──────▼──────┐
   │  Analytics  │ │    Email    │ │  Ad Platforms│
   │   (GA4)     │ │  (Klaviyo)  │ │  (Google/    │
   │             │ │             │ │   Meta)      │
   └──────┬──────┘ └──────┬──────┘ └──────┬──────┘
          │               │               │
          └───────────────┼───────────────┘
                          │
                   ┌──────▼──────┐
                   │    CDP      │
                   │  (Segment)  │
                   │ Single View │
                   │ of Customer │
                   └─────────────┘
```

**Integration Principles:**
1. **Single source of truth:** One system is authoritative for each data type (CRM for contacts, analytics for traffic)
2. **Unidirectional data flow preferred:** Avoid circular data flows that create conflicts
3. **Real-time where it matters:** Lead alerts need real-time sync; reporting can batch nightly
4. **Native integrations > custom:** Use built-in integrations before building custom APIs
5. **Test integrations regularly:** Data sync breaks silently — monitor it

### Build vs. Buy Decisions

| Factor | Build (Custom) | Buy (Off-the-shelf) |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | High upfront, low ongoing (if maintained) | Low upfront (SaaS), ongoing subscription |
| **Time to Value** | Months to years | Days to weeks |
| **Customization** | Unlimited | Limited to vendor's feature set |
| **Maintenance** | Your responsibility (dev team needed) | Vendor's responsibility |
| **Risk** | High (tech debt, key-person dependency) | Low (vendor manages updates) |
| **Best For** | Unique competitive advantage, core IP | Commodity capabilities, best practices |

**Decision Rule:** Build only when the capability is a core differentiator. Buy everything else.

**Real-Time Example:**
- Shopify BUILT their own analytics because data is core to their product offering
- Shopify BOUGHT Mailchimp integration (and later built their own email) because email was supplementary to their core
- Netflix BUILT their own recommendation engine (core differentiator) but USES AWS for infrastructure (commodity)

### MarTech Vendor Evaluation Framework

| Criteria | Weight | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|---|
| **Functionality** | 25% | Does it do what we need? Can we see a demo with our use case? |
| **Ease of Use** | 20% | Can our team learn it in <2 weeks? Is the UX intuitive? |
| **Integration** | 20% | Does it integrate with our existing stack? APIs available? |
| **Pricing** | 15% | Total cost (license + implementation + training)? Scaling costs? |
| **Support** | 10% | SLA for support? Dedicated account manager? Documentation quality? |
| **Security** | 5% | SOC2? GDPR compliance? Data residency? |
| **Vendor Viability** | 5% | Funded? Profitable? Growing? Risk of shutdown? |

### Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) Analysis

**TCO goes beyond subscription price:**

| Cost Category | Example |
|---|---|
| **License / Subscription** | ₹50K/month for HubSpot Professional |
| **Implementation** | ₹3L one-time setup (agency or consultant) |
| **Training** | ₹50K for team training (or 20 hours of lost productivity) |
| **Integration** | ₹1L for custom integrations with existing tools |
| **Maintenance** | ₹20K/month for admin, updates, troubleshooting |
| **Migration (future)** | ₹5L estimated cost to migrate data if you switch |
| **Opportunity Cost** | What you can't do because this tool doesn't support it |
| **3-Year TCO** | License: ₹18L + Implementation: ₹3L + Training: ₹50K + Integration: ₹1L + Maintenance: ₹7.2L = **₹29.7L** |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Inventory all current tools** — list every tool with cost, users, and purpose
2. **Map data flows** — how does data move between tools? Where are gaps?
3. **Audit utilization** — which tools are actually used? Which are "shelfware"?
4. **Identify overlaps and gaps** — consolidate redundant tools, fill capability gaps
5. **Define integration requirements** — which tools MUST talk to each other?
6. **Evaluate vendors** using the weighted scoring framework
7. **Calculate TCO** for new tool decisions — not just subscription price
8. **Build a 2-year MarTech roadmap** — planned additions, consolidations, and migrations

### Gap Finding

- Most companies don't know how many tools they have — tools are purchased by individuals without central oversight
- Integration is an afterthought — tools are bought for features, not for how they connect to the stack
- TCO is rarely calculated — teams see the monthly subscription but miss implementation, training, and maintenance costs
- Tool utilization is shockingly low — industry data shows average utilization of MarTech stack features is 33%
- No one owns the MarTech stack — it grows organically without governance
- Build vs. buy decisions are emotional ("our CTO wants to build it") rather than strategic

### Best Practices

- Assign a MarTech owner — one person/role responsible for stack governance
- Conduct a stack audit twice per year — tools that aren't used should be cancelled
- Integration capability is a top-3 evaluation criterion — a great tool that doesn't integrate is a silo
- Calculate 3-year TCO, not annual price — make decisions on full cost
- Start simple, add complexity only when needed — every tool adds cognitive and operational overhead
- Prefer platforms over point solutions when possible — fewer vendors = fewer integration headaches

## Key Takeaways

1. **Team structure should match company stage** — don't build an enterprise team for a startup

## 15.4.1 Crisis Preparedness

### Types of Digital Crises

| Crisis Type | Description | Real-Time Example | Severity | Speed |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Social Media Backlash | Viral negative reaction to a post, campaign, or action | Pepsi's Kendall Jenner ad (2017) — trivializing protests | High | Hours |
| Data Breach | Customer data compromised, hacked, or leaked | Equifax breach (2017) — 147 million records exposed | Critical | Days–Weeks |
| Product Failure | Product causes harm, recalls, or widespread complaints | Samsung Galaxy Note 7 battery explosions (2016) | Critical | Days |
| Executive Misconduct | CEO or leadership behavior becomes public scandal | WeWork's Adam Neumann scandal (2019) | High | Days–Weeks |
| Misinformation | False information spreads about your brand | 5G conspiracy theories targeting telecom companies (2020) | Medium–High | Hours–Days |
| Customer Service Failure | Viral customer complaint goes mainstream | United Airlines dragging passenger off flight (2017) | High | Hours |
| Employee Controversy | Employee behavior reflects poorly on the brand | Amy's Baking Company social media meltdown (2013) | Medium | Hours |
| Legal / Regulatory | Lawsuits, regulatory action, compliance failures | Facebook/Cambridge Analytica (2018) | Critical | Weeks–Months |
| Environmental / Safety | Environmental damage or safety incidents | BP Deepwater Horizon oil spill (2010) | Critical | Months |

### Crisis Communication Plan Development

**A crisis communication plan should be created BEFORE a crisis occurs. Once a crisis hits, it's too late to plan.**

**Plan Components:**

| Component | Details |
|---|---|
| Crisis Definition | What constitutes a crisis vs. a complaint vs. a trend |
| Crisis Team | Names, roles, contact information, decision authority |
| Severity Framework | How to assess Level 1 (minor) through Level 5 (existential) |
| Communication Channels | Which channels to use, in what order, for what audiences |
| Messaging Templates | Pre-approved holding statements for common scenarios |
| Approval Workflow | Who approves what, maximum time from draft to publication |
| Stakeholder Map | Employees, customers, investors, regulators, media, public |
| Dark Site | Pre-built crisis landing page ready to activate |
| Monitoring Triggers | Keyword thresholds, sentiment drops, mention velocity spikes |
| Contact Lists | Media contacts, legal counsel, PR agency, executive team |

### Crisis Severity Framework

| Level | Name | Characteristics | Response Time | Decision Authority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Monitor | Isolated negative mention, no spread | 24 hours | Social media team |
| 2 | Respond | Multiple complaints, growing conversation | 4-8 hours | PR manager |
| 3 | Manage | Media picking up the story, trending locally | 1-2 hours | VP Communications |
| 4 | Escalate | National/international coverage, viral social spread | 30-60 minutes | C-Suite + Legal |
| 5 | All Hands | Existential threat, stock impact, regulatory action | Immediate | CEO + Board |

### Real-Time Example: Johnson & Johnson's Tylenol Crisis (1982) — The Gold Standard

**What happened:**
- Seven people in Chicago died after taking cyanide-laced Tylenol capsules
- J&J's Tylenol had 37% of the painkiller market

**What J&J did RIGHT:**
1. Immediately recalled 31 million bottles (cost: $100M+) before being told to by regulators
2. CEO James Burke personally appeared on TV to address the public
3. Offered free product exchanges
4. Cooperated transparently with FBI and FDA
5. Invented the tamper-proof seal before relaunching
6. Communicated daily with the public via press conferences

**Result:**
- Tylenol regained its #1 market position within 1 year
- J&J's reputation was not just preserved — it was strengthened
- Became THE case study for crisis communication excellence
- 40+ years later, still referenced as the standard

### Crisis Team Roles and Responsibilities

| Role | Responsibility | Decisions They Make |
|---|---|---|
| Crisis Leader (usually CMO or CEO) | Overall strategy and final decision authority | Go/no-go on public statements, escalation level |
| Communications Lead | Draft statements, manage media, coordinate messaging | Message framing, channel selection, timing |
| Social Media Manager | Monitor social channels, post approved responses | Social response tone, engagement/silence decisions |
| Legal Counsel | Review statements for legal risk, advise on liability | What can/cannot be said, regulatory compliance |
| Customer Service Lead | Handle inbound customer inquiries during crisis | Escalation of patterns, frontline messaging |
| HR Lead | Internal communication to employees | Staff talking points, morale management |
| Subject Matter Expert | Provide technical/factual context | Technical accuracy of public statements |
| External PR Agency | Provide objective counsel, media relationships | Strategy guidance, journalist management |

### Pre-Approved Messaging Templates

**Holding Statement (use within first 30 minutes):**

```
"We are aware of [description of the situation] and are actively 
investigating. The safety/satisfaction of our [customers/users/community] 
is our top priority. We will provide an update within [timeframe]. 
For immediate concerns, please contact [contact information]."
```

**Data Breach Template:**

```
"We have identified a security incident affecting [description]. 
We immediately [actions taken: engaged cybersecurity experts, 
notified authorities, secured systems]. We are working to determine 
the scope and will notify all affected individuals directly. 
We have established a dedicated resource at [URL/phone number]."
```

**Product Issue Template:**

```
"We have identified an issue with [product] affecting [scope]. 
Customer safety is our absolute priority. We are [immediate action: 
voluntary recall, temporary pause, free replacement]. 
Affected customers should [specific instructions]. 
We sincerely apologize for the inconvenience."
```

### Dark Site and Holding Statements

**What is a Dark Site:**
A pre-built, hidden web page that can be activated immediately during a crisis. It includes:
- A clean, simple design with no distracting elements
- Space for official statements (pre-templated, fill in specifics)
- FAQ section (pre-drafted for common scenarios)
- Contact information (customer service, media, affected parties)
- Timeline of events (updated as the crisis unfolds)
- Resources and next steps for affected stakeholders

**Why you need it:**
- Your main website may crash during a crisis (traffic spike)
- Provides a single source of truth
- Journalists will link to it, creating a controlled narrative
- Can be activated in minutes vs. building a page from scratch

### Crisis Simulation and Tabletop Exercises

**Frequency:** Run at least 2 tabletop exercises per year

**How to Run a Tabletop Exercise:**

| Step | Duration | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Set the scenario | 10 min | Present a realistic crisis scenario (e.g., "A customer's personal data was exposed in a breach; a journalist calls for comment") |
| 2. Initial response | 30 min | Team drafts a holding statement and identifies immediate actions |
| 3. Escalation | 20 min | Introduce a complication (e.g., "The story has gone viral on Reddit; a competing journalist has obtained internal emails") |
| 4. Media simulation | 30 min | Simulate journalist questions; test spokesperson responses |
| 5. Debrief | 30 min | What worked? What didn't? What needs to be updated in the plan? |

## 15.4.2 Crisis Response

### Assessment: Severity, Reach, Stakeholders

**The First 15 Minutes Checklist:**

1. What exactly happened? (Facts only, no assumptions)
2. How did we find out? (Internal report, media inquiry, social media?)
3. Who is affected? (Customers, employees, partners, investors?)
4. What is the current reach? (Local, national, international? Online, offline?)
5. Is it still happening or is it contained?
6. What is our legal exposure?
7. Who needs to be notified internally — immediately?

### Real-Time Example: Zoom's Security Crisis (2020)

**What happened:**
- As COVID-19 drove millions to Zoom, security researchers discovered multiple vulnerabilities
- "Zoombombing" became a mainstream term — strangers interrupting meetings with offensive content
- Schools and governments started banning Zoom
- Stock dropped, media coverage was overwhelmingly negative

**Zoom's Response (A+ execution):**

**Week 1 (Assessment + Holding):**
- CEO Eric Yuan acknowledged the issues publicly within 24 hours
- Published a transparent blog post detailing every known vulnerability
- Announced a 90-day feature freeze to focus exclusively on security

**Week 2-4 (Action):**
- Hired a CISO and established a security advisory board
- Implemented default passwords for all meetings
- Added waiting rooms and enhanced host controls
- Launched a bug bounty program

**Month 2-3 (Recovery + Communication):**
- Weekly "Ask Eric Anything" webinars — CEO personally answering questions
- Published a transparency report
- Achieved SOC 2 compliance
- Launched end-to-end encryption

**Result:**
- Zoom's daily meeting participants went from 10M to 300M during the crisis period
- Stock recovered and grew 5x by end of 2020
- Trust was rebuilt because leadership was transparent, fast, and took extreme action

### The Golden Hour of Crisis Communication

```
MINUTE 0-15:   Detect and assess
MINUTE 15-30:  Notify crisis team, begin drafting holding statement
MINUTE 30-45:  Legal review of holding statement, approve
MINUTE 45-60:  Publish holding statement on primary channel
HOUR 1-2:      Prepare comprehensive response, begin stakeholder outreach
HOUR 2-4:      Full response published, media briefing, customer communication
HOUR 4-24:     Monitor reaction, iterate messaging, prepare follow-up
```

**Why the golden hour matters:**
- If you don't tell your story in the first hour, someone else will
- Social media accelerates: a local complaint can become global in 30 minutes
- The first narrative often becomes the dominant narrative — control it or lose it

### Communication Channels During Crisis

| Channel | Use For | Timing | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Company website (dark site) | Official statements, FAQ, timeline | Immediate (holding), then updated continuously | Formal, factual, empathetic |
| Social media | Acknowledge, direct to official statement, respond to individuals | Within 30 minutes | Human, empathetic, transparent |
| Email (customer) | Notify affected individuals directly | Within 2-4 hours | Personal, specific to their situation |
| Email (employee) | Internal alignment, talking points | Before or simultaneously with public statement | Honest, supportive, clear instructions |
| Press statement / media briefing | Formal media response | Within 1-2 hours of going public | Professional, controlled, quotable |
| CEO video / open letter | Demonstrate leadership accountability | Within 24-48 hours | Personal, empathetic, action-oriented |

### Tone and Messaging: The CARE Framework

| Principle | What It Means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| C — Compassion | Show genuine concern for affected people | "We understand the impact this has on you and your family" |
| A — Accountability | Own the mistake, don't blame others | "This happened on our watch, and we take full responsibility" |
| R — Remediation | Explain specific actions you're taking | "We have immediately engaged forensic experts and notified authorities" |
| E — Evolution | Commit to systemic change | "We are implementing new security protocols to ensure this never happens again" |

**What NEVER to say:**
- "No comment" (signals guilt or indifference)
- "It's not our fault" (blame-shifting destroys trust)
- "We take [issue] very seriously" without action (empty words)
- "This is an isolated incident" before you have the facts
- "Per our legal counsel..." (sounds defensive and corporate)
- Humor or sarcasm (wildly inappropriate during a crisis)

### Social Media Response During Crisis

| Action | Guidance |
|---|---|
| Pause scheduled content | Immediately stop all scheduled posts across channels |
| Pin crisis response | Pin your official statement to the top of all profiles |
| Respond to individuals | Acknowledge individual concerns, direct to official resources |
| Don't delete posts | Deleting the original problematic post amplifies backlash (Streisand Effect) |
| Monitor 24/7 | Assign team members to round-the-clock monitoring during active crisis |
| Track sentiment | Use listening tools to gauge real-time public reaction |
| Avoid arguments | Never argue with individuals on social media during a crisis |
| Document everything | Screenshot all interactions for legal and analysis purposes |

### Internal Communication During Crisis

**The "Inside-Out" Rule:** Employees should NEVER learn about a crisis from the news. Always communicate internally before or simultaneously with external communication.

| Audience | Content | Channel | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Executive team | Full situation briefing, legal implications, response plan | Emergency call/meeting | Immediately |
| All employees | What happened, what we're doing, what they should say | Company-wide email + Slack | Before public statement |
| Customer-facing staff | Detailed talking points, FAQs, escalation procedures | Team briefing + document | Before public statement |
| Partners/vendors | Appropriate level of detail, assurance of continuity | Email or direct call | Within hours |

## 15.4.3 Post-Crisis Recovery

### Reputation Repair Strategies

**The Recovery Timeline:**

| Phase | Timeframe | Focus | Actions |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stabilization | Week 1-2 | Stop the bleeding | Resolve immediate issues, maintain communication cadence |
| Rebuilding | Month 1-3 | Demonstrate change | Publish progress updates, implement systemic fixes |
| Rehabilitation | Month 3-12 | Rebuild trust | Positive content creation, media relationship repair, community engagement |
| Long-term | Year 1+ | Prevent recurrence | Ongoing monitoring, updated crisis plans, culture change |

### Real-Time Example: Domino's "Pizza Turnaround" Campaign (2009-2010)

**The crisis:**
- Domino's had the worst taste perception of any national pizza chain
- Two employees posted a viral video of themselves contaminating food in the kitchen
- Customer satisfaction scores hit rock bottom

**The recovery (brilliant execution):**

1. **Immediate response:** CEO Patrick Doyle appeared on YouTube within 48 hours, fired the employees, cooperated with health department

2. **"Pizza Turnaround" campaign:** Instead of hiding from criticism, Domino's created a documentary-style campaign where they:
   - Read the harshest customer reviews ON CAMERA
   - Admitted their pizza wasn't good enough
   - Showed themselves completely reformulating the recipe
   - Invited critics to try the new pizza on camera

3. **Transparency strategy:**
   - Launched "Show Us Your Pizza" campaign where customers could post real photos
   - Installed webcams in stores so customers could watch their pizza being made
   - Published all feedback — good and bad — on their website

**Results:**
- Same-store sales grew 14.3% in Q1 2010 (fastest growth in fast-food history)
- Stock went from $8 (crisis low) to $400+ over the following decade
- Became a case study in how radical transparency can turn a crisis into a growth opportunity

### Content Creation for Recovery

| Content Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| CEO Open Letter | Personal accountability and forward-looking commitment | Published on company blog, LinkedIn, and sent to customers |
| Progress Reports | Show measurable improvement | Monthly updates on actions taken, metrics improved |
| Behind-the-Scenes | Demonstrate systemic change | Video tours of new processes, interviews with team leads |
| Customer Stories | Rebuild trust through proof | Testimonials from customers who've seen improvement |
| Third-Party Validation | External credibility | Security audits, certifications, independent reviews |
| Community Engagement | Show you're giving back | CSR initiatives, partnerships, community investment |

### Lessons Learned Documentation

**Post-Crisis Review Framework:**

| Question | Purpose |
|---|---|
| What was the root cause? | Prevent recurrence |
| When did we first detect the issue? | Improve monitoring |
| How fast was our initial response? | Benchmark against target |
| Was our messaging effective? | Improve templates and tone |
| How well did internal communication work? | Strengthen internal processes |
| What tools/systems failed us? | Technology investment decisions |
| What worked well? | Preserve and codify best practices |
| What would we do differently? | Concrete process improvements |
| What new scenarios should we plan for? | Update crisis plan |
| How has public sentiment recovered? | Measure effectiveness of recovery |

| Gap | Impact | How to Close |
|---|---|---|
| No crisis plan exists | Team scrambles, makes mistakes under pressure | Develop a comprehensive crisis plan and store it where the team can access it instantly |
| Never run simulations | Plan looks good on paper but fails under pressure | Run 2 tabletop exercises per year with realistic scenarios |

## Best Practices for 15.4

1. **Prepare before crisis hits** — The time to write a crisis plan is NOT during a crisis
2. **Speed over perfection** — A good holding statement in 30 minutes beats a perfect statement in 4 hours
3. **Lead with empathy** — Show you care about the people affected, not just the business impact
4. **Communicate early and often** — Silence is interpreted as guilt or indifference
5. **Own the mistake** — Taking responsibility builds more trust than any excuse
6. **Tell employees first** — They are your ambassadors or your leakers; treat them accordingly
7. **Don't delete, don't hide** — The internet never forgets; the Streisand Effect is real
8. **Separate facts from speculation** — Only communicate what you know for certain
9. **Monitor the narrative** — Track how the public is responding and adjust messaging if needed
10. **Follow through on commitments** — If you promise change, deliver it visibly and publicly
11. **Document everything** — Every decision, every communication, every timeline for legal and learning purposes
12. **Thank those who helped** — Acknowledge team members, partners, and supportive community members after the crisis passes

## Strategies for 15.4

### The "Radical Transparency" Strategy
When a crisis hits, share more information than anyone expects. Publish timelines, actions taken, what you know and what you don't. This disarms critics and builds trust faster than any other approach. (Example: Buffer, Zoom)

### The "CEO Front and Center" Strategy
Put the CEO or highest-ranking leader in front of the camera within 24-48 hours. Personal accountability from the top signals that the company takes the issue seriously. Written statements are secondary. (Example: J&J, Zoom's Eric Yuan)

### The "Fix and Show" Strategy
Don't just promise to fix the problem — show the fix in real time. Live updates, progress dashboards, public audits. Turn the recovery itself into a transparent, ongoing story. (Example: Domino's Pizza Turnaround)

### The "Inoculation" Strategy
Before a crisis hits (or between crises), proactively build a reservoir of goodwill: community investment, CSR initiatives, positive media coverage, strong customer relationships. This "goodwill reserve" provides buffer when a crisis inevitably occurs.

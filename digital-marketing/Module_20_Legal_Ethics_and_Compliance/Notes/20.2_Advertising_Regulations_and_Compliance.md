## 20.2.1 FTC Advertising Guidelines (US)

### What Is the FTC's Role in Advertising

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) is the primary US agency that polices advertising. Its core mandate: advertisements must be **truthful, not misleading, and substantiated.** The FTC has the power to investigate, sue, and fine companies for deceptive advertising — and in recent years, it has aggressively expanded enforcement to influencer marketing, dark patterns, and AI-generated content.

### Truth in Advertising — The Three Pillars

| Pillar | Meaning | Violation Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Truthful** | Ads must not make false statements | A weight-loss supplement claiming "lose 30 lbs in 7 days" with no evidence |
| **Not Misleading** | Ads must not create false impressions, even with technically true statements | "Up to 50% off" when only one item has that discount |
| **Substantiated** | Claims must be backed by evidence before making them | A skincare brand claiming "clinically proven" without any clinical studies |

**The "Reasonable Consumer" Standard:** The FTC evaluates ads based on how a reasonable consumer would interpret them — not how a lawyer would read the fine print.

### Substantiation of Claims

Before making any performance, health, safety, or comparative claim, you must have a "reasonable basis" — which typically means:
- **Health/safety claims:** Require competent and reliable scientific evidence (usually peer-reviewed clinical trials)
- **Performance claims:** Require testing demonstrating the product performs as claimed
- **"Up to" claims:** The advertised result must be achievable by a significant portion of users, not just one outlier
- **Testimonials:** If a customer says "I lost 30 lbs," you must disclose typical results if they differ significantly

**Real-Time Example:**
The FTC sued Luminosity (makers of Lumosity brain training app) for $2 million in 2016. Lumosity claimed their games could help prevent Alzheimer's and improve cognitive function, but the FTC found no scientific evidence supporting these claims. The settlement required clear disclosures and refunds.

### Endorsement and Testimonial Guidelines (Updated 2023)

The FTC significantly updated its Endorsement Guides in 2023 to address influencer marketing, fake reviews, and AI content.

**Key Rules:**

| Rule | Explanation | Violation |
|---|---|---|
| **Material Connection Disclosure** | Any relationship that could affect credibility must be disclosed | Influencer posts about a product they were paid to promote without saying so |
| **Honest Opinions Only** | Endorsers must actually use the product and share genuine opinions | Influencer claims to love a product they've never used |
| **Typical Results** | If a testimonial is atypical, disclose what the average consumer can expect | "I made $10,000/month" without disclosing most users earn $200 |
| **Brand Liability** | Brands are liable for influencer non-compliance, not just the influencer | Brand provides no disclosure guidance to its ambassador program |
| **Fake Reviews** | Businesses cannot create, purchase, or incentivize fake reviews | Company pays for 5-star reviews on Amazon or Google |
| **Employee Disclosure** | Employees reviewing their own company's products must disclose | Marketing team member leaves a glowing Google review without identifying as employee |
| **AI-Generated Endorsements** | AI-fabricated testimonials or reviews are explicitly prohibited | Using ChatGPT to generate fake customer reviews |

### Influencer Marketing Disclosure Requirements

**Where Disclosures Must Be:**
- At the beginning of content or superimposed on images/video
- In the same language as the endorsement
- Clear and conspicuous — not buried in hashtags

**Acceptable Disclosures:**

| Format | Compliant | Non-Compliant |
|---|---|---|
| **Instagram Post** | #ad or #sponsored at the beginning of caption | #ad buried in 30+ hashtags at the bottom |
| **Instagram Story** | "Paid partnership with [Brand]" label | Only visible for 1 second in a 15-second story |
| **YouTube Video** | Verbal disclosure in first 30 seconds + text overlay | Only in the description box |
| **TikTok** | "Paid promotion" or #ad visible throughout | Disclosure only in comments |
| **Podcast** | Verbal disclosure at beginning of segment | Only in show notes |
| **Twitter/X** | #ad or "Sponsored by [Brand]" in the tweet | Disclosure in a reply tweet |

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2022, the FTC sued fashion retailer Fashion Nova for $4.2 million for suppressing negative customer reviews. The company used a third-party review platform that automatically approved 4- and 5-star reviews but routed 1- to 3-star reviews to internal approval where they were never published.

### Environmental Marketing Claims ("Green" Claims)

The FTC's Green Guides govern environmental marketing claims. Companies face enforcement for:

| Claim Type | Requirement | Example Violation |
|---|---|---|
| **"Recyclable"** | Must be recyclable in most communities | Labeling a product recyclable when only 10% of facilities accept it |
| **"Biodegradable"** | Must decompose within 1 year in normal disposal | Plastic bags labeled biodegradable that take 500+ years in landfill |
| **"Carbon Neutral"** | Must substantiate with verified offsets | Claiming carbon neutral based on unverified offset certificates |
| **"Sustainable"** | Extremely broad — must qualify with specifics | Using "sustainable" without explaining what is sustainable and how |
| **"Eco-friendly"** | Too vague for standalone use | Marketing "eco-friendly" without substantiating environmental benefit |

**Real-Time Example:**
Keurig Canada was fined $3 million in 2022 for misleading recycling claims on K-Cup pods. The company said pods were "recyclable" but most municipal recycling programs could not actually process them.

### Health and Wellness Marketing Claims

The FTC, along with the FDA, closely monitors health claims:
- **Structure/function claims** ("supports immune health") require substantiation but no FDA pre-approval
- **Disease claims** ("treats diabetes") require FDA approval
- **Implied claims** are treated the same as explicit claims — if a reasonable consumer would interpret an image or testimonial as making a health claim, it counts

### FTC Enforcement Actions — Notable Cases

| Year | Company | Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2023 | **Epic Games (Fortnite)** | Dark patterns tricking children into purchases | $520 million |
| 2022 | **Fashion Nova** | Suppressing negative reviews | $4.2 million |
| 2022 | **Roomster** | Fake reviews to boost apartment listing app | $5.6 million |
| 2021 | **Teami** | Influencer health claims without disclosure | $15.2 million |
| 2019 | **Sunday Riley** | CEO directed employees to write fake Sephora reviews | Settlement + monitoring |
| 2016 | **Luminosity** | Unsubstantiated brain training claims | $2 million |

### Implementation Steps for FTC Compliance

1. **Advertising Review Process** — Establish a legal/compliance review for all ad creative before launch
2. **Substantiation Files** — Maintain evidence files for every product claim (clinical studies, test results, surveys)
3. **Influencer Agreements** — Include mandatory disclosure language in all influencer/ambassador contracts
4. **Disclosure Training** — Train influencers and affiliates on proper disclosure with visual examples
5. **Review Monitoring** — Audit customer reviews for authenticity; never suppress or incentivize reviews unfairly
6. **Green Claims Audit** — Have environmental claims vetted by a third-party sustainability consultant
7. **Health Claims Review** — If making any health or wellness claims, consult both FTC Green Guides and FDA labeling requirements
8. **Document Everything** — Keep records of all substantiation, influencer contracts, and disclosure compliance monitoring

| **Grin** | Influencer platform with contract and disclosure management |
| **TINT (formerly Filestack)** | UGC rights management — permission tracking for user content |
| **Bazaarvoice** | Authentic review management platform |

| **Advertising Self-Regulatory Council (ASRC)** | Industry body for pre-screening ad claims |

### Best Practices

- **"Clear and Conspicuous" Test:** If a viewer could miss the disclosure while scrolling, watching, or listening — it fails
- **Annual Influencer Training:** Update your disclosure guide annually and require all influencers to re-acknowledge
- **Claim Library:** Maintain a centralized library of approved claims with their supporting evidence — marketing teams should only use pre-approved language
- **Monitor Affiliate Content:** Affiliates are an extension of your brand — audit their content monthly for compliance
- **Comparative Advertising:** You can name competitors, but claims must be truthful, substantiated, and not misleading

## 20.2.2 Platform-Specific Advertising Policies

### Google Ads Policies

**Prohibited Content:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Counterfeit goods** | Fake branded products, knockoffs |
| **Dangerous products** | Recreational drugs, weapons, explosives, tobacco |
| **Dishonest behavior** | Phishing, click fraud, deceptive ad practices |
| **Inappropriate content** | Hate speech, violence, cruelty, exploitation |

**Restricted Content (allowed with limitations):**

| Category | Restrictions |
|---|---|
| **Alcohol** | Must comply with local laws, age-gating required, no targeting minors |
| **Gambling** | Requires Google certification, restricted geographies |
| **Healthcare/Pharma** | Prescription drugs restricted by country, requires certification |
| **Financial Services** | APR disclosures required, restrictions on crypto ads |
| **Political Ads** | Identity verification required, transparency reports, targeting limits |

**Special Ad Categories:**
Google requires advertisers in housing, employment, and credit to follow additional restrictions including limited audience targeting to prevent discrimination.

**Real-Time Example:**
Google suspended over 5.2 billion ads in 2022 across all policy violations. The most common reasons were ad misrepresentation, destination mismatch, and restricted content violations.

### Meta (Facebook/Instagram) Advertising Policies

**Key Restrictions:**

| Area | Policy |
|---|---|
| **Personal Attributes** | Cannot assert or imply personal attributes ("We know you're struggling with...") |
| **Before/After Images** | Prohibited in weight loss and cosmetic ads |
| **Discriminatory Targeting** | Housing, employment, credit ads cannot target by age, gender, zip code |
| **Cryptocurrency** | Requires written permission, strict approval process |
| **Misleading Claims** | No "miracle" products, exaggerated results, or sensationalized content |
| **Engagement Bait** | Cannot ask users to like, share, or comment for rewards |

**Special Ad Categories on Meta:**
When you declare a "Special Ad Category" (credit, employment, housing, social issues/politics), Meta automatically:
- Removes age, gender, and zip code targeting
- Restricts detailed targeting options
- Requires identity verification for political ads

**Real-Time Example:**
In 2023, the US Department of Justice settled with Meta for $115,000 over allegations that its housing ad delivery algorithm discriminated by race — even when advertisers did not intend to discriminate. This showed that algorithmic bias in ad delivery can create compliance risk for platforms AND advertisers.

### TikTok Advertising Policies

| Area | Policy Detail |
|---|---|
| **Age-Gating** | No ads targeting users under 18 for restricted products |
| **Branded Content** | Must use TikTok's branded content toggle |
| **Political Ads** | Completely banned on TikTok |
| **Influencer Disclosure** | Platform-level "Paid Partnership" label required |
| **Misleading Claims** | Weight loss, financial gain claims heavily scrutinized |

### Common Ad Disapprovals and How to Resolve Them

| Disapproval Reason | Common Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Misleading claims** | Exaggerated performance/results language | Use qualified language ("may help," "results vary") |
| **Destination mismatch** | Landing page content doesn't match ad | Ensure ad copy and landing page are aligned |
| **Prohibited content** | Restricted product without certification | Apply for platform certification or adjust creative |
| **Clickbait** | Sensationalized headlines or images | Use factual, specific ad copy |
| **Policy circumvention** | Trying to bypass policies with workarounds | Follow policies directly; circumvention results in account suspension |
| **Copyright/trademark** | Using competitor brand names or logos improperly | Remove unauthorized brand references |

### Implementation Steps

1. **Policy Review Checklist** — Before launching on any platform, review its advertising policies (they update frequently)
2. **Creative Brief Template** — Include compliance checkboxes in your creative brief (special categories, disclaimers, restricted content)
3. **Pre-Launch Review** — Run all ad creative through platform policy review tools before submission
4. **Appeal Process** — Develop a standard operating procedure for handling ad disapprovals and filing appeals
5. **Multi-Platform Compliance Matrix** — Maintain a spreadsheet mapping which products/claims are allowed on which platforms

### Best Practices

- **Read the Policy Updates:** Google and Meta update policies quarterly — subscribe to their policy update emails
- **Special Categories First:** Always check if your industry falls under special ad categories before building targeting
- **Screenshot Everything:** Keep screenshots of approved ad creative and settings as compliance records
- **Platform Certifications:** Proactively get certified for restricted categories (pharma, gambling, alcohol) before you need them

## 20.2.3 Email Marketing Laws

### CAN-SPAM Act (US)

The CAN-SPAM Act (Controlling the Assault of Non-Solicited Pornography And Marketing) applies to all commercial email sent to US recipients.

**Key Requirements:**

| Requirement | Detail | Penalty for Violation |
|---|---|---|
| **No Misleading Headers** | "From," "To," "Reply-To" must be accurate | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **No Deceptive Subject Lines** | Subject must reflect content of the email | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **Identify as Ad** | Must clearly disclose the message is an advertisement | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **Physical Address** | Must include a valid postal address | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **Unsubscribe Mechanism** | Must provide a clear, working opt-out method | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **Honor Opt-Outs** | Must process unsubscribe requests within 10 business days | Up to $51,744 per email |
| **Monitor Third Parties** | You're responsible for emails sent on your behalf | Up to $51,744 per email |

**Important:** CAN-SPAM does NOT require prior consent for commercial email — the US model is opt-out. However, best practice is to use opt-in to ensure deliverability and engagement.

### CASL (Canada's Anti-Spam Legislation)

CASL is significantly stricter than CAN-SPAM — it requires express consent BEFORE sending commercial electronic messages (CEMs) to Canadian recipients.

| Consent Type | Duration | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Express Consent** | Indefinite until withdrawn | Explicit opt-in (checkbox, sign-up form) |
| **Implied Consent** | 2 years from last purchase, 6 months from inquiry | Existing business relationship |

**Penalties:** Up to $10 million per violation for businesses, $1 million per violation for individuals. CASL also includes a private right of action.

**Real-Time Example:**
Compu-Finder (a training company) was fined $1.1 million in 2015 — the first major CASL penalty — for sending commercial emails without proper consent. They had purchased email lists and emailed people who had never opted in.

### GDPR Email Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Legal Basis** | Consent (explicit opt-in) or legitimate interest (existing customers, similar products) |
| **Double Opt-In** | Recommended best practice (not legally required but practically necessary for proof) |
| **Granular Consent** | Must separate consent for different communication types |
| **Easy Unsubscribe** | Must be as easy to unsubscribe as it was to subscribe |
| **Record Keeping** | Must prove when and how consent was obtained |

### Transactional vs. Commercial Email

| Type | Examples | CAN-SPAM Rules Apply? |
|---|---|---|
| **Transactional** | Order confirmation, shipping update, password reset, account alerts | Exempt from most rules (but cannot be misleading) |
| **Commercial** | Promotions, newsletters, product announcements, sales | Full compliance required |
| **Hybrid** | Order confirmation with a promotional section | Primary purpose determines classification — if primarily commercial, full rules apply |

### Third-Party Email List Usage

| Practice | CAN-SPAM | CASL | GDPR |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Buying email lists** | Technically legal (opt-out model) | Illegal without express consent | Illegal without valid consent chain |
| **Renting email lists** | Legal if sender identified | Illegal without consent | Illegal without consent |
| **Co-registration** | Legal if disclosed | Legal if consent specific | Legal only with clear, specific consent |

**Best Practice Across All Jurisdictions:** Never buy or rent email lists. Build organic lists through content, lead magnets, and genuine value exchange.

### Implementation Steps

1. **Audit Current Lists** — Verify consent status for every email address in your database
2. **Segment by Jurisdiction** — Tag subscribers by region and apply the appropriate consent standard
3. **Double Opt-In Flow** — Implement confirmation emails for all new subscribers
4. **Preference Center** — Let subscribers choose frequency, content type, and channels
5. **Unsubscribe Testing** — Test your unsubscribe flow monthly — make it work in one click
6. **Consent Records** — Store timestamp, IP address, and exact consent language for every opt-in
7. **Sunset Policy** — Automatically suppress subscribers who haven't engaged in 6-12 months
8. **Vendor Compliance** — Ensure your ESP (Mailchimp, HubSpot, Klaviyo) meets compliance requirements for your jurisdictions

### Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Mailchimp** | Built-in GDPR consent fields, double opt-in, compliance features |
| **Klaviyo** | Advanced consent management for e-commerce, sunset flow automation |
| **HubSpot** | GDPR-compliant forms with granular consent options |
| **Litmus** | Email testing including CAN-SPAM compliance checks |
| **ZeroBounce** | Email list validation to reduce bounce rates and spam complaints |
| **Mail-Tester** | Free tool to check if your emails might be flagged as spam |

### Gaps Commonly Found

| Gap | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No unsubscribe link in emails | $51,744 per email (CAN-SPAM) | Add clear unsubscribe link to every commercial email |
| No physical address | CAN-SPAM violation | Include valid postal address in email footer |
| Purchased email lists being used | CASL/GDPR violation, deliverability disaster | Immediately stop; transition to organic list building |
| Unsubscribe takes more than 10 days | CAN-SPAM violation | Automate instant unsubscribe processing |
| No consent records | Cannot prove compliance | Retroactively request re-consent; log all future opt-ins |
| Same email treatment for all regions | Violating stricter laws | Segment by region and apply appropriate consent standards |

### Best Practices

- **One-Click Unsubscribe:** Don't make people log in, confirm, or answer a survey to unsubscribe — one click, done
- **List Hygiene:** Clean your list quarterly — remove bounced addresses, unengaged subscribers, and role-based emails
- **Sunset Flows:** Before removing unengaged subscribers, send a "We miss you" re-engagement series — then suppress if no response
- **Transparent Subject Lines:** Never use misleading subjects like "RE:" or "FWD:" on marketing emails
- **Deliverability Monitoring:** Monitor sender reputation with Google Postmaster Tools, Microsoft SNDS, and your ESP's deliverability dashboards

## 20.2.4 Telecommunications & SMS Regulations

### TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act)

The TCPA is the strictest marketing regulation in the US when it comes to penalties. It governs calls and text messages (SMS/MMS) to consumers.

**Key Requirements:**

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prior Express Written Consent** | Required before sending marketing texts or making autodialed calls |
| **Consent Must Be Clear** | Written agreement must clearly authorize specific types of messages |
| **Not a Condition of Purchase** | Cannot require consent to receive texts as a condition of buying |
| **Opt-Out Honoring** | Must process opt-outs (STOP) immediately |
| **Calling Hours** | Cannot call before 8 AM or after 9 PM in the consumer's local time |
| **Caller ID** | Must provide accurate caller identification |

**Penalties:**
- $500 per unsolicited text/call
- $1,500 per willful violation
- These are PER MESSAGE — a 10,000-message blast without consent = $5-15 million in potential liability

**Real-Time Example:**
Papa John's settled a TCPA class-action lawsuit for $16.5 million in 2016 for sending unsolicited text messages. The class included over 500,000 consumers who received promotional texts without proper consent.

### A2P 10DLC Registration

Application-to-Person (A2P) messaging over 10-digit long codes (10DLC) requires registration with carriers through The Campaign Registry (TCR).

**Registration Tiers:**

| Component | What It Is | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand Registration** | Register your business identity | Carriers verify you're a legitimate business |
| **Campaign Registration** | Register each messaging use case | Carriers review your message content and opt-in flow |
| **Trust Score** | Carrier-assigned score based on your brand reputation | Higher score = higher throughput and delivery rates |

**Without Registration:** Messages may be filtered, blocked, or marked as spam by carriers. Throughput drops to as low as 1 message per second.

### Short Code Compliance

Short codes (5-6 digit numbers like 12345) require:
- CTIA approval for all short code programs
- Full disclosure of program terms (frequency, message types, data usage)
- Keyword opt-in (e.g., text "JOIN" to 12345)
- HELP and STOP keyword support mandatory
- Monthly compliance audits by your short code provider

### SMS Opt-In and Opt-Out Requirements

**Valid Opt-In Methods:**

| Method | Compliant Example |
|---|---|
| **Web Form** | Checkbox (not pre-ticked): "I agree to receive marketing texts from [Brand] at [frequency]. Msg & data rates may apply. Reply STOP to unsubscribe." |
| **Keyword** | "Text JOIN to 55555 to get 10% off. Msg freq varies. Msg & data rates apply. Reply STOP to cancel." |
| **Point of Sale** | Written form or tablet sign-up with clear text consent language |
| **QR Code** | QR code leading to a compliant web form opt-in |

**Mandatory Disclosures at Opt-In:**
1. Program name/brand identity
2. Message frequency (e.g., "up to 4 msgs/month")
3. "Msg & data rates may apply"
4. How to opt out (Reply STOP)
5. How to get help (Reply HELP)
6. Link to privacy policy and terms

### Implementation Steps

1. **Register Your Brand** — Complete A2P 10DLC registration through your SMS provider (Twilio, Attentive, Klaviyo, Postscript)
2. **Register Campaigns** — Submit each messaging campaign with sample messages and opt-in flow documentation
3. **Build Compliant Opt-In Flows** — Include all required disclosures (frequency, data rates, opt-out, help)
4. **Configure STOP/HELP Keywords** — Automate immediate processing of opt-out and help requests
5. **Maintain Consent Records** — Log opt-in method, timestamp, IP/device, and exact consent language
6. **DNC List Scrubbing** — Scrub your calling/texting list against the National Do Not Call Registry before every campaign
7. **Calling Hours Enforcement** — Configure your platform to respect time zones and calling windows
8. **Regular Audits** — Audit opt-in flows, consent records, and message content quarterly

| Tool | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Twilio** | SMS/MMS API with built-in A2P 10DLC registration and compliance |
| **Attentive** | SMS marketing platform with TCPA-compliant opt-in flows |
| **Postscript** | Shopify-focused SMS marketing with compliance features |
| **Klaviyo** | Unified email + SMS platform with consent management |
| **TCR (The Campaign Registry)** | A2P 10DLC brand and campaign registration |
| **Gryphon.ai** | TCPA compliance monitoring and DNC list management |

### Gaps Commonly Found

| Gap | Risk | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No written consent for marketing texts | $500-$1,500 per message liability | Implement explicit written consent flows |
| Pre-checked consent boxes | Consent is invalid | Use unchecked boxes with clear language |
| No STOP keyword processing | TCPA violation | Configure automated opt-out processing |
| Not scrubbing against DNC list | TCPA violation + state DNC violations | Integrate DNC scrubbing into campaign workflow |
| Missing disclosures in opt-in | Consent may be invalid | Add all required disclosures (frequency, rates, opt-out) |
| No A2P 10DLC registration | Messages filtered/blocked by carriers | Register through your SMS provider |

### Best Practices

- **Double Opt-In for SMS:** Send a confirmation text after sign-up: "Reply YES to confirm you want texts from [Brand]"
- **Frequency Caps:** Never send more messages than disclosed at opt-in — if you said "up to 4/month," stick to it
- **Quiet Hours:** Even though TCPA allows texts until 9 PM, best practice is to avoid texting after 8 PM local time
- **Value-First Messaging:** Every SMS should deliver value — exclusive offers, order updates, or genuinely useful info — not just noise
- **Consent Expiration:** Re-confirm consent after 18-24 months of inactivity — stale consent increases complaint risk
- **Separate Transactional from Marketing:** Use different sending numbers for transactional (order updates) and marketing messages

## Key Strategies for Advertising Compliance

### Strategy 1: Compliance-by-Design Creative Process

Build compliance into your creative workflow, not as a last-minute checkpoint:
- Include compliance questions in creative briefs ("Is this a restricted category? What claims are being made? What substantiation do we have?")
- Require legal review for all claims-based creative (health, financial, performance)
- Create a "pre-approved claims library" that marketing teams can draw from

### Strategy 2: Platform-Specific Playbooks

Create a playbook for each ad platform:
- Map your products to platform-specific policies
- Pre-build compliant ad templates per platform
- Document the appeal process for each platform

### Strategy 3: Influencer Compliance Infrastructure

Treat influencer compliance as a system, not ad hoc:
- Automated disclosure verification tools (CreatorIQ, Grin)
- Contractual language requiring platform-native disclosure tools
- Monthly content audits with documented findings
- Annual compliance refresher training for all influencers/affiliates

### Strategy 4: Proactive Regulatory Monitoring

Stay ahead of enforcement:
- Subscribe to FTC press releases and enforcement actions
- Monitor platform policy update blogs
- Join industry groups (ANA, IAB, ASRC) for early guidance on emerging regulations
- Conduct annual mock audits modeled on FTC investigative processes

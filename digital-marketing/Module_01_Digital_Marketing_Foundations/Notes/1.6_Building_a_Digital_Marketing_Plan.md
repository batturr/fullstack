## 1.6.1 Situation Analysis

Before building any strategy, you must deeply understand where you are today. A situation analysis answers: "What is the current state of our digital marketing?"

### Internal Audit: Current Digital Presence, Assets, Capabilities

**What to audit:**

```
WEBSITE
├── Domain authority (Moz/Ahrefs)
├── Monthly traffic (GA4)
├── Page speed (Google PageSpeed Insights)
├── Mobile responsiveness
├── Conversion rate by page type
├── Technical SEO health (crawl errors, sitemap, robots.txt)
└── Content inventory (blog posts, landing pages, resources)

EMAIL
├── List size and growth rate
├── Open rate, CTR, unsubscribe rate
├── Deliverability rate
├── Segmentation status
└── Automation sequences in place

SOCIAL MEDIA
├── Followers by platform
├── Engagement rate
├── Posting frequency
├── Content types performing best
└── Share of voice vs. competitors

PAID MEDIA
├── Active campaigns and budgets
├── ROAS by channel/campaign
├── CPA by channel
├── Creative assets inventory
└── Audience lists and pixel data

CONTENT
├── Total assets (blog, video, podcast, ebooks, etc.)
├── Top performing content (traffic, conversions, shares)
├── Content gaps vs. competitor
├── Brand guidelines and style guide
└── Content creation capacity (internal vs. outsourced)

TECHNOLOGY STACK
├── CRM system
├── Analytics tools
├── Marketing automation
├── SEO tools
├── Social management tools
└── Ad platforms and pixels installed
```

**Real-Time Example — Internal Audit Results (Fictional SaaS Company):**

| Area | Current State | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Website | 35K monthly visits, DA 28, 1.8% conversion rate | Below industry avg (3%) — UX issues identified |
| Email | 12K subscribers, 19% open rate, 2.1% CTR | Healthy engagement but small list — growth needed |
| Social | 5K LinkedIn, 2K Twitter, 800 Instagram | Underinvested — competitors have 5-10x following |
| Paid | $8K/month Google Ads, 3.2:1 ROAS | Performing well — room to scale |
| Content | 45 blog posts, no video, 2 ebooks | Major content gap — competitors publish 15+ posts/month |
| Tech Stack | HubSpot Free, GA4, Mailchimp | Fragmented — need unified platform |

### External Audit: Market Conditions, Competition, Regulations

**What to assess:**
- Market size and growth rate (TAM, SAM, SOM)
- Competitive landscape and recent moves
- Regulatory environment (GDPR, CCPA, CAN-SPAM)
- Platform changes (algorithm updates, new features)
- Economic conditions affecting buyer behavior
- Technology trends impacting your industry

**Real-Time Example:**
A health tech startup conducting an external audit in 2025 finds:
- **Market:** Digital health market growing at 27% CAGR ($500B by 2028)
- **Competition:** 3 new VC-backed competitors entered in the last 6 months
- **Regulation:** HIPAA compliance required for all health data marketing
- **Technology:** AI-powered health chatbots are trending (20K monthly searches)
- **Insight:** Opportunity to position as the "AI-first, HIPAA-compliant" solution before competitors adopt AI

### PESTLE Analysis

Macro-environmental analysis that identifies external forces affecting your business.

| Factor | Questions to Ask | Example (E-commerce Brand, 2025) |
|---|---|---|
| **Political** | Government policies, trade laws, taxation? | New digital services tax in EU (3% on revenue) |
| **Economic** | Consumer spending, inflation, interest rates? | Post-COVID recovery but inflation squeezing discretionary spending |
| **Social** | Demographics, cultural trends, lifestyle? | Sustainability demand — 73% of Gen Z willing to pay more for sustainable products |
| **Technological** | Innovations, automation, AI disruption? | AI-powered personalization increasing conversion 2-5x |
| **Legal** | Data privacy, advertising regulations? | GDPR enforcement fines doubled in 2024; FTC cracking down on influencer disclosures |
| **Environmental** | Sustainability, carbon footprint, ESG? | Consumers expect carbon-neutral shipping options |

**Real-Time Example — How Patagonia Uses PESTLE:**
- **Political:** Trade tensions → diversified supply chain messaging
- **Economic:** Recession fears → "Buy Less, Demand More" (anti-consumerism as brand differentiation)
- **Social:** Environmental activism growing → doubled down on climate marketing
- **Technological:** Used blockchain for supply chain transparency → trust differentiator
- **Legal:** EU Green Claims Directive → made sure all sustainability claims are substantiated
- **Environmental:** Carbon-neutral since 2025 → used as primary marketing message

### Digital Maturity Assessment

Rate your organization across these dimensions:

| Dimension | Level 1 (Beginner) | Level 3 (Intermediate) | Level 5 (Leader) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Strategy** | Reactive, campaign-by-campaign | Annual plan with quarterly reviews | Real-time optimization with AI |
| **Data** | Basic GA, no CRM | CRM + multi-touch attribution | CDP + predictive analytics + ML |
| **Content** | Occasional blog posts | Editorial calendar, multi-format | Personalized content at scale |
| **Technology** | Manual processes, disconnected tools | Integrated marketing stack | Full automation + AI orchestration |
| **Talent** | Generalist wearing multiple hats | Specialists per channel | T-shaped marketers + data scientists |
| **Customer Experience** | Same message for everyone | Segmented messaging | 1:1 personalization across channels |

**Real-Time Example:** Most SMBs are at Level 1-2. Getting to Level 3 creates significant competitive advantage. Level 5 is where companies like Amazon, Netflix, and Spotify operate.

## 1.6.2 Strategy Development

### Defining Your Value Proposition

Your value proposition answers: "Why should a customer choose you over every alternative, including doing nothing?"

**Value Proposition Canvas (Strategyzer):**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                                      │
│   CUSTOMER PROFILE                    VALUE MAP                      │
│   ┌──────────────────┐               ┌──────────────────┐           │
│   │ Jobs to Be Done  │               │ Products/Services│           │
│   │ (what they need)  │     FIT →    │ (what you offer) │           │
│   │                  │               │                  │           │
│   │ Pains            │               │ Pain Relievers   │           │
│   │ (frustrations)   │     FIT →    │ (how you solve)  │           │
│   │                  │               │                  │           │
│   │ Gains            │               │ Gain Creators    │           │
│   │ (desired outcome)│     FIT →    │ (how you delight)│           │
│   └──────────────────┘               └──────────────────┘           │
│                                                                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Real-Time Example — Slack's Value Proposition:**

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Customer Job** | Communicate effectively with team without drowning in email |
| **Pain** | Email overload (avg. worker spends 28% of day on email), information silos, lost messages |
| **Gain** | Faster decisions, organized channels, searchable history |
| **Product** | Real-time messaging platform with channels, threads, integrations |
| **Pain Reliever** | Reduces email by 48%, searchable message history, organized channels |
| **Gain Creator** | 2,400+ app integrations, custom workflows, everything in one place |
| **Value Proposition** | "Where work happens. Slack replaces email inside your company — making your team more productive and aligned." |

### Positioning Strategy

| Strategy | Definition | When to Use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Differentiation** | Be different from competitors | You have a unique feature, approach, or experience | Apple: Premium design + ecosystem |
| **Cost Leadership** | Be the cheapest option | You can operate at lower margins | Walmart: "Everyday Low Prices" |
| **Niche Focus** | Serve a specific segment better than anyone | You can't compete with giants head-on | ConvertKit: Email marketing for creators only |
| **Category Creation** | Create a new category | Your product doesn't fit existing categories | HubSpot created "Inbound Marketing" as a category |

**Positioning Statement Template:**
"For [target audience] who [need/want], [product name] is a [category] that [key benefit]. Unlike [competitor], we [key differentiator]."

**Real-Time Example:**
"For marketing teams at growing SaaS companies who need to automate their outbound sales, **Outreach.io** is a sales engagement platform that increases pipeline 3x while reducing manual work. Unlike Salesforce (which requires heavy customization), Outreach works out of the box with guided workflows."

### Channel Strategy: Selecting the Right Channels

**Channel Selection Framework:**

| Criterion | Weight | Score (1-5) | Formula |
|---|---|---|---|
| Audience presence | 25% | Is your audience on this channel? | High presence = 5 |
| Competition level | 20% | How crowded is the channel? | Less crowded = 5 |
| Content fit | 20% | Does your content work on this channel? | Strong fit = 5 |
| Cost efficiency | 15% | What's the CPM/CPC relative to budget? | Low cost = 5 |
| Measurement capability | 10% | Can you track ROI? | Full tracking = 5 |
| Internal capability | 10% | Does your team have skills for this channel? | Strong skills = 5 |

**Real-Time Example — Channel Scoring for B2B SaaS:**

| Channel | Audience (25%) | Competition (20%) | Content Fit (20%) | Cost (15%) | Measurement (10%) | Capability (10%) | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| LinkedIn | 5 (1.25) | 3 (0.60) | 5 (1.00) | 2 (0.30) | 4 (0.40) | 4 (0.40) | **3.95** |
| Google Ads | 5 (1.25) | 2 (0.40) | 4 (0.80) | 3 (0.45) | 5 (0.50) | 3 (0.30) | **3.70** |
| Blog/SEO | 5 (1.25) | 3 (0.60) | 5 (1.00) | 5 (0.75) | 4 (0.40) | 4 (0.40) | **4.40** |
| TikTok | 2 (0.50) | 5 (1.00) | 2 (0.40) | 4 (0.60) | 2 (0.20) | 1 (0.10) | **2.80** |
| Email | 4 (1.00) | 3 (0.60) | 5 (1.00) | 5 (0.75) | 5 (0.50) | 5 (0.50) | **4.35** |

## 1.6.3 Budget Planning

### Budget Allocation Methods

| Method | How It Works | Best For | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Percentage of Revenue** | Allocate 5-15% of revenue to marketing | Established companies with predictable revenue | $10M revenue → $1M marketing budget (10%) |
| **Objective-Based** | Budget based on what's needed to achieve goals | Growth-stage companies with clear targets | "We need $500K to generate 2,000 leads at $250/lead" |
| **Competitive Parity** | Match competitor spending | Mature markets with established competition | "Competitors spend $2M/year on digital; we should match" |
| **Zero-Based** | Start from zero every cycle; justify every dollar | Companies needing to optimize spend | Every line item must prove expected ROI |

**Industry benchmarks for marketing budget as % of revenue:**
| Industry | Marketing Budget (% of Revenue) |
|---|---|
| Technology / SaaS | 15-25% |
| Consumer packaged goods | 10-15% |
| Retail / E-commerce | 8-15% |
| Financial services | 7-12% |
| Healthcare | 5-10% |
| Manufacturing / Industrial | 3-7% |

### Fixed Costs vs. Variable Costs

| Cost Type | Examples | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|
| **Fixed** | Tool subscriptions, salaries, agency retainers | Paid regardless of performance |
| **Variable** | Ad spend, freelancer fees, commission-based partnerships | Scales with activity/results |
| **Semi-variable** | Content production (minimum baseline + scaling for campaigns) | Base + variable component |

**Budget planning rule:** Keep fixed costs below 30% of marketing budget. The rest should be variable and performance-linked.

### Agency vs. In-House Cost Comparison

| Factor | In-House | Agency | Freelancer |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Salary + benefits ($60-150K/year per person) | Retainer ($5-50K/month) | Project-based ($50-200/hour) |
| **Speed** | Slow to hire (3-6 months) | Quick to start (2-4 weeks) | Quick (days) |
| **Expertise** | Deep in your industry | Broad across industries | Specialist in one area |
| **Control** | Full control | Managed via briefs/reviews | Project-based control |
| **Scalability** | Hard to scale quickly | Easy to scale up/down | Easy to scale |
| **Risk** | Fixed cost even if performance dips | Can switch agencies | Can switch freelancers |

**When to use each:**
- **In-house:** Core competencies, brand-sensitive work, long-term channel management
- **Agency:** Specialized expertise (PPC, SEO, PR), surge capacity, strategic planning
- **Freelancer:** Specific deliverables (blog posts, ad design), budget-conscious, testing before hiring

**Real-Time Example — Buffer's Hybrid Model:**
- **In-house:** Content strategy, community management, brand voice
- **Agency:** SEO (technical audit), PR (media outreach)
- **Freelancers:** Blog writers (scale content), video editors, graphic designers
- This hybrid approach allows Buffer to maintain brand consistency (in-house) while accessing specialized expertise (agency/freelancer)

### Sprint-Based Marketing (Agile Marketing)

Applying agile development principles to marketing execution.

**The Marketing Sprint:**

```
SPRINT CYCLE (2 weeks)
├── PLANNING (Day 1): Prioritize backlog, assign tasks, define sprint goals
├── EXECUTION (Days 2-9): Create, launch, monitor
├── REVIEW (Day 10): Measure results, gather learnings
└── RETROSPECTIVE (Day 10): What worked? What didn't? What to change?
```

**Agile marketing board (Kanban):**

```
┌─────────────┬────────────────┬────────────────┬────────────────┬─────────────┐
│  BACKLOG    │  TO DO         │  IN PROGRESS   │  IN REVIEW     │  DONE       │
│             │  (This Sprint) │                │                │             │
│ Blog: AI    │ Email: Welcome │ Landing page   │ Blog: SEO      │ Google Ads  │
│ Video: Demo │ Social: 5 posts│ redesign       │ guide review   │ campaign    │
│ Webinar     │ Ad creative x3 │                │                │ launched    │
│ Case study  │                │                │                │             │
└─────────────┴────────────────┴────────────────┴────────────────┴─────────────┘
```

**Real-Time Example — How HubSpot Uses Agile Marketing:**
- 2-week sprints with cross-functional pods (writer + designer + developer + analyst)
- Each pod owns a specific metric (e.g., blog traffic, email conversion)
- Daily 15-min standup via Slack
- End-of-sprint retrospective feeds into next sprint planning
- Result: Doubled content output while improving quality metrics

|---|---|---|---|

## Implementation Steps for Section 1.6

### Step 1: Conduct the Situation Analysis (Week 1-2)
- Complete internal digital audit across all channels
- Run PESTLE and SWOT analyses
- Assess digital maturity level
- **Tools:** GA4, SEMrush, SimilarWeb, Google PageSpeed Insights

### Step 2: Define Audience and Strategy (Week 2-3)
- Finalize buyer personas (use 1.2 research)
- Write value proposition and positioning statement
- Score and select priority channels
- Define 3-5 content pillars
- **Tools:** HubSpot MakeMyPersona, Xtensio, Miro

### Step 3: Set Goals and KPIs (Week 3)
- Align with business leadership on objectives
- Define SMART goals per channel
- Set up KPI tracking dashboard
- **Tools:** Google Sheets, Looker Studio, Databox

### Step 4: Build the Budget (Week 3-4)
- Allocate budget by channel using scoring matrix output
- Create 3 scenario models (conservative, base, aggressive)
- Identify tool/technology requirements and costs
- **Tools:** Google Sheets, financial modeling templates

### Step 5: Create the Execution Roadmap (Week 4)
- Build 90-day plan with weekly milestones
- Create campaign calendar for the first quarter
- Set up agile sprint structure (2-week sprints)
- Assign ownership for every channel and initiative
- **Tools:** Asana, Monday.com, Notion, Trello

### Step 6: Document and Present (Week 4-5)
- Compile the full marketing plan document
- Present to stakeholders for alignment and buy-in
- Get budget and strategy approval
- **Tools:** Google Slides/PowerPoint, Notion, Loom for async presentations

## Gap Analysis for Section 1.6

| Common Gap | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| No documented marketing plan | Teams work in silos, no strategic alignment | Create a shared plan document (even a simple one) |
| Plan exists but isn't followed | Plan was created for leadership, then shelved | Break plan into quarterly sprints with weekly check-ins |
| Budget not tied to goals | Spending money on channels without clear ROI targets | Connect every budget line to a specific SMART goal |
| No situation analysis done | Strategy based on assumptions, not data | Conduct annual external audit + quarterly internal audit |
| Overplanning, underexecuting | Spending 3 months planning, 1 month executing per quarter | Use agile sprints — plan for 2 weeks, execute, iterate |
| No contingency plans | One bad algorithm update or budget cut derails everything | Document top 5 risks with mitigation plans |
| Team structure doesn't match strategy | Investing in video strategy but have no video person | Align hiring/outsourcing to channel strategy |
| No executive alignment | Marketing priorities conflict with business priorities | Quarterly alignment meetings with C-suite |

## Strategies

1. **The 1-Page Marketing Plan:** Distill your full plan into a single page. If you can't explain the strategy in one page, it's too complicated. Use this for team alignment and stakeholder buy-in.
2. **The Sprint-Review-Scale Cycle:** Launch every initiative as a 90-day sprint. Review results. Scale winners, kill losers, iterate on middle-performers.
3. **The 70-20-10 Budget Rule:** 70% on proven channels, 20% on emerging opportunities, 10% on pure experimentation.
4. **The Anti-Fragile Marketing Plan:** Build a plan that gets stronger when things break. Diversify channels, maintain large email list, keep cash reserve for opportunistic spending.
5. **The Growth Equation:** Growth = (Traffic × Conversion Rate × Frequency × AOV) - Costs. Optimize the variable with the most room for improvement first.

## Best Practices

1. **Start with the customer, not the channel.** Build strategy around customer needs, then select channels to reach them.
2. **Document everything.** Plans, processes, results, learnings. Your future self (and future team members) will thank you.
3. **Plan in pencil, not pen.** Marketing plans should be living documents that adapt to market changes.
4. **Prioritize ruthlessly.** You can't do everything. Pick the 3-5 initiatives with highest expected impact and resource them fully.
5. **Measure weekly, optimize monthly, strategize quarterly.** Don't wait a year to assess strategy.
6. **Build in slack time.** Plan for 80% capacity. The remaining 20% handles unexpected opportunities and fires.
7. **Communicate the plan to the entire company.** Everyone from sales to support should understand the marketing strategy.
8. **Review and revise quarterly.** A plan made in January that isn't updated by April is already stale.
9. **Celebrate wins publicly.** When campaigns succeed, share the results company-wide. It builds buy-in for future investments.
10. **Learn from failures systematically.** Every failed campaign should produce a documented learning that prevents the same mistake.

## Additional Insights

### The Marketing Strategy Stack

```
VISION (3-5 year aspirational goal)
    ↓
STRATEGY (1-year plan: audience, positioning, channels)
    ↓
TACTICS (Quarterly: campaigns, content, experiments)
    ↓
EXECUTION (Weekly/daily: tasks, ads, posts, emails)
    ↓
MEASUREMENT (Continuous: KPIs, reports, optimization)
    ↓
LEARNING → feeds back into STRATEGY
```

Most teams jump straight to execution without defining strategy. Strategy without execution is a dream. Execution without strategy is a nightmare.

### The Marketing Compounding Effect

| Channel | Month 1 | Month 6 | Month 12 | Month 24 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Paid Ads** | 1,000 visits | 1,000 visits | 1,000 visits | 1,000 visits |
| **SEO/Content** | 100 visits | 2,000 visits | 10,000 visits | 40,000 visits |
| **Email List** | 200 subs | 3,000 subs | 12,000 subs | 40,000 subs |

Paid is linear. Content and email are exponential. The best marketing plans invest in compounding channels while using paid for immediate results.

### Final Thought: The 3 Laws of Digital Marketing Plans

**Law 1: No plan survives first contact with the market.**
Plan thoroughly, but be ready to pivot. The value of planning is in the thinking, not the document.

**Law 2: The best plan is the one that gets executed.**
A simple plan that your team actually follows beats a brilliant plan that sits in a Google Drive folder.

**Law 3: Plans are hypotheses.**
Every strategic choice is a bet. The best marketers treat their plans as experiments — measure, learn, iterate.

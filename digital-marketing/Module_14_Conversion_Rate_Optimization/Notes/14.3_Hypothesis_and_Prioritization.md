# 14.3 Hypothesis & Prioritization — Comprehensive Notes

---

## 14.3.1 Building Strong Hypotheses

### What Is a CRO Hypothesis?

A CRO hypothesis is a testable statement that predicts how a specific change will impact a measurable outcome, grounded in evidence from research. It transforms research insights into actionable experiments.

A hypothesis is NOT a guess. It is an evidence-backed prediction that connects a proposed change to an expected result with a clear rationale.

---

### The Hypothesis Structure

**Standard Format:**

```
"If we [make this specific change],
then [this measurable outcome will happen],
because [this evidence/insight supports it]."
```

**Extended Format (Recommended):**

```
"Based on [research finding / data point],
we believe that [making this change]
for [this audience/segment]
will cause [this metric to move in this direction]
because [psychological/behavioral reason].
We will measure this by [specific metric and success threshold]."
```

---

### Examples of Strong vs. Weak Hypotheses

#### Weak Hypotheses (Avoid These)

| Weak Hypothesis | Why It's Weak |
|-----------------|---------------|
| "Changing the button color to green will increase conversions" | No rationale, no research backing, no specific metric |
| "We should redesign the homepage" | Not testable, too vague, no expected outcome |
| "Adding more content will help" | No specificity on what content, where, or why |
| "Users will like the new design better" | "Like" is not measurable; no conversion metric |
| "Let's test a video on the landing page" | No reason why a video would help; no audience insight |

#### Strong Hypotheses (Model These)

| Strong Hypothesis | Why It's Strong |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| "Based on exit survey data showing 34% of visitors leave because they cannot find integration information, if we add an integrations section above the fold on the pricing page, then trial sign-ups will increase by 10%, because visitors will have the confidence that our tool works with their existing stack." | Specific evidence, clear change, measurable outcome, behavioral rationale |
| "Session recordings show 45% of mobile users abandon the checkout form at the 'Billing Address' field. If we add autofill/autocomplete for address fields, then mobile checkout completion will increase by 15%, because reducing typing friction on mobile eliminates the primary abandonment trigger." | Quantified evidence, specific page/element, device-specific, clear mechanism |
| "Heatmap data reveals that only 12% of users scroll past the hero section on our landing page. If we move customer testimonials from below the fold to within the hero section, then page-to-sign-up conversion will increase by 8%, because social proof placed where users actually see it will reduce anxiety and increase trust." | Visual data backing, specific placement change, psychological reasoning |

---

### Data-Informed Hypotheses vs. Opinion-Based Guesses

| Data-Informed (DO THIS) | Opinion-Based (AVOID THIS) |
|-------------------------|---------------------------|
| "GA4 shows a 62% drop-off at step 3 of checkout where we ask for phone number. Removing it should reduce drop-off." | "I think the checkout has too many fields." |
| "3 out of 5 usability test participants could not find the search bar on mobile." | "The search bar should be bigger." |
| "Support tickets show 'shipping cost' is the #1 pre-purchase question (28% of tickets)." | "We should probably show shipping costs earlier." |
| "Click heatmap shows 0 clicks on the 'Learn More' link in the sidebar." | "Nobody reads the sidebar content." |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Conversion Rate Experts** worked with **Moz** and built their hypotheses entirely from data. Their research showed that Moz's free trial page listed features but did not communicate the transformation users would experience. Based on user interviews where prospects said "I don't know what Moz will actually do for my business," they hypothesized: "If we replace the feature list with a before/after transformation narrative, trial sign-ups will increase because prospects need to envision the outcome, not just the tool." Result: 52% increase in paying customers, generating $1M+ in additional revenue.

---

### Linking Hypotheses to Research Findings

Every hypothesis should trace back to at least one research source:

| Research Method | Insight Example | Hypothesis |
|----------------|-----------------|------------|
| **GA4 Funnel** | 48% drop-off from product page to add-to-cart | "If we add a sticky 'Add to Cart' button that stays visible on scroll, then add-to-cart rate will increase by 12%..." |
| **Heatmap** | Users clicking on non-clickable product images | "If we make product images clickable with a zoom/gallery feature, then time on page and add-to-cart rate will increase..." |
| **Session Recording** | Users scrolling up and down the pricing page repeatedly | "If we add a feature comparison table with sticky headers, then pricing page-to-trial conversion will increase..." |
| **Exit Survey** | "I wasn't sure about the return policy" (27% of responses) | "If we add return policy information next to the 'Buy Now' button, then purchase conversion will increase..." |
| **User Interview** | "I could not figure out which plan was right for me" | "If we add a 'Help me choose' quiz to the pricing page, then plan selection rate will increase..." |
| **Support Analysis** | "How do I cancel?" is a top question | "If we add a visible 'Cancel anytime' badge near the sign-up CTA, then sign-up rate will increase..." |

### Categorizing Hypotheses

Organize your hypothesis backlog by:

**By Page/Location:**
- Homepage, Product pages, Category pages, Pricing page, Checkout flow, Landing pages, Onboarding

**By Funnel Stage:**
- Awareness (TOFU), Consideration (MOFU), Decision (BOFU), Retention

**By Element Type:**
- Headlines/Copy, CTAs, Forms, Images/Media, Navigation, Layout, Trust elements, Pricing display

**By Optimization Theme:**
- Reducing friction, Increasing motivation, Building trust, Improving clarity, Adding urgency, Enhancing relevance

---

### Hypothesis Documentation and Repository

**Essential Fields for Each Hypothesis:**

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Hypothesis ID** | Unique identifier (e.g., H-2026-042) |
| **Hypothesis Statement** | Full "If/Then/Because" statement |
| **Research Source** | Link to the data/insight that inspired it |
| **Page/URL** | Where the test would run |
| **Funnel Stage** | TOFU / MOFU / BOFU / Retention |
| **Element Type** | CTA, headline, form, layout, etc. |
| **Primary Metric** | The KPI being measured (conversion rate, click-through rate, etc.) |
| **Secondary Metrics** | Other metrics to monitor (bounce rate, AOV, revenue per visitor) |
| **Prioritization Score** | ICE / PIE / RICE score |
| **Status** | Backlog / In Test / Completed / Archived |
| **Test Results** | Win / Loss / Inconclusive with data |
| **Learnings** | What did this test teach us regardless of result? |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Booking.com** maintains a centralized experiment repository with 10,000+ documented tests. Every new team member reviews past experiments in their area before proposing new tests. This prevents redundant testing and builds institutional knowledge that compounds over time.

## 14.3.2 Prioritization Frameworks

### Why Prioritization Matters

Most CRO programs generate 50–200 hypothesis ideas. You can only run 2–8 tests per month. Prioritization ensures you test the ideas most likely to drive significant results first.

Without prioritization:
- Teams waste months testing low-impact ideas
- High-value opportunities sit in the backlog
- Testing velocity is consumed by "pet projects" from leadership
- ROI of the CRO program suffers

---

### ICE Framework: Impact, Confidence, Ease

The simplest and most widely used framework. Score each hypothesis 1–10 on three dimensions.

| Dimension | What It Measures | Scoring Guide |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Impact** | How much will this improve the target metric? | 1 = <1% improvement, 5 = 5–10% improvement, 10 = 20%+ improvement |
| **Confidence** | How confident are we that this will work? | 1 = gut feeling, 5 = some data, 10 = strong evidence from multiple sources |
| **Ease** | How easy is it to implement and test? | 1 = months of dev work, 5 = few days, 10 = can do it in hours with a visual editor |

**Score Calculation:** ICE = (Impact + Confidence + Ease) / 3

**Real-Time Example:**

| Hypothesis | Impact | Confidence | Ease | ICE Score | Priority |
|-----------|--------|------------|------|-----------|----------|
| Add trust badges near checkout CTA | 6 | 7 | 9 | 7.3 | 1st |
| Redesign entire checkout to single page | 9 | 6 | 3 | 6.0 | 3rd |
| Change CTA color from gray to green | 3 | 4 | 10 | 5.7 | 4th |
| Add video testimonials to product pages | 7 | 8 | 5 | 6.7 | 2nd |
| Build a custom product configurator | 8 | 5 | 2 | 5.0 | 5th |

**When to Use ICE:** Early-stage CRO programs, smaller teams, when speed is more important than precision.

**Limitations:** Subjective scoring, no weighting, can be gamed, does not account for reach.

---

### PIE Framework: Potential, Importance, Ease

Developed by WiderFunnel, PIE adds business importance to the equation.

| Dimension | What It Measures | Scoring Guide |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Potential** | How much room for improvement exists? | Based on current performance: poor UX = high potential, already-optimized page = low potential |
| **Importance** | How valuable is this page/element to the business? | Based on traffic volume, revenue contribution, strategic importance |
| **Ease** | How complex is the implementation? | Technical difficulty, design effort, organizational barriers |

**Score Calculation:** PIE = (Potential + Importance + Ease) / 3

**Real-Time Example:**
- **WiderFunnel** used PIE to prioritize testing for **SAP** and identified that their demo request form (highest importance due to revenue per lead, high potential due to low current completion rate) should be prioritized over homepage redesign (lower importance per visit, already performing at industry average).

**When to Use PIE:** When you want to prioritize by business value, not just conversion rate potential. Best when different pages have vastly different business value.

---

### RICE Framework: Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort

Originally from Intercom for product prioritization, adapted for CRO. More rigorous than ICE.

| Dimension | What It Measures | How to Score |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Reach** | How many users will be affected per month? | Actual number (e.g., 10,000 visitors/month to this page) |
| **Impact** | How much will it move the conversion rate? | 0.25 = minimal, 0.5 = low, 1 = medium, 2 = high, 3 = massive |
| **Confidence** | How sure are we of the impact estimate? | 100% = high confidence (strong data), 80% = medium, 50% = low |
| **Effort** | How many person-weeks of work? | Actual estimate (e.g., 2 person-weeks) |

**Score Calculation:** RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) / Effort

**Real-Time Example:**

| Hypothesis | Reach | Impact | Confidence | Effort | RICE Score |
|-----------|-------|--------|------------|--------|------------|
| Simplify checkout form (mobile) | 25,000 | 2 | 80% | 2 weeks | 20,000 |
| Add social proof to pricing page | 15,000 | 1 | 90% | 0.5 weeks | 27,000 |
| Redesign product page layout | 40,000 | 1 | 50% | 4 weeks | 5,000 |
| Add exit-intent popup with offer | 30,000 | 0.5 | 70% | 0.5 weeks | 21,000 |

In this example, adding social proof to the pricing page scores highest because it has strong confidence, low effort, and decent reach — even though checkout simplification might have higher raw impact.

**When to Use RICE:** Medium to large CRO programs with good analytics data. Best when you can accurately estimate reach (traffic data) and effort (dev team estimates).

---

### PXL Framework (WiderFunnel's Evidence-Based Prioritization)

PXL uses binary (yes/no) scoring instead of subjective 1–10 scales, making it more objective.

| Question | Yes/No | Weight |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Is the change above the fold? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×2 |
| Is it on a high-traffic page? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×2 |
| Does it address a known user problem? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×2 |
| Is there analytics data supporting this change? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×1 |
| Is there qualitative data supporting this change? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×1 |
| Is there usability testing data supporting this change? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×1 |
| Is the change easy to implement? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×1 |
| Is the change easy to measure? | Yes = 1, No = 0 | ×1 |

**When to Use PXL:** When team members tend to inflate subjective scores. PXL forces binary decisions backed by evidence.

---

### Opportunity Scoring

Opportunity scoring identifies the biggest gaps between what users want and how well your site currently delivers.

**Method:**
1. Survey users: "How important is [feature/element] to you?" (1–10)
2. Survey users: "How satisfied are you with [feature/element] on our site?" (1–10)
3. **Opportunity Score = Importance + (Importance - Satisfaction)**

High importance + low satisfaction = highest opportunity.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **UserVoice** used opportunity scoring to prioritize their product roadmap and found that "pricing transparency" had an importance score of 9.2 but a satisfaction score of 3.8, giving an opportunity score of 14.6 (the highest). They completely redesigned their pricing page, resulting in a 40% increase in self-serve plan sign-ups.

---

### Building a Prioritized Testing Backlog

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Collect all hypotheses** from research, team brainstorms, support analysis, and competitive research into a single backlog
2. **Score each hypothesis** using your chosen framework (start with ICE if unsure)
3. **Rank by score** from highest to lowest
4. **Validate with stakeholders** — present the top 10 and get alignment
5. **Assign to testing slots** based on testing capacity (tests/month)
6. **Review and re-score monthly** as new data comes in
7. **Archive completed tests** with full documentation

**Backlog Health Metrics:**
- Backlog should have 3–6 months of test ideas at current velocity
- No more than 20% of ideas should come from leadership "requests" without data backing
- At least 50% of hypotheses should have quantitative + qualitative evidence
- Review and prune the backlog quarterly — remove stale ideas that are no longer relevant

---

### Balancing Quick Wins with High-Impact Experiments

**The 70/20/10 Rule:**
- **70%** of testing capacity: High-impact, high-confidence tests on key conversion pages
- **20%** of testing capacity: Strategic experiments (redesigns, new features, radical changes)
- **10%** of testing capacity: Wild-card tests (innovative, unexpected ideas with high risk/reward)

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Netflix** follows a similar balance. Most of their experiments focus on optimizing existing flows (thumbnail selection, content recommendation ranking). But 10–20% of their experiments are radical bets — like testing whether auto-playing trailers would increase engagement (it did — 20% more content discovery).

## Implementation Steps for Hypothesis & Prioritization

### Step 1: Create Your Hypothesis Template
- Design a standardized template with all required fields
- Train the team on writing strong hypotheses using the "If/Then/Because" format
- Set up the tracking system (Notion database, Airtable, or spreadsheet)

### Step 2: Conduct a Hypothesis Sprint
- Block 2–4 hours with the CRO team
- Review all research findings (analytics, heatmaps, surveys, recordings, interviews)
- Generate 15–30 hypotheses as a team
- Write each in full format with evidence links

### Step 3: Score and Prioritize
- Choose a framework (ICE for simplicity, RICE for rigor, PXL for objectivity)
- Score each hypothesis independently (have each team member score, then average)
- Rank by composite score
- Identify the top 5–10 for the next testing cycle

### Step 4: Build the Testing Calendar
- Map prioritized hypotheses to available testing slots
- Account for test duration (typically 2–4 weeks per test)
- Ensure no overlapping tests on the same page/audience
- Schedule design and development prep 1–2 weeks before test launch

### Step 5: Establish Review Cadence
- Weekly: Quick status check on running tests
- Bi-weekly: Score new hypotheses and add to backlog
- Monthly: Full backlog review, reprioritize based on new data
- Quarterly: CRO program performance review with leadership

## Gap Finding for Hypothesis & Prioritization

| Gap | Symptoms | How to Fix |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| **No structured hypothesis process** | Tests are run based on random ideas or leadership whims | Implement the "If/Then/Because" format and require evidence for every test |
| **Subjective prioritization** | The loudest voice in the room decides what to test | Use PXL framework (binary scoring) to remove subjectivity |
| **Stale backlog** | Hypothesis backlog has not been updated in months | Schedule monthly backlog reviews; archive irrelevant ideas |
| **No documentation** | Past test results are lost or poorly recorded | Set up a centralized repository; make documentation part of the test completion checklist |
| **Testing without evidence** | Hypotheses have no research backing | Require at least one data source (quant or qual) for every hypothesis |
| **Imbalanced testing** | Only testing small UI tweaks, never strategic changes | Apply the 70/20/10 rule to ensure a mix of tactical and strategic tests |
| **No cross-functional input** | Only marketing generates hypotheses | Invite product, engineering, sales, and support to hypothesis sprints |

## Best Practices

1. **Evidence first, hypothesis second** — Never create a hypothesis without a research insight to back it
2. **Be specific and measurable** — Vague hypotheses lead to vague results
3. **Include the "because"** — The rationale is what makes a hypothesis learnable; even failed tests teach you something when you understand why you expected it to work
4. **Score independently before discussing** — Prevent groupthink by having each team member score privately first
5. **Do not over-index on ease** — Easy tests are tempting but often have low impact; balance effort with potential value
6. **Revisit failed hypotheses** — A hypothesis that failed might work with a different execution or on a different segment
7. **Track your hit rate** — If less than 25% of tests are winning, your research or hypothesis quality needs improvement
8. **Time-box prioritization** — Do not spend more time scoring than testing; 80% accuracy in prioritization is good enough

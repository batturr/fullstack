## 14.2.1 Quantitative Research

Quantitative research answers the **"what"** — what is happening on your site, where are users dropping off, and what patterns exist in the data.

---

### Google Analytics: Funnel Analysis, Drop-Off, and Segmentation

#### What It Does
Google Analytics (GA4) tracks user behavior across your website: page views, events, conversions, demographics, acquisition channels, and user journeys.

#### Key CRO Applications

**1. Funnel Analysis (GA4 Exploration Reports)**
Build custom funnels to visualize the exact path users take from landing to conversion, and identify where they abandon.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Zalando** (European fashion e-commerce) used GA4 funnel analysis and discovered that 38% of users dropped off between "Add to Cart" and "View Cart." Investigation revealed that the mini-cart overlay was confusing on mobile — users could not find the "Proceed to Checkout" button. After redesigning the mini-cart with a prominent, full-width CTA, the drop-off reduced to 22%, increasing monthly revenue by €2.3M.

**2. Segment Comparison**
Compare conversion rates across segments: new vs. returning users, mobile vs. desktop, organic vs. paid, geographic regions.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Canva** segmented their GA4 data by user type and found that users who watched their onboarding tutorial had a 3.5× higher trial-to-paid conversion rate. They then made the tutorial a mandatory part of the sign-up flow for new users, which increased overall paid conversions by 14%.

**3. Drop-Off Identification**
Identify specific pages where users exit the funnel at unexpectedly high rates.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Set up GA4 property with proper data stream configuration
2. Configure conversion events (purchase, sign_up, generate_lead, etc.)
3. Set up custom dimensions for user properties (plan type, user role, etc.)
4. Build funnel exploration reports for each key conversion path
5. Create audience segments for comparison analysis
6. Set up custom dashboards for weekly CRO monitoring
7. Configure BigQuery export for advanced analysis (if needed)

### Heatmaps: Click Maps, Scroll Maps, and Movement Maps

#### Types of Heatmaps

| Type | What It Shows | CRO Use Case |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **Click Maps** | Where users click on a page | Identify if users are clicking on non-clickable elements, ignoring CTAs, or clicking unexpected areas |
| **Scroll Maps** | How far users scroll down | Determine optimal content placement; are users seeing your CTA below the fold? |
| **Movement Maps** | Where the mouse cursor moves (proxy for eye tracking) | Understand reading patterns and attention areas |
| **Attention Maps** | Combined time + scroll data showing where users spend most time | Identify high-engagement content zones |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Basecamp** used Crazy Egg scroll maps on their homepage and discovered that only 18% of visitors scrolled past the hero section. Their pricing information and customer testimonials — two critical conversion elements — were buried at the bottom. They restructured the page to place social proof and pricing above the fold. Result: 14% increase in sign-up rate.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Fab.com** (design e-commerce) used click heatmaps and found that 42% of users were clicking on product images expecting a zoom feature that did not exist. Adding a click-to-zoom functionality increased product page-to-cart conversion by 23%.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Install the heatmap tool's tracking script on your site (usually via GTM)
2. Configure page-level recording rules (focus on high-traffic, high-value pages)
3. Collect data for a minimum of 1,000–5,000 page views per page for statistical reliability
4. Analyze click maps for dead clicks, rage clicks, and ignored CTAs
5. Review scroll maps to ensure critical content is above the "fold line" (where 50% of users stop scrolling)
6. Compare heatmaps by device type (mobile vs. desktop patterns differ significantly)
7. Document findings and link them to specific hypotheses

### Session Recordings

#### What It Is
Session recordings capture real user sessions as video playback, showing every click, scroll, hover, and form interaction a user makes on your site.

#### What to Look For
- **Rage clicks:** Users clicking the same element repeatedly (frustration signal)
- **Dead clicks:** Users clicking on non-interactive elements (expectation mismatch)
- **U-turns:** Users navigating to a page and immediately going back (content mismatch)
- **Error encounters:** Users hitting error messages or broken functionality
- **Hesitation:** Users hovering over elements for extended periods (confusion signal)
- **Form struggles:** Users clearing and re-typing fields, tabbing between fields repeatedly

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Walmart.com** watched 500+ session recordings of their checkout flow and noticed a recurring pattern: users were repeatedly trying to apply coupon codes but the coupon field was collapsed under an accordion that 70% of users never found. Making the coupon field visible by default reduced checkout abandonment by 4%, translating to an estimated $50M+ in recovered annual revenue.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Install recording tool (Hotjar, FullStory, or Clarity)
2. Set sampling rates (record 10–100% of sessions based on traffic volume)
3. Set up triggers to capture high-value sessions (users who reach checkout, users who abandon)
4. Watch 30–50 recordings per week, focusing on different funnel stages
5. Tag recordings with behavioral patterns (confusion, error, rage click)
6. Compile a report of recurring issues with frequency counts
7. Prioritize issues by frequency × conversion impact

### Form Analytics

#### What It Measures
- **Field-level drop-off:** Which specific form fields cause users to abandon
- **Completion time:** How long users take to fill each field
- **Interaction rate:** Which fields users interact with vs. skip
- **Error rate:** Which fields trigger the most validation errors
- **Refill rate:** Which fields users clear and re-enter (confusion signal)

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Expedia** famously removed a single form field — "Company Name" — from their booking form. Users were confused about what to enter and either abandoned the form or entered incorrect billing information. Removing this one field increased revenue by $12 million per year.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Marketo** analyzed their lead generation form and found that reducing fields from 9 to 5 increased form completions by 34%. However, they also found that adding one qualifying question ("What is your company size?") actually increased the quality of leads so much that sales revenue per lead increased by 55%, more than offsetting the slightly lower volume.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Set up form analytics tracking (Hotjar Forms, Zuko, or Formisimo)
2. Identify your highest-volume forms (sign-up, checkout, lead gen, contact)
3. Collect data for a minimum of 500 form interactions per form
4. Analyze field-level drop-off: which fields lose the most users?
5. Review error rates: which fields cause the most validation failures?
6. Measure completion time per field to identify friction points
7. Cross-reference with session recordings of form interactions
8. Build hypotheses for form optimization tests

### Page-Level Metrics

| Metric | Definition | CRO Significance |
|--------|-----------|------------------|
| **Bounce Rate** | % of single-page sessions (GA4: sessions with no engagement) | High bounce = irrelevant content, poor UX, or slow loading |
| **Exit Rate** | % of page views where that page was the last in the session | High exit on checkout page = conversion barrier |
| **Time on Page** | Average time users spend on a page | Very low = content not engaging; Very high on form pages = friction |
| **Engagement Rate** | % of sessions that were engaged (GA4: >10s, 2+ pages, or conversion) | Better than bounce rate for understanding true engagement |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Buffer** noticed their pricing page had a 72% bounce rate — users were landing, viewing pricing, and leaving immediately. Qualitative research revealed that users found the pricing confusing (too many tiers, unclear feature differences). They simplified from 5 tiers to 3 with clearer feature comparison, reducing bounce to 48% and increasing trial sign-ups by 16%.

## 14.2.2 Qualitative Research

Qualitative research answers the **"why"** — why users behave a certain way, what frustrates them, and what would make them convert.

---

### User Surveys

#### Types of On-Site Surveys

| Survey Type | When to Use | Example Question |
|-------------|------------|------------------|
| **On-page poll** | While user is browsing a specific page | "What information are you looking for on this page?" |
| **Exit survey** | When user shows exit intent (mouse leaves viewport) | "What stopped you from completing your purchase today?" |
| **Post-purchase survey** | Immediately after conversion | "What almost stopped you from buying?" |
| **NPS survey** | After using product/service | "How likely are you to recommend us? (0–10)" |
| **CSAT survey** | After a specific interaction | "How satisfied were you with the checkout experience?" |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Groove** (helpdesk SaaS) added a simple on-page exit survey on their pricing page: "What's holding you back from starting a trial?" The top 3 responses were: (1) "I'm not sure it integrates with my tools" — 34%, (2) "Pricing is confusing" — 28%, (3) "I need to check with my team" — 22%. They added an integration page link, simplified pricing, and added a "Share with your team" feature. Trial sign-ups increased by 23%.

#### Survey Best Practices
- Keep surveys to 1–3 questions maximum
- Use open-ended questions for discovery, closed-ended for validation
- Trigger surveys based on behavior (time on page, scroll depth, exit intent), not randomly
- Offer incentives for longer surveys (but not for on-page polls)
- Minimum 100 responses for reliable qualitative themes

---

### User Interviews

#### Structure for CRO-Focused Interviews

**Opening (5 min):** Build rapport, explain purpose, get consent for recording
**Context (10 min):** Understand their role, goals, and how they found your site/product
**Task Walk-through (15 min):** Ask them to complete a task while narrating their thoughts
**Deep Dive (15 min):** Probe specific friction points, confusing elements, unmet expectations
**Closing (5 min):** Ask what one thing they would change

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Slack** conducted 30 user interviews during their early growth phase and discovered a critical insight: teams that sent 2,000+ messages in their first month had a 93% retention rate, while teams below 500 messages churned within 60 days. This insight shaped their entire onboarding CRO strategy — they optimized every touchpoint to drive teams past the 2,000-message threshold.

#### Key Interview Questions for CRO
1. "Walk me through the last time you tried to [complete goal] on our site."
2. "What was the most confusing part of the experience?"
3. "At any point, did you consider leaving? What almost made you leave?"
4. "What would have made this experience better?"
5. "What were you looking for that you could not find?"
6. "How does this compare to [competitor]?"

### Customer Support Analysis

Mining customer support tickets, live chat logs, and phone call transcripts reveals the most common friction points in your conversion funnel.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Zappos** analyzed 10,000 customer service calls and found that the #1 pre-purchase question was about return policies. Despite having a legendary 365-day return policy, it was buried in the footer. They moved the return policy to the product page (right next to the "Add to Cart" button) and added a banner: "Free Returns — 365 Days, No Questions Asked." Conversion rate increased by 6%.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Export 3–6 months of support tickets into a spreadsheet
2. Categorize tickets by topic: billing, product confusion, technical issues, feature requests, shipping
3. Identify the top 10 most frequent pre-purchase questions
4. Map questions to specific pages/steps in the conversion funnel
5. Create FAQ sections, tooltips, or content that proactively answers these questions
6. Track whether addressing top questions reduces support volume AND increases conversions

## 14.2.3 Usability Testing

---

### Moderated Usability Testing

A facilitator guides the participant through tasks in real-time, asking follow-up questions and probing for deeper insights.

**When to Use:** Complex flows (checkout, onboarding), new feature evaluation, early-stage designs

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Intuit (TurboTax)** conducts moderated usability tests with 15–20 participants before every tax season launch. In one session, they discovered that users did not understand the difference between "Standard Deduction" and "Itemized Deduction." Adding a simple comparison table with a recommendation engine ("Based on your situation, Standard Deduction saves you more") reduced time-to-completion by 23% and increased filing completion rates by 8%.

#### Implementation Steps
1. Define 3–5 key tasks you want users to complete (e.g., "Find a product and add it to cart," "Complete checkout")
2. Recruit 5–8 participants who match your target audience (5 users find 80% of usability issues — Nielsen Norman Group)
3. Prepare a test script with task instructions and probing questions
4. Set up screen recording and audio capture
5. Conduct sessions (45–60 minutes each)
6. Synthesize findings: group observations into themes
7. Prioritize issues by severity (critical, major, minor, cosmetic)

---

### Unmoderated Usability Testing

Participants complete tasks independently without a facilitator, using a testing platform that records their screen and audio.

**Advantages:** Faster, cheaper, larger sample sizes, more natural behavior (no observer effect)

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Spotify** uses Maze for unmoderated testing of new UI features. Before launching their "Blend" playlist feature, they tested the discovery flow with 200 users across 5 countries. They found that 60% of users could not find the Blend feature in the menu. This led to a redesign that placed Blend prominently on the social tab, increasing feature adoption by 3×.

### Five-Second Tests

Show a design or page to a participant for exactly 5 seconds, then ask what they remember. This measures:
- **First impression clarity:** Can users understand what the page is about?
- **Value proposition recall:** Do users remember the key benefit?
- **Visual hierarchy effectiveness:** What elements are most memorable?

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Basecamp** ran five-second tests on their homepage and found that only 32% of participants could correctly identify what the product does. They rewrote the headline from "The All-In-One Toolkit for Working Remotely" to "Basecamp is the project management tool that helps teams get more done with less chaos." Five-second recall of the product's purpose jumped to 78%, and homepage-to-trial conversion increased by 11%.

---

### Card Sorting and Tree Testing

**Card Sorting:** Users organize topics into groups that make sense to them. This informs information architecture and navigation design.

**Tree Testing:** Users navigate a text-only site structure to find specific information. This tests whether your navigation labels and hierarchy are intuitive.

**Real-Time Example:**
- **IKEA** used card sorting with 150 participants to restructure their online product categories. They discovered that users thought of furniture by room ("living room," "bedroom") rather than by type ("sofas," "tables"). Restructuring navigation by room increased product discovery metrics by 22% and reduced time-to-first-purchase by 15%.

### Heuristic Evaluation Frameworks

#### Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics (Applied to CRO)

| Heuristic | CRO Application | Example Violation |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Visibility of system status | Show progress bars in checkout, loading indicators | No feedback after form submission — user clicks "Submit" multiple times |
| Match between system and real world | Use language your audience understands, not jargon | SaaS sign-up asks for "Organization Slug" instead of "Company Name" |
| User control and freedom | Easy undo, clear back navigation, simple cancellation | No way to edit cart items without starting over |
| Consistency and standards | Follow platform conventions (blue underlined links, standard icons) | Checkout button is gray and looks disabled |
| Error prevention | Prevent errors before they happen (input masks, confirmation dialogs) | No address validation leads to failed deliveries |
| Recognition rather than recall | Show recent searches, saved items, contextual options | Users must remember product codes instead of seeing product thumbnails |
| Flexibility and efficiency | Shortcuts for power users, saved preferences | No option to save payment methods for repeat purchases |
| Aesthetic and minimalist design | Remove unnecessary elements that distract from conversion | Homepage has 12 competing CTAs and 4 pop-ups |
| Help users recognize, diagnose errors | Clear, specific error messages with solutions | "Error 422" instead of "Your password must include a number" |
| Help and documentation | Searchable FAQ, contextual help tooltips | No help available during checkout; user must leave to find FAQ |

#### LIFT Model

| Factor | Description | Optimization Focus |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Value Proposition** | The perceived benefit of converting | Is the offer compelling? Does the visitor understand what they get? |
| **Clarity** | How easy is it to understand the page and offer | Clear headlines, logical layout, intuitive navigation |
| **Relevance** | Message-to-market match | Does the page match the ad/email/link that brought the user here? |
| **Urgency** | Motivation to act now | Limited time offers, countdown timers, seasonal relevance |
| **Anxiety** | Concerns that prevent conversion | Security fears, privacy concerns, hidden costs, commitment phobia |
| **Distraction** | Elements that pull attention away from conversion | Competing CTAs, unnecessary links, visual clutter |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Humana** (health insurance) used the LIFT model to audit their Medicare landing pages. They found: low urgency (no enrollment deadline prominently displayed), high anxiety (no security badges near the form), and poor clarity (medical jargon in headlines). After addressing all three: adding an enrollment countdown, placing trust badges near the form, and rewriting headlines in plain language, landing page conversion increased by 433%.

### Expert Walkthrough of Key Conversion Paths

#### How to Conduct an Expert Walkthrough

1. **Define the path:** Map the exact steps from entry (ad click, organic search) to conversion
2. **Walk each step:** Go through every page/screen as a first-time user would
3. **Score each step** against the LIFT model or Nielsen's heuristics
4. **Document friction points** with screenshots and severity ratings
5. **Compare across devices** — desktop, mobile, tablet
6. **Compare across browsers** — Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge
7. **Compile findings** into a prioritized action list

### Cognitive Walkthrough Methodology

A cognitive walkthrough evaluates the learnability of a system by stepping through tasks from the user's perspective and asking four questions at each step:

1. Will the user try to achieve the right effect? (Do they understand the goal?)
2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available? (Is the CTA visible?)
3. Will the user associate the correct action with the effect? (Does the label make sense?)
4. If the correct action is performed, will the user see progress? (Is there feedback?)

**Real-Time Example:**
- **LinkedIn** used cognitive walkthroughs to optimize their premium upsell flow. They found that users did not associate the "Try Premium" button with getting InMail credits (the most desired feature). Changing the CTA from "Try Premium" to "Get 5 Free InMails + Premium Features" increased trial starts by 32%.

## Gap Finding for CRO Research

| Gap | Symptoms | How to Identify | Action |
|-----|----------|----------------|--------|
| **Data blindness** | No heatmaps, no recordings, relying solely on GA4 | Audit your tool stack | Install Hotjar or Clarity immediately |
| **Qualitative vacuum** | Lots of numbers but no understanding of "why" | Check if you have any survey or interview data | Run 5 user interviews + deploy an on-site poll |
| **Mobile neglect** | Only reviewing desktop experiences | Compare device-specific conversion rates | Conduct mobile-specific heatmap and recording review |
| **Competitor ignorance** | Never audited competitor flows | Check if you can describe competitors' checkout or sign-up experience | Conduct a competitive analysis sprint |
| **Support disconnection** | Support team insights never reach marketing/product | Ask support team when last they shared insights | Set up monthly support-marketing sync meeting |
| **Technical blind spots** | No Core Web Vitals monitoring | Check PageSpeed Insights for key pages | Set up automated performance monitoring |

## Research Best Practices

1. **Triangulate data sources** — Never rely on a single research method; combine quant + qual + usability
2. **Document everything** — Create a centralized research repository (Notion, Dovetail, or Airtable)
3. **Recruit representative participants** — Usability testing with your actual target audience, not colleagues
4. **Separate observation from interpretation** — Record what happened, then interpret why
5. **Maintain a "voice of customer" repository** — Continuously collect and categorize user quotes and feedback
6. **Research before every test** — Never start A/B testing without understanding the problem first
7. **Make research ongoing, not one-off** — Set up always-on surveys and monthly recording reviews
8. **Share research across teams** — Monthly "insights lunch" where research findings are shared with sales, product, and leadership

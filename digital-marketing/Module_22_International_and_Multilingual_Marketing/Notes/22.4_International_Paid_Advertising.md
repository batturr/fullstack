## 22.4.1 Global PPC Strategy

### The Fundamental Challenge

International PPC is not "domestic PPC in another language." Everything changes: auction dynamics, competitive landscape, user behavior, regulations, currency, and cultural expectations. A Google Ads campaign that converts at 5% in the US might convert at 0.5% in Japan with the exact same approach — not because the product is wrong, but because the strategy is wrong.

### Account Structure for Multiple Markets

There are three main approaches to structuring multi-market Google Ads accounts:

| Structure | How It Works | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Single account, multi-market** | One Google Ads account with campaigns segmented by country | Simple to manage, shared learnings, easy budget reallocation | Limited currency flexibility, hard to give local teams access, performance data blurred | Small businesses testing 2–3 markets |
| **One account per market** | Separate Google Ads accounts per country under one MCC (Manager Account) | Clean data separation, local currency billing, local team access | More accounts to manage, harder to share audiences across markets | Mid-size businesses with 3–10 markets |
| **MCC hierarchy** | Manager account → regional sub-managers → country accounts | Scalable, role-based access, regional oversight | Complex setup, requires governance framework | Enterprise with 10+ markets |

**Recommended structure for most businesses:**

```
MCC (Global Manager Account)
├── Google Ads Account: US (USD billing)
│   ├── Campaign: Brand US
│   ├── Campaign: Non-Brand US
│   └── Campaign: Remarketing US
├── Google Ads Account: UK (GBP billing)
│   ├── Campaign: Brand UK
│   ├── Campaign: Non-Brand UK
│   └── Campaign: Remarketing UK
├── Google Ads Account: Germany (EUR billing)
│   ├── Campaign: Brand DE
│   ├── Campaign: Non-Brand DE
│   └── Campaign: Remarketing DE
└── Google Ads Account: Japan (JPY billing)
    ├── Campaign: Brand JP
    ├── Campaign: Non-Brand JP
    └── Campaign: Remarketing JP
```

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Booking.com** operates separate Google Ads accounts for 200+ countries/territories under a massive MCC structure. Each account has local language ads, local currency bidding, and locally optimized landing pages. They spend an estimated $4B+ annually on Google Ads globally, making them one of the largest advertisers on Earth.

### Budget Allocation Across Markets

**The Portfolio Approach:**
Do NOT split budget equally across markets. Allocate based on opportunity and performance.

| Allocation Method | How It Works | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Market size proportional** | Allocate based on TAM/SAM per market | Initial allocation when you have no performance data |
| **Performance-based** | Shift budget to highest-performing markets (lowest CPA, highest ROAS) | After 3+ months of data per market |
| **Opportunity-based** | Invest more in markets with low competition and high potential | Testing new markets with high growth potential |
| **Hybrid** | 60% performance-based, 20% opportunity-based, 20% experimental | Recommended for most — balances growth and efficiency |

**Budget Allocation Framework:**

| Market Maturity | % of International Budget | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Established markets** (proven ROAS, 12+ months data) | 50–60% | Maximize ROI from known winners |
| **Growing markets** (6–12 months data, positive trend) | 25–30% | Scale what's working |
| **Test markets** (0–6 months data, validating) | 10–15% | Learn quickly, decide whether to invest or exit |
| **Experimental** (new markets being explored) | 5–10% | $3K–$10K per market for initial demand testing |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Wise (TransferWise)** allocates PPC budget using a sophisticated model that factors in: corridor opportunity (US→India vs. UK→Australia money transfer volume), local CPC costs, conversion rates by corridor, and customer lifetime value by market. They discovered that marketing spend in India had 3x the payback period of the UK, but 5x the long-term LTV — so they shifted budget toward India despite short-term metrics looking worse.

### Currency and Bid Management

| Challenge | Solution |
|---|---|
| **Exchange rate fluctuations** | Set budgets in local currency per account. Review monthly for macro changes |
| **CPC varies wildly by market** | US CPC for "insurance" = $50+. India CPC for equivalent = $0.50. Budget accordingly |
| **Conversion value in different currencies** | Normalize all reporting to a base currency (usually USD) for comparison |
| **Bidding strategy per market** | Markets with enough data: automated bidding (Target CPA/ROAS). New markets: manual CPC to control spend and learn |
| **Seasonal CPC spikes** | Ramadan in Middle East, Diwali in India, Golden Week in Japan — CPCs spike. Budget for it |

### Ad Copy Localization and Cultural Adaptation

| Element | What to Localize | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Headlines** | Value proposition framed for local culture | US: "Save 50% Today!" → Japan: "多くのお客様にご愛用いただいています" (Trusted by many customers) — Japanese respond to social proof more than discounts |
| **Description lines** | Benefits, trust signals, cultural references | Germany: Include "TÜV-geprüft" (TÜV-certified) or "Testsieger" (test winner) — Germans trust third-party certifications |
| **CTAs** | Urgency levels, formality | US: "Buy Now!" → Germany: "Jetzt bestellen" (Order Now — more formal). Japan: polite/humble language is essential |
| **Display URLs** | Path text in local language | example.com/de/angebot (not example.com/de/offer) |
| **Ad extensions** | Local phone numbers, local addresses, local reviews | Use local phone format, show local office address, display local review ratings |
| **Numbers and symbols** | Currency placement, number formatting | US: $1,000.00 → Germany: 1.000,00 € → Japan: ¥100,000 |

**The Three-Layer Localization Rule for Ads:**
1. **Language:** Translate the words (minimum viable)
2. **Culture:** Adapt the messaging to cultural values (good)
3. **Market:** Tailor to local competitive positioning and consumer behavior (excellent)

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Airbnb** adapts their Google Ads not just linguistically but culturally:
  - US ad: "Find unique places to stay" (adventure, discovery)
  - Japan ad: "安心・安全な宿泊先" (Safe and secure accommodation) — Japanese travelers prioritize safety and reliability over uniqueness
  - Germany ad: "Ferienwohnungen zu fairen Preisen" (Holiday apartments at fair prices) — Germans value value-for-money messaging

### Landing Page Localization for Paid Campaigns

Sending paid traffic to a non-localized page is burning money. Literally.

| Landing Page Element | Must Localize | Common Mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Headline** | Yes — must match ad copy promise | Ad in German, landing page headline in English |
| **Body copy** | Yes | Machine-translated body text that reads unnaturally |
| **CTA button** | Yes | English CTA on a German page |
| **Social proof** | Yes — local testimonials, local logos, local numbers | Showing US customer logos to Japanese visitors |
| **Images** | Yes — local models, local context | American stock photos for Indian audience |
| **Form fields** | Yes — name format, phone format, address format | Requiring US ZIP code from German customers |
| **Payment options** | Yes — show local methods | Credit card only for Dutch customers (they use iDEAL) |
| **Legal/compliance** | Yes — local privacy policy, cookie consent | Missing GDPR cookie banner for EU visitors |
| **Page load speed** | Yes — CDN with local PoPs | Page loads in 8 seconds because server is in the US |
| **Currency** | Yes — display in local currency | Showing USD to Indian visitors |

### Local Regulations for Ad Content

| Region | Regulation | Impact on Ads |
|---|---|---|
| **EU** | GDPR (data), DSA (digital services), unfair advertising laws | Must include data consent, transparent pricing, no misleading claims |
| **Germany** | Strict comparative advertising laws (UWG) | Cannot directly name competitors in ads without factual comparison basis |
| **France** | Loi Toubon — French language required in advertising | All ad copy must be in French (with limited exceptions for brand names) |
| **China** | Advertising Law (2015, updated 2023) | Cannot use superlatives ("best," "number one"), cannot use national flags/emblems, strict health claims rules |
| **Middle East** | Content restrictions on alcohol, gambling, dating, LGBTQ | These categories cannot be advertised at all in many markets |
| **India** | ASCI guidelines, tobacco/alcohol ad bans | No tobacco ads, surrogate ads restricted, influencer disclosure required |
| **Australia** | ACCC fair advertising, therapeutic goods advertising | Health/pharmaceutical claims heavily regulated |
| **Japan** | Act Against Unjustifiable Premiums and Misleading Representations | Very strict about misleading representations — fines are severe |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Google Ads rejected 5.2 billion ads** in 2023 for policy violations globally. A significant portion were international campaigns that violated local regulations the advertisers weren't aware of. In China, using "最好" (best/finest) in ad copy is literally illegal — a global campaign using "World's Best [Product]" would be rejected instantly.

### Time Zone and Scheduling Considerations

| Scenario | Challenge | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Managing ads across 5+ time zones** | Peak hours differ (9 AM Tokyo ≠ 9 AM London) | Set ad schedules per campaign in local market time |
| **B2B campaigns** | Business hours vary by country/culture | Monday–Friday in West; Sunday–Thursday in Middle East; Saturday working day in many Asian countries |
| **Real-time bid management** | Can't manually optimize at 3 AM your time | Automated bidding handles this; Smart Bidding adjusts by time of day |
| **Reporting cadence** | Team meetings across time zones | Use asynchronous reporting; dashboards updated in real-time, reviews async |
| **Seasonal time shifts** | DST changes vary by country | Update ad schedules when DST changes; some countries (Japan, India, China) don't use DST |

## 22.4.2 International Social Media

### Platform Popularity by Country

This is NOT optional knowledge — it's essential. Posting on Facebook for a Korean audience or Instagram for a Chinese audience is wasted spend.

| Country/Region | Primary Platforms | Secondary Platforms | Dead/Blocked |
|---|---|---|---|
| **US** | Instagram, YouTube, TikTok, Facebook | X (Twitter), LinkedIn, Snapchat, Reddit | — |
| **UK** | Instagram, YouTube, TikTok, Facebook | X, LinkedIn, Snapchat | — |
| **Germany** | Instagram, YouTube, WhatsApp, LinkedIn | TikTok, Xing (LinkedIn competitor) | Facebook declining with under-30s |
| **China** | WeChat, Douyin (TikTok), RED (Xiaohongshu), Weibo | Bilibili, Kuaishou | Facebook, Instagram, YouTube, X, Google — ALL BLOCKED |
| **Japan** | LINE, X (Twitter — uniquely popular), YouTube, Instagram | TikTok | Facebook is minor |
| **South Korea** | KakaoTalk, Naver Blog, Instagram, YouTube | TikTok, X | Facebook is minor |
| **India** | WhatsApp, YouTube, Instagram | Facebook, LinkedIn, ShareChat, Moj | TikTok BANNED since 2020 |
| **Brazil** | WhatsApp, Instagram, YouTube, TikTok | Facebook, X | — |
| **Russia/CIS** | VK (VKontakte), Telegram, YouTube | Odnoklassniki (OK) | Instagram, Facebook — BLOCKED since 2022 |
| **Middle East** | WhatsApp, Instagram, Snapchat, TikTok | X, YouTube | Content restrictions on dating/alcohol platforms |
| **Southeast Asia** | TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Facebook | LINE (Thailand), Grab | — |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **X (Twitter) in Japan** is a unique phenomenon. While X usage has declined in the US and Europe, Japan has the second-largest X user base globally (67M users). Japanese users treat X as their primary social platform — for news, product discovery, and customer service. A brand ignoring X in Japan is like ignoring Instagram in the US.

### Social Media Culture Differences by Region

| Dimension | Western (US/UK) | East Asian (Japan/Korea) | Middle Eastern | Latin American |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tone** | Casual, humorous, bold | Polite, aesthetic, subtle | Respectful, family-oriented | Warm, emotional, expressive |
| **Engagement style** | Comments, shares, debate | Likes, saves, quiet engagement | DMs, private shares | Comments, stories, live video |
| **Brand voice** | Personality-driven, sometimes edgy | Kawaii (cute), professional, minimal | Trust-focused, community | Fun, relatable, passionate |
| **Content type** | Short-form video, memes, UGC | Aesthetic photos, character-based, tutorial | Storytelling, family values | Music, dance, celebration |
| **Response to negativity** | Public responses, transparency | Private resolution, avoid public conflict | Reputation protection, private | Emotional responses, community defense |
| **Influencer culture** | Mega and micro influencers | KOLs (Key Opinion Leaders), VTubers | Family influencers, religious figures | Micro influencers, celebrity |
| **Privacy** | Relatively open | Very private; anonymous accounts common | Privacy-conscious, gender-specific norms | Open, personal |

### Influencer Marketing in Different Markets

| Market | Influencer Landscape | Platform | Typical Cost (per post) | Key Consideration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **US** | Mega ($50K–$1M+), Macro ($5K–$50K), Micro ($500–$5K), Nano ($50–$500) | Instagram, YouTube, TikTok | Varies widely | FTC disclosure rules, saturated market |
| **China** | KOLs and KOCs (Key Opinion Consumers), live-stream selling | Douyin, RED, WeChat, Taobao Live | KOLs: $10K–$500K+, KOCs: $50–$500 | Live commerce is massive — KOLs sell $100M+ in single streams |
| **Japan** | Tarento (TV celebrities), YouTubers, VTubers | YouTube, X, LINE | $5K–$100K | VTubers (virtual influencers) are mainstream — not a novelty |
| **India** | Bollywood celebrities, regional language influencers, micro-creators | Instagram, YouTube | $100–$50K | Regional language influencers (Tamil, Telugu, Hindi) have massive reach |
| **South Korea** | K-pop idol partnerships, beauty/fashion influencers | Instagram, YouTube, Naver | $2K–$200K | K-pop fan communities are incredibly powerful marketing channels |
| **Brazil** | YouTubers, Instagram personalities, TikTokers | Instagram, YouTube, TikTok | $500–$50K | Brazilian influencers are among the most engaged globally |
| **Middle East** | Lifestyle influencers, family influencers | Instagram, Snapchat, TikTok | $1K–$100K | Gender norms impact influencer selection and content |

**Real-Time Example:**
- **Live commerce in China** is an entirely different category of influencer marketing. Li Jiaqi (Austin Li), known as the "Lipstick King," sold $1.7 billion worth of products in a single 12-hour live stream on Alibaba's Singles' Day in 2023. He tests lipsticks on his own lips, gives honest reviews, and offers exclusive deals. This format doesn't exist at scale in Western markets — brands entering China must understand live commerce or they're invisible.

## Implementation Steps

1. **Audit your current paid media performance domestically** — Establish benchmarks for CPC, CPA, ROAS, conversion rates. These become your comparison points.
2. **Select 2–3 target markets for testing** — Use market scoring from Section 22.1. Don't spread budget across 10 markets.
3. **Set up proper account structure** — Separate accounts per market under MCC for mid-size+. Single account with country campaigns for small tests.
4. **Conduct local keyword/audience research** — With native speakers. Not translated from English.
5. **Create locally adapted ad creative** — Three layers: language, culture, market positioning. Get native speaker review before launch.
6. **Build localized landing pages** — Match the ad promise. Local currency, local testimonials, local payment, local imagery.
7. **Set budgets and bidding** — Start conservative. Manual CPC for new markets until you have data. Switch to automated after 50+ conversions.
8. **Identify and set up market-specific platforms** — WeChat for China, LINE for Japan, Naver for Korea. These require separate strategies and often separate agencies.
9. **Launch with 6–8 week test periods** — Enough time for statistical significance. Weekly optimization, not daily.
10. **Analyze, compare, and reallocate** — After test period, compare performance across markets. Shift budget to winners. Exit or iterate on underperformers.
11. **Scale with local social media strategy** — Choose platforms based on where your audience actually is, not where you're comfortable operating.
12. **Build a global reporting dashboard** — Currency-normalized, market-comparable KPIs. Weekly automated reports.

## Gap Analysis

| Common Gap | Symptom | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Machine-translated ad copy** | Low CTR, high bounce rate on landing pages | Invest in human ad copy creation per market |
| **Single global campaign** | One set of ads pushed to all markets | Separate campaigns per market with local creative |
| **Ignoring market-specific platforms** | Zero presence on WeChat (China), LINE (Japan), Naver (Korea) | Platform-specific strategies with local expertise |
| **No localized landing pages** | High CPC but terrible conversion rates in international markets | Build landing pages that match local expectations |
| **Equal budget allocation** | Overspending in low-ROI markets, underspending in high-ROI markets | Performance-based budget allocation after initial test period |
| **Ignoring local regulations** | Ads rejected, accounts suspended, legal issues | Legal review of ad content per market before launch |
| **Domestic bidding strategy applied globally** | Automated bidding fails with too little data in new markets | Manual CPC in new markets; automated only after 50+ conversions |
| **Ignoring social commerce** | Missing revenue from platforms where users buy directly | Integrate social commerce capabilities per market (TikTok Shop, WeChat Mini Programs) |

| **Live commerce integration** | Revenue channel in Asia-Pacific markets | High | E-commerce brands targeting China, SE Asia |
| **Local influencer partnerships** | Trust and awareness in new markets | Medium | All consumer brands entering new markets |

## Best Practices

1. **Never launch international ads with translated copy.** Hire a local copywriter. Even one poorly worded ad erodes trust in your brand.
2. **Always separate accounts per market.** Mixing markets in one account pollutes your data, prevents proper bidding, and makes optimization nearly impossible.
3. **Start with manual bidding in new markets.** Automated bidding needs data. In a new market, you have no data. Manual CPC gives you control while you learn.
4. **Match your landing page to your ad — fully localized.** If the ad is in German, the landing page must be in German. With German pricing, German testimonials, German payment methods. No exceptions.
5. **Budget for platform diversity.** Google Ads is not enough in China, Japan, Korea, or Russia. Budget for platform-specific campaigns.
6. **Respect local advertising regulations.** Get a legal review per market before launching. A rejected ad is wasted money. A legal violation is worse.
7. **Don't judge international performance by domestic benchmarks in month one.** New markets take time. Set separate KPIs: awareness metrics first, conversion metrics later.
8. **Invest in local creative assets.** Stock photos from the US don't work in India. Local photography and videography is one of the highest-ROI investments in international marketing.
9. **Monitor brand safety across cultures.** A placement that's perfectly safe in the US might be next to controversial content in another market. Adjust brand safety settings per market.
10. **Build relationships with local platform representatives.** In China, Korea, and Japan, platform reps can unlock features, provide insights, and resolve issues faster than any support ticket.

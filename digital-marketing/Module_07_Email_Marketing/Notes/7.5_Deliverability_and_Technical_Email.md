## 7.5.1 Email Deliverability Fundamentals

### What Is Deliverability vs. Delivery Rate

These are two different metrics that marketers frequently confuse:

| Term | Definition | What It Tells You |
|---|---|---|
| **Delivery rate** | % of emails accepted by the receiving mail server (not bounced) | Whether the server accepted it (doesn't mean inbox) |
| **Deliverability (Inbox placement rate)** | % of delivered emails that land in the inbox (not spam/promotions) | Whether the subscriber actually sees it |

**The Critical Difference:**
- An email can be "delivered" (server accepted it) but placed in the spam folder → subscriber never sees it
- You could have a 98% delivery rate but only 60% inbox placement, meaning 38% of your emails are going to spam/junk
- Delivery rate is what your ESP reports; inbox placement requires separate testing tools

**Real-Time Example — The Hidden Deliverability Crisis:**
- A mid-size e-commerce brand had a 97% delivery rate (looked great in reports)
- Ran inbox placement tests using GlockApps and discovered only 72% landed in inbox
- 25% were going to Gmail Promotions tab, 3% to spam
- After fixing authentication and engagement issues, inbox placement improved to 91%
- Email revenue increased by 28% with zero change in content or list size — just better inbox placement

```

├── ENGAGEMENT SIGNALS (30%)
│   ├── Open rate patterns

│   ├── HTML quality

    ├── SPF, DKIM, DMARC

```

### Sender Reputation: Domain vs. IP

| Type | What It Is | Who Controls It | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Domain reputation** | Reputation tied to your sending domain (yourbrand.com) | You (through sending practices) | High — travels with you if you change ESPs |
| **IP reputation** | Reputation tied to the IP address sending the email | ESP (shared IP) or You (dedicated IP) | Medium-High — affected by all senders on shared IPs |

**Key Insight:** Domain reputation has become MORE important than IP reputation since 2020. Google and Microsoft now weigh domain reputation more heavily, meaning you can't fix deliverability just by switching to a clean IP.

### Warming Up New Domains and IPs

When you start sending from a new domain or IP, ISPs don't trust you. You must "warm up" by gradually increasing volume.

**IP/Domain Warm-Up Schedule:**

| Week | Daily Volume | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 50-100 | Send only to your most engaged subscribers |
| 2 | 200-500 | Expand to engaged subscribers |
| 3 | 500-1,000 | Include moderately engaged |
| 4 | 1,000-5,000 | Monitor bounce rates and complaints closely |
| 5 | 5,000-10,000 | Check Google Postmaster for reputation status |
| 6 | 10,000-25,000 | Continue monitoring; adjust pace if issues |
| 7 | 25,000-50,000 | Should be near full volume |
| 8+ | Full volume | Maintain consistent sending patterns |

**Rules During Warm-Up:**
- Send to your most engaged subscribers first (highest open/click rates)
- Maintain consistent daily volume (don't skip days or spike volume)
- Monitor bounce rates daily — pause if hard bounces exceed 2%
- Monitor complaint rates — pause if complaints exceed 0.1%
- Authenticate fully before sending the first email (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)

**Real-Time Example — Warm-Up Failure:**
- A startup launched their email program by blasting 50,000 emails on day one from a brand-new domain
- Result: Gmail blacklisted the domain within 24 hours
- It took 6 weeks of slow warm-up with a new subdomain to recover
- Lesson: Patience in warm-up prevents months of deliverability problems

### Shared vs. Dedicated IP Addresses

| Factor | Shared IP | Dedicated IP |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Included in ESP pricing | $20-$100+/month additional |
| **Control** | Other senders affect your reputation | Full control over reputation |
| **Volume needed** | Any volume | Need 50K-100K+ emails/month to maintain warm status |
| **Warm-up** | Pre-warmed by ESP | You must warm it up yourself |
| **Best for** | Small-medium senders (<50K/month) | Large senders (100K+/month), regulated industries |
| **Risk** | Bad neighbor can hurt your deliverability | Only your practices affect reputation |

**Recommendation:** Stay on shared IP until you're sending 100K+ emails/month consistently. A dedicated IP with low volume actually hurts deliverability because ISPs see inconsistent sending.

## 7.5.2 Email Authentication

Email authentication is the technical foundation of deliverability. Without it, your emails look suspicious to ISPs.

### SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

**What it does:** Tells receiving servers which IPs are authorized to send email on behalf of your domain.

**How it works:**
1. You publish a DNS TXT record listing authorized sending IPs
2. When your email arrives, the receiving server checks the SPF record
3. If the sending IP matches the SPF record → passes
4. If it doesn't match → fails (likely goes to spam or is rejected)

**Setup:**
```
DNS TXT Record for yourdomain.com:
v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:servers.mcsv.net include:sendgrid.net ~all
```

**Breakdown:**
- `v=spf1` — SPF version
- `include:_spf.google.com` — authorizes Google Workspace
- `include:servers.mcsv.net` — authorizes Mailchimp
- `include:sendgrid.net` — authorizes SendGrid
- `~all` — soft fail for unauthorized sources (recommended over `-all` for monitoring)

**Common Mistakes:**
- Multiple SPF records (only one allowed per domain)
- Too many DNS lookups (SPF allows max 10 DNS lookups; exceeding causes failure)
- Using `+all` (allows anyone to send as your domain — never do this)

### DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

**What it does:** Adds a cryptographic signature to your emails proving they haven't been tampered with in transit.

**How it works:**
1. Your sending server signs each email with a private key
2. The public key is published in your DNS records
3. The receiving server uses the public key to verify the signature
4. If the signature matches → authentic; if not → potentially forged

**Setup:**
```
DNS TXT Record (selector._domainkey.yourdomain.com):
v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIGfMA0GCSqGSIb3DQEBAQUAA4GNADCBiQ...
```

Your ESP provides the DKIM key. You add it to your DNS as a TXT or CNAME record.

**Best Practices:**
- Use 2048-bit keys (1024-bit is minimum but increasingly rejected)
- Rotate DKIM keys annually
- Test with `dig` or MXToolbox after setup to verify

### DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance)

**What it does:** Tells receiving servers what to do with emails that fail SPF or DKIM authentication, and sends you reports about authentication results.

**DMARC Policies:**

| Policy | Behavior | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| `p=none` | Monitor only — delivers even if auth fails, but sends reports | Start here — gather data |
| `p=quarantine` | Failed emails go to spam/junk | After reviewing reports and fixing auth |
| `p=reject` | Failed emails are blocked entirely | Maximum protection — only after thorough testing |

**Setup:**
```
DNS TXT Record (_dmarc.yourdomain.com):
v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:dmarc-reports@yourdomain.com; ruf=mailto:dmarc-forensic@yourdomain.com; pct=100
```

**DMARC Implementation Path:**
1. **Month 1-2:** Set `p=none` → collect reports → identify all legitimate sending sources
2. **Month 3:** Fix any authentication failures found in reports
3. **Month 4:** Move to `p=quarantine` with `pct=25` (only quarantine 25% of failures)
4. **Month 5:** Increase to `pct=50`, then `pct=100`
5. **Month 6+:** Move to `p=reject` when confident all legitimate sources are authenticated

**Real-Time Example — PayPal DMARC Implementation:**
- PayPal was the first major brand to adopt DMARC at `p=reject` in 2014
- Before DMARC: 25 million phishing emails/day spoofing @paypal.com
- After DMARC: Phishing attempts dropped by 70% within 6 months
- Now: virtually zero PayPal domain spoofing reaches inboxes

### BIMI (Brand Indicators for Message Identification)

**What it does:** Displays your brand logo next to your email in supported inboxes (Gmail, Yahoo, Apple Mail).

**Requirements:**
1. DMARC at `p=quarantine` or `p=reject` (prerequisite)
2. Verified Mark Certificate (VMC) from a certificate authority (~$1,500/year)
3. Trademarked logo in SVG Tiny PS format
4. DNS record pointing to your logo and VMC

**Setup:**
```
DNS TXT Record (default._bimi.yourdomain.com):
v=BIMI1; l=https://yourdomain.com/logo.svg; a=https://yourdomain.com/vmc.pem
```

**Real-Time Example — CNN:**
- CNN displays their red logo badge in Gmail next to every newsletter
- Impact: 10%+ higher open rates compared to generic avatar circles
- Brand recognition: users instantly identify CNN emails in a crowded inbox
- Worth the $1,500/year VMC investment for brands sending millions of emails

### Google and Yahoo 2024 Sender Requirements

Starting February 2024, Gmail and Yahoo enforce new requirements for all bulk senders (5,000+ emails/day):

| Requirement | Detail | Impact if Missing |
|---|---|---|
| **SPF + DKIM** | Both must be configured and passing | Emails rejected |
| **DMARC** | Must have a DMARC record (p=none minimum) | Emails deprioritized or rejected |
| **List-Unsubscribe header** | RFC 8058 one-click unsubscribe in email header | Emails may be rejected |
| **One-click unsubscribe** | Must process unsubscribe within 2 days | Compliance violation |
| **Spam complaint rate** | Must stay below 0.3% (target <0.1%) | Domain throttled or blocked |
| **Valid forward/reverse DNS** | Sending IPs must have proper PTR records | Delivery failures |
| **TLS encryption** | Send using TLS for transit encryption | Delivery issues |

## 7.5.3 Avoiding Spam Filters

### Content-Based Spam Triggers

| Trigger | Examples | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **Spammy words** | "Free!!!", "Act now", "Limited time", "Guaranteed", "No obligation" | Use sparingly; context matters more than individual words |
| **ALL CAPS** | "BUY NOW", "FREE SHIPPING", "HURRY" | Use sentence case; capitalize only 1-2 words for emphasis |
| **Excessive punctuation** | "Don't miss out!!!", "Ready???" | Max one exclamation/question mark |
| **Excessive emojis** | Subject lines with 5+ emojis | 0-2 emojis per subject line |
| **URL shorteners** | bit.ly, tinyurl links in email body | Use full branded URLs |
| **Misleading subject** | Subject doesn't match email content | Always ensure subject reflects content |
| **Single large image** | Email is one big image, no text | Maintain 60:40 text-to-image ratio |

**Important Nuance:** Modern spam filters (especially Gmail) weight engagement signals (open rate, click rate, complaint rate) far more heavily than content-based triggers. A well-engaged list can send "free shipping" without problems, while a poorly engaged list will have issues even with clean content.

### Engagement-Based Filtering

**Gmail Tabs and Priority Inbox:**

| Tab/Category | What Goes There | How to Get to Primary |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Personal emails, important business | High engagement rate, replies, contacts in address book |
| **Promotions** | Marketing emails, sales, offers | Most brand emails default here |
| **Updates** | Transactional, notifications | Automated system emails |
| **Social** | Social network notifications | Platform notifications |

**How to improve Primary inbox placement:**
1. Encourage subscribers to reply to your emails (ask questions)
2. Ask subscribers to "move this email to Primary" in your welcome email
3. Ask subscribers to add your from address to their contacts
4. Send from a personal name (not "marketing@company.com")
5. Reduce promotional language in early emails to build engagement signals

**Real-Time Example — Harry's (Men's Grooming):**
- Welcome email asks: "Reply with your top grooming concern and we'll personalize your recommendations"
- 15% of new subscribers reply, which signals to Gmail that this sender is "important"
- Harry's emails land in Primary inbox for subscribers who replied
- Result: 35% higher open rates for "repliers" vs. non-repliers, sustained over 12+ months

### Monitoring Blacklists

If your sending IP or domain appears on a blacklist, your deliverability will crater.

**Major Blacklists to Monitor:**

| Blacklist | Impact | Check Tool |
|---|---|---|
| **Spamhaus** | Most impactful — used by most ISPs | mxtoolbox.com/blacklists |
| **Barracuda** | Enterprise email systems | barracudacentral.org |
| **SpamCop** | Consumer ISPs | spamcop.net |
| **SORBS** | Widely used | sorbs.net |
| **UCEProtect** | European ISPs | uceprotect.net |

**If You're Blacklisted:**
1. Identify the cause (spam trap hit, high complaint rate, compromised account)
2. Fix the root cause (clean list, fix authentication, change practices)
3. Submit a delisting request to the blacklist operator
4. Wait 24-72 hours for removal
5. Monitor daily for 30 days to prevent re-listing

### Feedback Loops and Complaint Rates

A Feedback Loop (FBL) is a service where ISPs send you notifications when subscribers mark your email as spam.

**Complaint Rate Benchmarks:**
- Good: < 0.05%
- Acceptable: 0.05-0.1%
- Warning: 0.1-0.3%
- Critical: > 0.3% (Gmail will throttle/block your domain)

**How to Reduce Complaints:**
1. Set clear expectations at opt-in (what, how often)
2. Make unsubscribe easier than marking as spam
3. Honor unsubscribe requests immediately
4. Don't email people who didn't opt in
5. Use engagement-based segmentation to exclude non-engagers

## Implementation Steps for Section 7.5

### Step 1: Authenticate Your Domain (Week 1)
- Set up SPF record listing all authorized sending services
- Configure DKIM for your ESP (they provide the key, you add to DNS)
- Create a DMARC record starting with `p=none` for monitoring
- Verify all records using MXToolbox or your ESP's verification tool
- **Tool:** MXToolbox (DNS checks), your DNS provider (GoDaddy, Cloudflare, etc.)

### Step 2: Set Up Monitoring (Week 1-2)
- Register for Google Postmaster Tools and verify your domain
- Register for Microsoft SNDS and add your sending IPs
- Set up weekly blacklist monitoring (MXToolbox free monitoring)
- Configure feedback loops with major ISPs (most ESPs do this automatically)
- **Tool:** Google Postmaster Tools (free), MXToolbox (free), Microsoft SNDS (free)

### Step 3: Run Baseline Deliverability Tests (Week 2)
- Send test emails to GlockApps or Litmus seed list
- Record inbox placement rates across major providers (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, Apple)
- Identify any providers where you're landing in spam
- Document baseline for future comparison
- **Tool:** GlockApps ($59/mo) or Mail-Tester.com (free basic tests)

### Step 4: Fix Authentication Issues (Week 2-3)
- Review DMARC reports (aggregate reports come to the email you specified)
- Identify any unauthorized senders using your domain
- Fix SPF/DKIM for any legitimate services that are failing authentication
- Use a DMARC analysis tool to parse XML reports
- **Tool:** DMARC Analyzer, Valimail, dmarcian, or Postmark's free DMARC tool

### Step 5: Implement Ongoing Hygiene (Week 3+, Ongoing)
- Set up automated hard bounce removal in your ESP
- Create a segment for 90-day inactive subscribers
- Build and activate re-engagement automation
- Schedule quarterly full-list verification with ZeroBounce or NeverBounce
- Review Google Postmaster weekly; blacklists monthly
- **Tool:** ZeroBounce, Google Postmaster Tools, your ESP

## Strategies

1. **The "Authentication-First" Strategy:** Never send a single marketing email until SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are fully configured and verified. Authentication is the foundation — everything else builds on it.
2. **The "Reputation Score" Strategy:** Treat your domain reputation like a credit score. Every decision (list quality, send frequency, content quality, complaint handling) either builds or damages your score. Check Google Postmaster weekly.
3. **The "Graduated DMARC" Strategy:** Don't jump to `p=reject`. Spend 2 months on `p=none` collecting reports, 2 months on `p=quarantine` at increasing percentages, then move to `p=reject`. Rushing causes legitimate email to be blocked.
4. **The "Engagement-First Deliverability" Strategy:** The single most effective deliverability strategy is simply having high engagement rates. ISPs reward senders whose subscribers open, click, and reply. Focus on content quality and segmentation first, technical fixes second.
5. **The "Separate Streams" Strategy:** Use different subdomains and IPs for marketing vs. transactional email (marketing.yourdomain.com vs. mail.yourdomain.com). This prevents marketing reputation issues from affecting critical transactional emails.

## Best Practices

1. **Check authentication monthly, not just at setup.** DNS changes, platform migrations, and new services can break SPF/DKIM without notice.
2. **Monitor Google Postmaster Tools weekly.** It's the single best free tool for understanding how Gmail (the largest email provider) sees your sending.
3. **Keep spam complaint rate under 0.05%.** The 0.3% threshold from Google/Yahoo is a maximum — aim for well below it.
4. **Send consistently.** ISPs are suspicious of senders who blast 100K emails once a month then go silent. Consistent daily or weekly volume builds trust.
5. **Warm up gradually for any volume increase.** If you normally send 10K/week and want to send a holiday campaign to 50K, ramp up over 2-3 weeks, not overnight.
6. **Use real-mailbox testing, not just spam score tools.** Mail-Tester gives you a score, but GlockApps or Litmus shows you actual inbox placement across providers.
7. **Fix deliverability issues before they compound.** A small reputation dip that goes unnoticed for 3 months becomes a major crisis. Catch it early.

## Additional Insights

### The Gmail Ecosystem Effect

Gmail holds 30-35% of the global email market share. How Gmail treats your emails essentially sets the tone for all other providers. If Gmail trusts you, Outlook and Yahoo usually do too. If Gmail puts you in spam, others often follow.

This is why Google Postmaster Tools is the single most important monitoring tool — it shows you exactly how the largest email provider in the world perceives your sending reputation.

### The Cost of Poor Deliverability

A simple calculation:
- List size: 50,000 subscribers
- Average inbox placement: 75% (meaning 25% go to spam/promotions)
- Emails going to spam: 12,500 per send
- If inbox placement improved to 95%: 9,500 more emails reach the inbox per send
- At $0.10 revenue per delivered email: $950 more revenue per send
- 4 sends per month: $3,800/month = $45,600/year in recovered revenue

Most deliverability improvements cost less than $200/month in tools but recover thousands in revenue.

### The "Postmaster Triangle" — Three Things ISPs Care About

1. **Authentication** — Are you who you say you are? (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
2. **Reputation** — Do your subscribers want your email? (engagement, complaints)
3. **Content** — Is your email legitimate and valuable? (not spammy, well-formatted)

Fix them in this order. Most brands focus on content (avoiding spam words) when the real issue is authentication or reputation.

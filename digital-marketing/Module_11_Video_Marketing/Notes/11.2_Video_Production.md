## 11.2.1 Pre-Production

Pre-production is where 80% of a video's success is determined. It's the planning phase that happens before the camera ever rolls. Skipping or rushing pre-production is the #1 reason marketing videos fail.

### Concept Development & Ideation

**The Ideation Framework:**

```
┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              VIDEO IDEATION PROCESS                   │
│                                                       │
│  1. AUDIENCE RESEARCH                                 │
│     What questions are they asking?                   │
│     What problems do they have?                       │
│     What content do they already consume?             │
│                                                       │
│  2. KEYWORD/TOPIC RESEARCH                            │
│     YouTube search suggestions                        │
│     TubeBuddy/vidIQ keyword explorer                  │
│     Google Trends + "People Also Ask"                 │
│     Community questions (Reddit, Quora, comments)     │
│                                                       │
│  3. COMPETITIVE GAP ANALYSIS                          │
│     What have competitors covered?                    │
│     What's missing or poorly done?                    │
│     What angle is unique to you?                      │
│                                                       │
│  4. CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT                               │
│     Working title + hook                              │
│     Key message (1 sentence)                          │
│     Target audience segment                           │
│     Desired viewer action after watching              │
│     Format decision (tutorial, interview, etc.)       │
│                                                       │
│  5. VALIDATION                                        │
│     Does this topic have search demand?               │
│     Can we provide unique value?                      │
│     Does it align with our content pillars?           │
│     Is this timely or evergreen?                      │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Real-Time Example:**
When Ali Abdaal ideates videos, he uses a "Video Ideas Database" in Notion with columns for: Title, Keyword Volume (from vidIQ), Competitor Saturation, Personal Expertise Level, and Audience Demand Score. He only greenlights videos that score 7+/10 across all criteria. This system helped him reach 5M subscribers by ensuring every video has a strong reason to exist.

### Scriptwriting: Structure, Pacing, Hooks, CTAs

**The anatomy of a great video script:**

| Section | Duration | Purpose | Technique |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | 0-5 seconds | Stop the scroll, create curiosity | Question, bold claim, result preview, conflict |
| **Problem Setup** | 5-30 seconds | Establish why the viewer should care | Relatable pain point, statistic, story |
| **Promise/Thesis** | 30-45 seconds | Tell them what they'll learn/gain | "By the end of this video, you'll know..." |
| **Content Body** | 1-8 minutes | Deliver value, teach, entertain | Sections with clear transitions |
| **Recap** | 15-30 seconds | Reinforce key takeaways | Numbered summary |
| **CTA** | 10-20 seconds | Tell them what to do next | Subscribe, click link, comment |

**Hook formulas that work:**

```
1. THE RESULT HOOK:
   "This one change increased our revenue by 340%"

2. THE QUESTION HOOK:
   "What if everything you know about SEO is wrong?"

3. THE CONTROVERSY HOOK:
   "Most marketing advice is actually hurting your business"

4. THE STORY HOOK:
   "Last month, I lost $50,000 on a single campaign. Here's what I learned."

5. THE HOW-TO HOOK:
   "In the next 8 minutes, I'll show you exactly how to..."

6. THE PREVIEW HOOK:
   [Show the end result first] "By the end, you'll be able to build this."

7. THE PATTERN INTERRUPT:
   [Unexpected visual or sound] + "This is NOT a regular tutorial."
```

**Real-Time Example:**
MrBeast is the undisputed master of hooks. His "$1 vs. $1,000,000,000 Yacht" — the title IS the hook. The first 3 seconds show the contrast visually. He has stated that his team spends more time on the first 30 seconds than the rest of the video combined. His retention rate for the first 30 seconds averages 85%+ (industry standard: 45-55%).

```
└── Location/set preparation

├── Setup: lighting, camera, audio (1-2 hours)

├── Rough cut assembly (3-4 hours)
├── Fine cut with music and graphics (3-4 hours)
├── Color correction and audio mix (2 hours)
├── Review round 1 and revisions (2 hours)
├── Captions and final export (1 hour)

DAY 6: Publishing
├── Upload and optimize metadata
├── Schedule cross-platform posts

```

## 11.2.3 Filming Techniques

### Framing & Composition Rules

**Rule of Thirds:**
```
┌─────┬─────┬─────┐
│     │     │     │
│  ●  │     │     │   Place subjects at intersection
│     │     │     │   points (●) for visual interest
├─────┼─────┼─────┤
│     │     │     │
│     │  ●  │     │   Center framing works for direct-
│     │     │     │   to-camera talking head content
├─────┼─────┼─────┤
│     │     │     │
│     │     │  ●  │
│     │     │     │
└─────┴─────┴─────┘
```

**Headroom and look room:**
- Leave space above the head (headroom) — too much looks amateur, too little feels claustrophobic
- When subject looks to one side, leave space in the direction they're looking (look room)
- Eyes should fall on the upper-third line

**Real-Time Example:**
Every MKBHD video uses the rule of thirds precisely — he positions himself in the left third, with his studio and product setups filling the right two-thirds. This creates visual balance and gives the viewer's eye a "path" to follow. Compare this to amateur YouTubers who center-frame with dead space above their head.

| Movement | Description | Effect | Use Case |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Static** | Locked-off, no movement | Stability, authority, focus | Talking head, interviews |

| **Handheld** | Camera held by operator | Energy, urgency, authenticity | BTS, vlogs, documentary style |

### B-Roll Capture

B-roll is supplementary footage that covers cuts, illustrates points, and adds visual interest. It separates professional videos from amateur talking-head content.

**B-roll categories to always capture:**

| Category | Examples | When to Film |
|---|---|---|
| **Product** | Close-ups, being used, packaging, textures | Dedicated product shoot |
| **Environment** | Office, location, city, nature | Before/after main filming |
| **Process** | Typing, writing, working, cooking | During demonstration |
| **People** | Team working, customer reactions, candid moments | Throughout the day |
| **Abstract** | Coffee pouring, time-lapse, slow motion | Dedicated creative session |
| **Screen** | Software demos, website, data, charts | Screen recording sessions |

**Real-Time Example:**
Peter McKinnon's B-roll is legendary. Even in a simple talking-head video about camera settings, he'll cut to: coffee pouring in slow motion, his camera clicking, hands adjusting a lens, a time-lapse of his studio, and cinematic city shots. These 2-5 second B-roll inserts transform a static video into a cinematic experience. He once shared that he shoots 10 minutes of B-roll for every 1 minute he uses.

### Interview Setup & Technique

**The professional two-camera interview setup:**

```
                    INTERVIEWER
                        ●
                       /
                      /
    CAM A ●──────── SUBJECT ────────● CAM B
    (main,                          (secondary,
     medium shot,                    close-up,
     eye-level)                      slight angle)
    
    ● Key Light                 ● Fill Light
    (45° from subject,          (opposite side,
     above eye-level)            softer/dimmer)
    
    ● Back Light / Hair Light
    (behind subject,
     creates separation)
```

**Interview best practices:**
1. Warm up the subject with easy questions first
2. Ask open-ended questions — never yes/no
3. Ask them to include the question in their answer ("The biggest challenge was..." not just "Budget")
4. Pause after they answer — they often add gold in the silence
5. Maintain eye contact (not with camera unless it's a direct-to-viewer format)

**Real-Time Example:**
Hot Ones (by First We Feast) has mastered the interview format. Host Sean Evans researches deeply, asks unexpected questions, and the "hot wings" gimmick creates natural, unguarded moments. The format has attracted A-list guests (Scarlett Johansson, Gordon Ramsay, Billie Eilish) and averages 10M+ views per episode. The lesson: a unique format + deep preparation = compelling interviews.

### Smartphone Filming Best Practices

Since 90%+ of social media video can be excellently produced on a phone:

1. **Clean the lens** — It sounds obvious but most phone videos are shot through a smudged lens
2. **Use the rear camera** — Higher quality than the selfie camera
3. **Lock exposure and focus** — Tap and hold on screen to lock
4. **Shoot in 4K/30fps** — Maximum flexibility in editing
5. **Use airplane mode** — Prevent calls/notifications during filming
6. **Go horizontal for YouTube, vertical for social** — Match the platform
7. **Add a phone stabilizer** — Even a $15 mini tripod transforms quality
8. **Use natural light facing you** — Face a window, never have a window behind you
9. **External microphone** — A $20 lavalier mic is the single best investment
10. **Use the native camera app or Filmic Pro** — More control over exposure and focus

### Editing Workflow

```
PROFESSIONAL EDITING PIPELINE:

1. INGEST & ORGANIZE
   ├── Import all footage, audio, assets
   ├── Create folder structure (A-roll, B-roll, Audio, Music, Graphics)
   ├── Label and color-code clips
   └── Create a project timeline with markers

2. ROUGH CUT (Assembly Edit)
   ├── Lay down A-roll (main footage) in sequence
   ├── Remove obvious mistakes and dead air
   ├── Establish overall structure and pacing
   └── Check total duration against target

3. FINE CUT
   ├── Tighten edits — remove filler words, pauses
   ├── Add B-roll to cover cuts and illustrate points
   ├── Insert transitions (use sparingly — cut is king)
   ├── Add lower thirds, text overlays, callouts
   └── Timing adjustments for pacing

4. COLOR CORRECTION & GRADING
   ├── Correct exposure, white balance across clips
   ├── Match shots for consistency
   ├── Apply creative grade (LUT or manual)
   └── Ensure skin tones look natural

5. AUDIO MIX
   ├── Level dialogue (-12 to -6 dB target)
   ├── Add background music (-18 to -24 dB under dialogue)
   ├── Noise reduction on dialogue tracks
   ├── Add sound effects where needed
   └── Ensure no audio clipping

6. FINISHING
   ├── Add subtitles/captions (SRT or burnt-in)
   ├── Add intro/outro and branding elements
   ├── Final review — watch fully at 1x speed
   ├── Export in platform-optimized settings
   └── Create thumbnail
```

### Color Correction vs. Color Grading

| Aspect | Color Correction | Color Grading |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Fix technical issues — exposure, white balance, contrast | Creative look — mood, tone, style |
| **When** | First — fix before you style | After correction is done |
| **Goal** | Make footage look neutral and consistent | Make footage look cinematic or branded |
| **Tools** | Waveform, vectorscope, histogram | LUTs, color wheels, curves |
| **Example** | Removing green cast from fluorescent lighting | Applying a warm, desaturated "film look" |

**Tools:** DaVinci Resolve (industry leader for color), Premiere Pro Lumetri, Final Cut Color Board

### Audio Editing Best Practices

| Parameter | Target Level | Tool |
|---|---|---|
| **Dialogue** | -12 to -6 dBFS peak | Premiere Pro, Audacity, Descript |
| **Music (under dialogue)** | -18 to -24 dBFS | Any editor's audio mixer |
| **Music (standalone)** | -12 to -6 dBFS | Any editor |
| **Sound effects** | -18 to -12 dBFS | Varies by context |
| **Overall master** | -3 to -1 dBFS peak, -14 LUFS avg | Loudness meters (LUFS) |

- ADA/accessibility compliance

- Non-native speakers benefit significantly

### Thumbnail Creation for YouTube

Thumbnails are the #1 factor in click-through rate (CTR). YouTube's own research shows that 90% of top-performing videos have custom thumbnails.

**Thumbnail best practices:**

| Element | Best Practice |
|---|---|
| **Face** | Close-up with emotion (surprise, excitement, curiosity) |
| **Text** | 3-5 words max, large and readable on mobile |
| **Colors** | High contrast, bright, complementary colors |
| **Composition** | Rule of thirds, visual hierarchy, single focal point |
| **Consistency** | Brand colors and style for channel recognition |
| **Avoid** | Clickbait that doesn't match content, small text, clutter |
| **Testing** | A/B test with TubeBuddy; iterate on winners |
| **Resolution** | 1280 x 720 pixels minimum, 16:9 ratio |

**Tools:** Canva, Photoshop, Figma, Thumbnail.ai, TubeBuddy (A/B testing)

**Real-Time Example:**
MrBeast's thumbnails follow a strict formula: one face with an extreme expression, 1-3 words of large text, bright saturated colors, and a clear visual of the "stakes" (money, food, challenge). He has revealed that his team creates 20+ thumbnail options per video and tests them before publishing. If a video's CTR drops below 8% after 48 hours, they change the thumbnail.

## Implementation Steps for Video Production

### Phase 1: Gear Up (Week 1)
1. Assess current equipment and identify gaps
2. Start with audio first — buy a quality microphone
3. Set up basic lighting (even a desk lamp or window setup)
4. Choose your editing software and complete a basic tutorial
5. Create your first test video (no publishing, just practice)

### Phase 2: Develop Workflow (Weeks 2-3)
6. Create script and storyboard templates
7. Establish your filming setup (desk, background, framing)
8. Practice the editing pipeline: ingest → rough cut → fine cut → export
9. Build a B-roll library by filming 30 minutes of ambient footage
10. Create your intro/outro and branding elements

### Phase 3: Produce First Batch (Weeks 3-4)
11. Script and film your first 3-5 videos
12. Edit using your established workflow
13. Create thumbnails and captions
14. Get feedback from team or peers before publishing
15. Refine process based on lessons learned

### Phase 4: Optimize & Scale (Month 2+)
16. Batch film (3-5 videos in a single session)
17. Build a team or outsource editing
18. Create SOPs for every stage of production
19. Build a reusable asset library (templates, music, graphics, LUTs)
20. Continuously upgrade equipment as budget allows

## Gap Finding — Common Production Mistakes

| Gap | Symptom | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Bad audio** | Echoey, tinny, background noise | Invest in a $50+ lavalier mic; record in quiet, soft-furnished rooms |
| **Poor lighting** | Dark, uneven, unflattering shadows | Use three-point lighting or face a large window |
| **No B-roll** | Static talking head for 10+ minutes | Shoot 10 min of B-roll per 1 min used; always capture cutaways |
| **Over-editing** | Too many transitions, effects, zooms | Cut is the only transition you need 95% of the time |
| **No captions** | Losing 85% of mobile viewers on social | Add burnt-in captions for social; SRT for YouTube |
| **Wrong aspect ratio** | Black bars, cropped content, unprofessional | Plan your aspect ratio before filming; shoot wide if repurposing |
| **Inconsistent branding** | Different fonts, colors, intros in each video | Build and use templates for lower thirds, intros, outros |
| **No script/outline** | Rambling, unfocused content | At minimum, create a bullet-point outline before filming |
| **Ignoring color** | Footage looks flat, green, or inconsistent | Apply basic color correction to every video |
| **No archival system** | Can't find old footage, lost files | Organize with date/project folders; back up to external + cloud |

## Best Practices — Video Production

1. **Audio first, always** — Record a test clip and listen on earbuds before filming the real thing.
2. **Three-point lighting is non-negotiable** — Even a $30 ring light + a window dramatically improves quality.
3. **Batch filming saves 60% of production time** — Set up once, film 3-5 videos in a session.
4. **Shoot more B-roll than you think you need** — You'll always wish you had more in editing.
5. **Keep backups** — 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media types, 1 offsite.
6. **Edit for pace** — If a moment doesn't add value, cut it. Viewers' attention spans demand tight editing.
7. **Invest in learning** — A 2-hour YouTube tutorial on Premiere Pro will save you 100 hours of trial-and-error.
8. **Create templates** — Build reusable Premiere/Final Cut project templates with your branding, music beds, and lower thirds.
9. **Use keyboard shortcuts** — Learn J-K-L (playback), I-O (in/out), and Ctrl/Cmd+Z (undo) in your editor. Editing speed doubles.
10. **Color correct EVERY video** — Even a basic auto-correction makes footage look 3x more professional.
11. **Mix audio with headphones** — Speakers hide problems that headphones reveal.
12. **Watch your video on a phone before publishing** — 70%+ of viewers will see it on mobile. Check text readability and framing.

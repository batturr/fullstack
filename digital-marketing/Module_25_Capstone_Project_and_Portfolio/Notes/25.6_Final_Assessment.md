## 25.6.1 Grading Criteria — Deep Dive

### Understanding How Your Capstone Is Evaluated

The grading criteria is not arbitrary — it reflects the exact skills that employers and clients evaluate when assessing a digital marketer. Understanding each criterion deeply allows you to prioritize your effort where it matters most.

**Real-Time Example — How Marketing Teams Are Evaluated at Top Companies:**
At **Google**, marketing team performance reviews evaluate:
- Strategic impact (did the campaign move a business metric?)
- Execution excellence (was the work on time, on brand, and technically sound?)
- Data-driven decision-making (were decisions backed by evidence?)
- Innovation (did the marketer try something new or improve existing approaches?)
- Communication (could they explain what they did and why to non-marketers?)

The capstone grading criteria mirrors this professional evaluation structure.

---

### Strategy Quality (20%)

**What evaluators are looking for:**

| Sub-Criteria | Weight | What "Excellent" Looks Like | What "Weak" Looks Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Research Depth** | 5% | Primary + secondary research; data-backed insights; competitor analysis includes specific metrics | Surface-level Google searches; no competitive data; assumptions without evidence |
| **Goal Clarity** | 5% | SMART goals tied to business outcomes; realistic targets with benchmark justification | Vague goals ("increase brand awareness"); no baselines; unrealistic targets |
| **Channel Rationale** | 5% | Channel selection justified by persona behavior data; budget allocation logic explained | Channels chosen because "everyone uses them"; no rationale for budget split |
| **Audience Understanding** | 5% | Rich personas with psychographics and behavioral data; customer journey mapped | Template-filled personas; no primary research; personas do not inform strategy |

**Implementation Steps to Score High on Strategy:**

1. **Start with WHY before WHAT** — Your strategy document should explain why each decision was made, not just what you plan to do.

2. **Use the "So What?" test** — For every research finding, ask "So what does this mean for our strategy?" If you cannot answer, the finding is irrelevant noise.

3. **Reference Module 1-5 frameworks** — Demonstrate that your strategy is grounded in the foundational modules (market research, consumer psychology, channel strategy, goal-setting).

4. **Include counterarguments** — Show what alternatives you considered and why you rejected them. This demonstrates strategic depth.

**Real-Time Example — Strategic Depth:**
When **Basecamp** decided to market their project management tool, they made a deliberate strategic choice: instead of targeting enterprise companies (where Salesforce, Microsoft, and Oracle dominate), they targeted small teams and freelancers. Their research showed:
- 60% of project management tools were designed for 500+ employee companies
- Teams of 2-10 people were underserved — they were using email and spreadsheets
- Small team decision-makers valued simplicity over features
- Price sensitivity was high — they priced at $99/month flat (not per-user)

This strategic positioning was the foundation for all execution: simple messaging, minimalist design, anti-enterprise branding ("We are not for everyone, and we are proud of that").

### Data & Analytics (20%)

| Sub-Criteria | Weight | What "Excellent" Looks Like | What "Weak" Looks Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tracking Setup** | 5% | GA4 fully configured; custom events for all key actions; GTM used properly; pixel verified | GA4 installed but not configured; no custom events; no conversion tracking |
| **Data Accuracy** | 5% | Numbers verified across platforms; anomalies noted and explained; data collection methodology documented | Numbers do not match between platforms; no verification; inconsistent reporting periods |
| **Analysis Depth** | 5% | Segmented analysis (by channel, audience, device, time); patterns identified; hypotheses formed from data | Surface-level reporting; only averages; no segmentation; no "why" behind the numbers |
| **Attribution** | 5% | Attribution model chosen and justified; multi-touch analysis attempted; limitations acknowledged | No attribution discussion; only last-click; no understanding of cross-channel effects |

**Real-Time Example — Analytics Depth That Impresses:**
When **Peloton** analyzed their marketing performance, they did not just look at ad spend vs. revenue. They discovered through deep analytics that:
- Customers who engaged with community features (leaderboards, groups) within 30 days of purchase had 95% retention vs. 60% for non-community users
- Instagram ads had higher CTR but lower conversion than Facebook feed ads — but Instagram acquirers had 40% higher LTV
- Morning email sends (6-7 AM) had 2x the conversion rate of afternoon sends because their audience was planning workouts for the day

This level of analysis informed completely different budget allocations than surface-level ROAS would suggest. Show this depth in your capstone.

**Analytics Tools Mastery Checklist:**

```
□ GA4 property configured with correct data streams
□ Custom events tracking: button clicks, form submissions, 
  scroll depth, video plays, outbound clicks
□ Key events marked as conversions in GA4
□ Custom dimensions (user properties) set up for segmentation
□ Looker Studio dashboard built with live data
□ Google Search Console verified and monitored
□ UTM parameters consistent across all campaigns
□ Cross-platform data reconciliation documented
□ Audience segments created for analysis (new vs. returning, 
  mobile vs. desktop, campaign source)
□ Weekly data export routine established for backup
```

### Results & Optimization (15%)

| Sub-Criteria | Weight | What "Excellent" Looks Like | What "Weak" Looks Like |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Performance Against KPIs** | 5% | Clear comparison table showing target vs. actual; context for hits and misses | No reference back to original goals; cherry-picked metrics |
| **Optimization Decisions** | 5% | Documented mid-campaign changes with data-backed rationale; before/after comparison for each optimization | "Set it and forget it"; no changes made; no testing |
| **Testing Rigor** | 5% | A/B tests with proper methodology; hypothesis-test-result-learning documented; statistical significance considered | No testing; "I changed the headline" without measuring impact |

**Optimization Documentation Template:**

```
OPTIMIZATION LOG

Date: [Date]
Channel: [Channel]
Observation: [What the data showed]
Hypothesis: [Why you think this is happening]
Action: [What you changed]
Expected Impact: [What you think will happen]
Actual Impact: [What actually happened — fill in later]
Learning: [What this teaches us for future campaigns]

EXAMPLE:
Date: Week 6, Tuesday
Channel: Meta Ads
Observation: CPA increased from $4.20 to $7.80 over 3 days; 
  frequency reached 4.2 on primary audience
Hypothesis: Audience fatigue — same 50,000 people seeing the 
  ad 4+ times is causing ad blindness
Action: Expanded audience by 30% (added new interest targets); 
  created 3 new ad creatives with different visual styles
Expected Impact: CPA returns to $4-5 range within 3-4 days
Actual Impact: CPA dropped to $3.90 within 5 days — new audience 
  segment responded even better than original
Learning: Budget-to-audience ratio matters. For $25/day budget, 
  audience should be 100K+ to prevent frequency issues within 
  a 4-week campaign window
```

## 25.6.2 Peer Review Process

### Why Peer Review Matters

Peer review simulates the real-world experience of presenting work to colleagues, stakeholders, and clients. It teaches you to receive criticism constructively, identify blind spots, and learn from others' approaches to the same challenge.

**Real-Time Example — How Peer Review Works at Top Companies:**
At **Amazon**, every significant initiative requires a 6-page memo that is reviewed by peers and leadership in a silent reading session before discussion. The author must defend their logic, data, and conclusions against rigorous questioning. This process:
- Eliminates presentation-driven bias (pretty slides hiding weak thinking)
- Forces clarity of thought (if you cannot write it clearly, you do not understand it)
- Creates better outcomes through collective intelligence

### Structured Peer Feedback Forms

**Quantitative Feedback:**

| Dimension | 1 (Needs Work) | 2 (Below Average) | 3 (Meets Standards) | 4 (Above Average) | 5 (Exceptional) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Strategic clarity | No clear strategy | Strategy exists but vague | Clear strategy with rationale | Well-researched, data-backed strategy | Strategy demonstrates unique insights and creative positioning |
| Execution quality | Incomplete or very low quality | Complete but errors present | Professional quality throughout | High quality with creative elements | Outstanding quality that could be used in a real professional context |
| Data analysis | No analysis | Basic reporting only | Analysis with some insights | Deep analysis with actionable insights | Analysis reveals non-obvious insights that change strategic direction |
| Results communication | No clear results | Results mentioned but not quantified | Results quantified and visualized | Results contextualized with benchmarks and implications | Results told as a compelling narrative that drives action |
| Presentation delivery | Unprepared, unclear | Prepared but lacking confidence | Clear and organized | Engaging and confident | Compelling, memorable, inspires audience |

**Qualitative Feedback Prompts:**
1. What was the single most impressive element of this project?
2. What one change would most improve this project?
3. What did you learn from reviewing this project that you can apply to your own work?
4. If you were the client, would you renew this person's contract? Why or why not?

### Constructive Criticism Guidelines

**The SBI Model for Peer Feedback:**

```
Situation: "When you presented the social media results..."
Behavior:  "...you only showed follower growth without engagement metrics..."
Impact:    "...which made it hard to assess whether the social strategy was 
           actually working, since follower count alone is a vanity metric."

Improved version (with suggestion):
"When you presented the social media results, I noticed the focus was on 
follower growth. Adding engagement rate and click-through data would 
strengthen the case significantly, since those metrics show whether 
followers are actually valuable."
```

**Feedback Rules:**
- Be specific, not vague ("Your ad creative was strong because the value proposition was clear in the first 3 words" vs. "Nice ads")
- Focus on the work, not the person
- Balance positives with constructive suggestions (not criticism)
- Offer solutions, not just problems
- Ask questions to understand decisions before judging them

### Cross-Pollination of Ideas

The most valuable aspect of peer review is learning from different approaches. Each reviewer should note:
- One tactic from the reviewed project to try in their own work
- One tool or platform they had not considered
- One strategic framework that provided a new perspective
- One mistake to avoid based on the reviewer's experience

## 25.6.3 Continuous Improvement Plan

### Why You Need a Post-Course Learning Plan

Digital marketing changes faster than any course can keep up with. Algorithm updates, new platforms, AI tools, privacy regulations, and consumer behavior shifts mean that the knowledge from this course has a half-life of about 12-18 months. A continuous improvement plan ensures you stay relevant and continue growing.

**Real-Time Example — The Pace of Change:**
- 2020: TikTok was "for teenagers." By 2023, it was the #1 social platform for product discovery across all age groups.
- 2022: ChatGPT launched. By 2024, 67% of marketers reported using AI tools daily. By 2025, AI-generated content was standard.
- 2023: Google introduced SGE (Search Generative Experience). By 2025, SEO strategies required fundamental rethinking.
- 2024: Third-party cookie deprecation was announced (again). First-party data strategies became non-negotiable.

If you stopped learning in 2022, you would be functionally obsolete by 2025.

## Best Practices for Final Assessment

1. **Treat the rubric as your roadmap** — Print it out. Check every sub-criterion before submission. Make sure no section is neglected.

2. **Ask for feedback before final submission** — Share your report and case study with a peer or mentor at least one week before the deadline. Fresh eyes catch what yours miss.

3. **Practice your presentation at least 3 times** — Once alone, once in front of a friend/family member, once in front of a peer. Get feedback each time.

4. **Include a "What I Would Do Differently" section** — Self-awareness scores higher than false perfection. Evaluators want to see that you can critically assess your own work.

5. **Back every claim with evidence** — "Social media performed well" is worthless. "Instagram engagement rate averaged 4.7%, 56% above the 3.0% industry benchmark for our niche, driven by our behind-the-scenes Reels strategy" is excellent.
